## What should a dealership look for in AI search?

The right question when evaluating dealership search isn't "does it look modern" - it's "does it get more shoppers to a VDP and into a lead form." A shopper who types "used silver Tahoe under 40k" and hits a blank page bounces. One who gets three relevant VDPs and can ask a follow-up question from the listing is on their way to a lead. Three things determine which outcome you get.

**Semantic matching.** Good AI search understands what a shopper means, not just what they typed. A query like "family SUV good on gas under 35k" should surface relevant inventory even if no listing says exactly those words.

**Automatic filters.** The best search experiences surface relevant filter options based on what's in your inventory - year, make, model, price, mileage, trim, color - without the shopper having to know which sidebar to use first.

**Conversational follow-up.** Shoppers who find a promising VDP often have a follow-up question: does it have heated seats, what's the warranty, can I finance with bad credit. A search tool that also handles those questions keeps the shopper on your site instead of bouncing them to Google.

## 2. Dealer.com

[Dealer.com](https://dealer.com) is Cox Automotive's flagship dealership website platform, and it powers tens of thousands of rooftops with inventory-driven sites, digital retailing widgets, and integrated lead capture. The search bar comes built into the platform, and it pairs tightly with VinSolutions CRM and Dealertrack DMS. For a GM running multiple rooftops, that's the real draw: one vendor relationship, one bundled contract, one set of integrations to maintain.

**Best for:** Multi-rooftop dealer groups already standardized on VinSolutions, Dealertrack, and Autotrader who want their website, CRM, DMS, and listings on one vendor stack.

**Pricing:** Quote-only, per-rooftop. Pricing scales with group size, with bundled discounts when paired with other Cox products like VinSolutions and Dealertrack.

**Pros:**
- Native integration with VinSolutions CRM, Dealertrack DMS, and Autotrader listings - one stack, one contract, one support line
- Mature inventory-driven site architecture that handles VDP and SRP volume at scale without custom development
- Digital retailing widgets and lead capture built into the standard product tier, so you're not bolting on a separate vendor for trade-in or financing flows

**Cons:**
- Cox Automotive ecosystem lock-in - using one Cox product nudges renewals toward the others, and untangling later is expensive
- Templated site experience; deep customization or non-Cox AI search overlays often require Cox-managed services or a premium tier
- Per-rooftop pricing scales fast for larger dealer groups and isn't published, so budget planning requires a quote

**Verdict:** Pick Dealer.com when you're already deep in the Cox stack and want the easiest path for the website layer; skip it if you want an AI-first answer layer with cited responses or you're trying to avoid further Cox lock-in.

## 3. DealerInspire

[DealerInspire](https://dealerinspire.com) is the Cars.com-owned dealership website platform, built around a modern page-builder, integrated digital-retailing widgets, and conversational features that pair naturally with the Cars.com inventory feed and lead-management ecosystem. For a rooftop already sending inventory to Cars.com and buying leads back from it, DealerInspire is the simplest way to put the website on the same vendor as the feed. A GM running multiple rooftops gets one renewal conversation for site, inventory syndication, and lead flow instead of three.

**Best for:** Dealerships already invested in the Cars.com inventory feed and lead-management ecosystem who want a modern site builder on the same vendor relationship.

**Pricing:** Quote-only, per rooftop. Premium positioning above entry-level website platforms, sold through Cars.com's dealer sales motion with no published numbers.

**Pros:**
- Modern page-builder makes campaign and inventory landing pages easier to launch than older templated platforms
- Digital-retailing tools for trade-in, financing, and credit are built into the site rather than bolted on as separate vendors
- Tight pairing with the Cars.com inventory feed and lead-management ecosystem - one vendor for site, feed, and lead flow

**Cons:**
- Cars.com Group ecosystem lock-in - same renewal pull as the Cox stack, just pointed in a different direction
- Conversational AI features are still maturing compared to vendors building answer layers from scratch
- No published pricing; premium tier above entry-level dealership website platforms, so budget planning needs a quote

**Verdict:** Pick DealerInspire when you're already on the Cars.com inventory and lead feed and want the website on the same vendor; skip it if you need the strongest AI search and answer layer rather than a website platform with conversational features added on.

## 4. DealerOn

[DealerOn](https://dealeron.com) is an independent dealership website platform that's built its reputation on organic search performance and analytics, rather than on bundling itself into a larger CRM or DMS stack. Its Cobalt 2.0 sites (DealerOn's current responsive website template) are designed around Google-friendly structure and page speed, and the reporting layer gives a GM enough visibility to act on traffic and conversion data without leaning on an engineering team.

**Best for:** Independent dealers and small franchise groups who prioritize organic search performance and want a website platform that isn't part of the Cox or Cars.com ecosystem.

**Pricing:** Quote-only, per rooftop. Mid-market positioning sold through a direct sales motion, with no published numbers.

**Pros:**
- Long-running SEO focus - sites are built around Google-friendly structure and page speed, and the results show in organic traffic over time
- Analytics and conversion reporting that a GM can act on without engineering help
- Independent of Cox and Cars.com, so dealers avoid the renewal pull of those broader ecosystems

**Cons:**
- Smaller install base than Cox or Cars.com platforms, which means less negotiating room in vendor contracts and a smaller partner ecosystem
- Less tightly integrated with major DMSes than the Cox stack, so DMS-side workflows take more configuration
- Mid-market focused, with fewer enterprise group features for the largest dealer groups

**Verdict:** Pick DealerOn when organic traffic and analytics independence matter more than ecosystem bundling; skip it if your group already runs on Cox or Cars.com and the integration cost of switching outweighs the SEO upside.

## 5. Yext

[Yext](https://yext.com) is a knowledge graph and answers platform built around one job: keeping the same set of facts consistent everywhere a shopper might land. For a dealer group running multiple rooftops, that means hours, service policies, location data, and vehicle information stay in sync across every owned site and every third-party listing - Google, Apple Maps, Bing, voice assistants, and the long tail of directories.

**Best for:** Dealer groups managing many rooftops who need consistent hours, service, policy, and vehicle answers across owned sites and third-party listings.

**Pricing:** Quote-only, sold per location. Pricing scales with the count of rooftops and the modules enabled.

**Pros:**
- Strong multi-location answer governance - one source of truth pushed to owned sites, third-party listings, and voice assistants
- Mature knowledge graph that handles structured data well, which AI search engines and AI Overviews consume directly
- Established footprint with multi-rooftop dealer groups already using it for hours, locations, and FAQs

**Cons:**
- Answers-and-listings focused, not purpose-built for VDP-level vehicle discovery on a single rooftop's inventory
- Not built for the year/make/model faceted search that's the daily job of a single-rooftop site search bar
- Per-location pricing adds up fast across a large group, and higher-tier features sit behind enterprise contracts

**Verdict:** Pick Yext when the problem is consistent answers across many rooftops and third-party listings; skip it when the actual pain is one rooftop's site search returning zero results for a real in-stock vehicle.

## How should a dealership GM pick between these tools?

Start with what's actually broken. If the bigger problem is that the dealership website itself feels dated, the question is which platform to move to - and the deciding factor is usually which ecosystem you already run.
