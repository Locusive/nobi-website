## Which site search platform should you pick: Constructor, Algolia, or Nobi?

Nobi is one of the three products in this comparison and we built it - that bias exists. What we can do is be honest about what each tool does well and where it falls short, including Nobi.

These three tools represent fundamentally different approaches to the same problem: helping shoppers find products.

**Constructor** believes search should optimize for revenue. Its ML models learn which products convert and rank them accordingly. Search is a revenue lever, not a utility.

**Algolia** believes search should be a powerful, flexible API. Give developers the tools to build anything, and they will build the right experience for their store.

**Nobi** believes search should be a conversation. Instead of forcing shoppers to formulate the perfect query, let them describe what they want in plain language and guide them there.

None of these philosophies is wrong. But each leads to a very different product, and the right choice depends on which one matches your reality.

- **Nobi** - [AI site search](https://nobi.ai/blog/best-ai-search-ecommerce-2026) plus a shopping assistant with cited answers, $25/mo base ($0.01 per extra search, $0.10 per extra message). Pick when on-site discovery is the job and you want a transparent per-unit bill instead of a revenue-share contract or an engineering project.
- **[Constructor](https://constructor.io)** - enterprise product discovery with session-signal personalization across search, browse, category, and recommendations. Revenue-share pricing, no published list. Pick when CVR work has to span the full site and you have a data team to match.
- **[Algolia](https://algolia.com)** - developer-first search API with sub-50ms response times and granular control. Usage-based, around $2,000+/mo at mid-market scale before relevance engineering, with NeuralSearch gated to higher tiers. Pick when a search engineer is owning the rule engine.

## How does pricing compare?

The three use different pricing models - and the model matters as much as the headline number.

Nobi publishes a flat per-unit bill: $25/month base, including 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages, then $0.01 per additional search and $0.10 per additional message. A head of ecommerce can plug their own volumes into that math and forecast the bill before signing anything.

Algolia is usage-based on search requests and records indexed. Mid-market deployments typically land around $2,000+/month before any custom relevance engineering, and NeuralSearch is gated to higher tiers. The bill moves with traffic, so a strong campaign or a viral product page can produce a surprise overage in a month you can't predict.

Constructor is revenue-share with no published list price. The bill scales with GMV, which means a successful CVR lift directly increases what you pay Constructor next quarter. The most common post-signing complaint is that costs grow in ways the buyer didn't model going in.

## Which one delivers better AI relevance and personalization?

Constructor leads on personalization. Real-time session signals reorder results based on what the shopper has clicked, viewed, and added during the visit, and that reordering runs across search, browse, category pages, and recommendations on one model. If a shopper clicks two black ankle boots in search, the next category page reflects that.

Algolia is keyword-first by default. A query like "wide-leg cropped trouser" hits a product titled "cropped wide pant" only if your team has built the synonym group by hand or paid up for NeuralSearch. Custom ranking strategies, query rules, and merchandising configs are all programmable, but the work is yours to do. Strong when an engineering team wants to own the rule engine in code; weaker when you want relevance that works on day one.

Nobi ships AI-native semantic search out of the box, plus a shopping assistant that answers shopper questions with inline numbered citation pills tied to source documents. Connected sources refresh twice a day, so a policy or pricing change lands in answers within hours. In a two-month A/B test against their prior search tool, [UNTUCKit](/customers/untuckit) saw revenue per searcher rise from $32.30 to $39.17 (+21.3%) and moved Nobi to 100% of traffic. Honest trade-off: Nobi's personalization today is starter messages and placeholder text only. Behavioral reranking is on the roadmap but not shipped, so Constructor is genuinely deeper on individualized result ordering.

## How much engineering effort does each require?

Algolia is built for engineering teams. Rules, ranking, synonyms, indexing, and frontend rendering all live in code, and the relevance you ship is roughly proportional to the engineering hours you put in. Strong when you have a search engineer who wants to own the rule engine; weaker when you don't.

Constructor's rollouts typically run weeks to months. Getting full value from the personalization layer usually means involving internal data or analytics resources to validate models against your catalog and shopper signals. Strong when you have that team; weaker when the head of ecommerce wants something live this quarter.

Nobi installs in hours. Paste in the Nobi snippet and drop a placeholder where the search bar should appear. AI-native relevance ranks results from your catalog using semantic matching by default, so there's no synonym list to seed and no pinning rules to maintain for every query. A merchandiser handles overrides in a no-code dashboard. The honest trade-off: Nobi isn't an API-first developer platform. If you want to build your own ranking logic from the ground up or wire search into a highly bespoke frontend, Algolia is the better fit.

## Where does each platform's coverage stop?

Constructor covers the full site on one ranking model. Search, browse, category pages, collection pages, and recommendations all draw from the same engine, and in-session signals on any one page feed the others.

Algolia is a search-API foundation. The API is broad enough to power category pages, recommendations, and personalization, but each of those is its own engineering build. Strong when you have the team and want full control. Weaker when you want it to ship without an internal project.

Nobi curates the search results page and adds a shopping assistant on every page - homepage, PDP, cart - with contextual suggestion pills scoped to where the visitor is. [Kilte](/customers/kilte) runs Nobi across three places on the storefront. The honest gap: Nobi does not merchandise category or collection pages. Brands whose primary CVR lever is category-page merchandising will need a separate tool for that.

## When is Constructor the right pick?

Pick Constructor when CVR work has to move across the full site, GMV is in the $50M+ range, and there's a data team to keep the personalization layer tuned. The unified session-signal model across search, browse, category, and recommendations is what the contract is paying for. Skip it when you want transparent per-unit pricing, when search is a standalone problem, or when there's no internal data team to validate models on an ongoing basis.

## When is Algolia the right pick?

Pick Algolia when a search engineer is owning the ranking levers, you want full API control over rules and rendering, and you have the developer hours to keep relevance tuned. Skip it when no one will own that work, when surprise bills during traffic spikes would break the budget conversation, or when a shopping assistant needs to live in the same contract.

## When is Nobi the right pick?

Pick Nobi when on-site discovery is the bottleneck, you want [AI-native search](https://nobi.ai/blog/nobi-vs-algolia) and a cited shopping assistant in one platform, and you need transparent per-unit pricing instead of a revenue-share contract or a usage-based bill that spikes with traffic.

The proof is on the CVR side. [UNTUCKit](/customers/untuckit) ran a two-month A/B test and saw conversion rate move from 15.0% to 17.6% (+17.1%), revenue per searcher rise from $32.30 to $39.17 (+21.3%), and AOV go from $215 to $222 (+3.3%) before moving Nobi to 100% of traffic. [Kilte](/customers/kilte) saw a 21.7% CVR lift versus default Shopify search. [Lucchese](/customers/lucchese) attributes $1M+ incremental revenue and 39x ROI to Nobi in year one.

Skip Nobi when behavioral reranking across category and browse pages is the headline requirement - Constructor is genuinely deeper there. Skip it too when a search engineer wants raw API control over every ranking lever.

## What's the honest verdict?

If you're a $500M retailer with a 10-person data team, Constructor will make you more money than Nobi can today. That's the truth.

If you're building a headless commerce experience with engineers who want complete control, Algolia gives you more flexibility than Nobi does. That's also the truth.

But if you're like most ecommerce teams - a few people wearing multiple hats, a Shopify store, no dedicated developers, and a budget that doesn't include six-figure annual software contracts - the question isn't "which is the most powerful tool?" It's "which tool will actually improve my search this month?"

[According to Baymard Institute](https://baymard.com/blog/ecommerce-search-query-types), 72% of ecommerce sites fail basic search usability. If your store is in that 72%, the first step isn't buying the most expensive tool. It's buying the tool you'll actually implement.
