## What is conversational commerce?

You've probably typed a question into a chat box on a store's site and gotten a straight answer back instead of a page of search results - that's conversational commerce, and it's been around longer than most people think. It means talking your way through a purchase instead of clicking through it: a shopper states what they want in plain language, "do you have this in a size 10 that runs narrow," and gets a direct answer or a recommendation instead of guessing a search term or scrolling a product listing page. When that exchange breaks down, the shopper doesn't file a complaint. They just close the tab. Live chat, SMS, voice, and AI shopping assistants are all different ways of running that same exchange, and the rest of this piece walks through how each one works and where it fits.

## What is conversational commerce?

The category spans more channels than most people realize. Live chat widgets on a PDP, SMS and WhatsApp threads, voice assistants, and [AI shopping assistants embedded on-site](https://nobi.ai/blog/ai-shopping-assistant-ecommerce) all count as conversational commerce, not just whatever's newest.

It's also not a new idea. Live chat and SMS ordering have counted as conversational commerce for well over a decade, long before AI entered the picture. AI-powered assistants are the newest tactic inside the category, not a replacement for it. What's changed is how well the conversation can actually understand what a shopper means, not whether a conversation is happening at all.

## Why does conversational commerce matter for ecommerce brands?

It matters because two things quietly turn real buyers into search abandonment: a high zero-result rate, and a mismatch between what shoppers type and how your catalog is tagged. That cost rarely shows up cleanly in a dashboard. A marketing manager watching bounce rate stay high on paid landing pages, or watching [on-site email capture](https://nobi.ai/blog/capture-leads-from-chat) stay flat, is often watching this exact problem play out without a name for it. The understanding gap the last section pointed to isn't abstract. It shows up first as a null result: a shopper searches "waterproof running shoes" against a catalog tagged "trail runner, weatherproof," gets nothing back, and leaves.

Zero-result and null-result searches are one of the biggest silent leaks in on-site conversion, because the shopper never even sees a product. And getting the conversational layer wrong carries more risk than it used to: 71% of consumers abandon a brand after a single bad AI interaction. That raises the stakes on doing this well, not just having something in place. A search bar or assistant that gives a confusing answer or a dead-end result can cost you a customer permanently, not just a session.

The upside runs the same direction. Getting conversational commerce right shows up in add-to-cart rate and revenue per visit, the metrics a marketing manager already tracks, not just in fewer support tickets.

## How does conversational commerce actually work?

Under the hood, a conversational commerce system has to do three things. First, it has to understand what the shopper is asking, a step called intent detection. Second, it has to find the answer in something real, like a live catalog or a policy document, instead of a static training snapshot. Third, it has to decide when to hand off to a human. The gap between systems that get add-to-cart and revenue per visit moving and systems that don't comes down to that second step: grounding.

Not every system works the same way. Rule-based flows use fixed decision trees: a shopper picks from a menu of preset options, and the system walks a scripted path. They're predictable but brittle, and they break the moment a shopper asks something outside the script. LLM-based systems flip that: a shopper can type anything in natural language, and the model generates an open-ended response. That solves the vocabulary mismatch problem, but it opens a new one. An LLM answering from general training data will fill gaps with a plausible-sounding guess instead of admitting it doesn't know, what Gorgias calls a "confident lie": a fabricated feature, a wrong price, a return policy that was never written.

RAG-grounded systems are the fix. Instead of answering from memory, the model retrieves the actual product page or policy document first, then answers only from what it found. Grounding is also what prevents hallucinated inventory, the specific failure where a bot tells a shopper "yes, we have that in stock" or recommends something that's actually sold out, because it never checked a live feed before answering.

Grounding alone doesn't cover every case. Escalation triggers define when the system stops trying to answer and hands off to a person instead of guessing its way through. Without them, a shopper who asks something the system can't resolve gets a non-answer, restates the question, and gets the same non-answer back: a circular conversation that ends the way most bad AI interactions do, with the shopper gone.

## What tactics fall under conversational commerce today?

In practice, conversational commerce splits into several distinct tactics, and most brands end up combining more than one rather than picking a single winner. Live chat with a staffed inbox, messaging-app and SMS commerce, quiz-led product finders, scripted proactive-chat flows, PDP-specific Q&A, and open-ended AI shopping assistants are all real, different approaches, not interchangeable labels for the same thing.

[Tidio](https://www.tidio.com) pairs a staffed chat widget with an AI responder called Lyro, which covers after-hours questions and first-touch triage until a human agent picks up the thread. It's built for teams that want AI supporting a live agent, not replacing one. [Podium](https://www.podium.com) works from the other direction: it stitches SMS, web chat, reviews, and payments into a single inbox for brands whose customers would rather text than fill out a widget on a page.

Two tactics skip open-ended conversation entirely. [Octane AI](https://www.octaneai.com) resolves a short quiz to a specific SKU, useful when a catalog is wide and a shopper doesn't know what they're looking for yet. [Rep AI](https://www.hellorep.ai)'s Flow Studio lets marketing ops build a deterministic conversation path, a Black Friday promo or an abandoned-cart rescue, without engineering help, and layers in a live-agent drop-in plus in-chat post-purchase tasks on top.

[Alby](https://www.albyapp.com) answers questions inline on the product detail page itself, now folded into Bluecore's stack after Bluecore acquired it. [Gorgias](https://www.gorgias.com) sits after the sale: it handles WISMO, returns, and exchange-over-refund steering, with Shopify order context inside every ticket.

## How do AI shopping assistants like Nobi fit into conversational commerce?

[AI shopping assistants are the newest branch of conversational commerce](https://nobi.ai/blog/ai-changing-online-shopping), and Nobi is one implementation: a conversational assistant that answers pre-purchase product and policy questions from a brand's own connected content, paired with a fast semantic search bar, on one $25/month base plan (2,500 searches and 250 messages included). It's built specifically to address the "confident lie" fear that keeps some marketing teams from trusting AI on-site.

That trust problem breaks into three separate fears, and Nobi answers each one directly. First, the fabrication fear: every answer carries an inline numbered citation pill, and hovering over it shows the source document, the date, and the exact excerpt the answer came from. A shopper or an operator can check the claim against the original page instead of taking the AI's word for it. Second, the paraphrasing risk on high-stakes questions: a return policy or warranty prompt can be locked to an exact, merchant-approved answer through a query override, so there's no LLM variation on the prompts that carry the most risk. Third, the stale-data fear: connected content refreshes twice a day, so a policy or price change reaches customer-facing answers within hours, not after the next model retrain.

One honest limit is worth naming here. Nobi's current personalization is personalized placeholder text and starter messages. Dedicated personalization platforms go further: they rerank results based on an individual shopper's click or purchase history. That behavioral reranking is a supporting piece of Nobi's product today, not the reason to choose it.

## Frequently asked questions about conversational commerce

**Is conversational commerce the same as a scripted messaging bot?** No. A scripted messaging bot is one implementation. Live chat with a staffed inbox, SMS and messaging threads, voice assistants, and AI shopping assistants all count as conversational commerce too, and most brands run more than one at once.

**Does conversational commerce require AI?** No. Staffed live chat and SMS programs have run for well over a decade without any AI layer. AI is an accelerant that makes the conversation understand more of what a shopper means. It's not a requirement for the category to exist.

**How is it different from a search bar?** A search bar returns ranked results for a typed query. Conversational commerce answers an open-ended question or holds a back-and-forth exchange, closer to how a shopper would ask a salesperson.

**Is voice commerce part of conversational commerce?** Yes. A voice assistant that lets a shopper ask a question or place an order by speaking is a channel within the category, though adoption on ecommerce sites specifically still trails chat and messaging.

**What's the biggest risk brands should watch for?** An ungrounded system: one that fabricates a feature, a price, or a policy, or claims stock that doesn't exist, instead of checking real, current content before it answers.
