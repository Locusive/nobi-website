## Why look for a DealerOn alternative?

DealerOn is a solid choice for dealers who prioritize organic search performance and want a website platform independent of the Cox or Cars.com ecosystems. Its SEO-forward "Cobalt 2.0" sites and analytics are real strengths, and its per-rooftop pricing keeps it independent of a bigger platform bundle. But dealers usually start looking elsewhere once the job shifts from ranking the website to [answering shopper questions fast and capturing more of the leads already landing on it](https://nobi.ai/blog/dealership-lead-capture): speed to lead on internet ups, after-hours coverage, and a safer way to handle pricing and inventory questions than a generic AI bolt-on.

## How did we evaluate these DealerOn alternatives?

We scored each tool on four criteria that matter to a dealership marketing manager: fit for the actual job, pricing transparency, implementation time, and safety against the two failure modes dealers fear most - quoting a sold car or the wrong price, and hallucinating inventory that's actually on the lot. Nobi is one of the tools evaluated here, alongside Dealer.com, DealerInspire, and fusionZONE.

Job fit split the list in two. Dealer.com and DealerInspire are full website platforms: they replace the site itself, from VDPs and SRPs down to the CMS. fusionZONE plays the same role for independent rooftops. Nobi isn't a website platform at all. It answers shopper questions and runs search on top of whatever site you already run, so it's not a fair substitute if what you actually need is a new site. It is a fair substitute if the job is answering shopper questions and capturing more of the leads your current site already attracts.

Pricing transparency varied across the list. Dealer.com and DealerInspire both run per-rooftop subscriptions with a custom quote once you factor in modules and dealer-group scale. fusionZONE doesn't publish pricing at all. Nobi's is public and usage-based: $25/month base, including 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages, with per-unit overage beyond that.

Implementation time was the sharpest split. A Dealer.com or DealerInspire rollout is a services-led project measured in weeks to months, since it involves rebuilding the site itself. Nobi's install is a small addition to the site you already have, live in hours.

Answer safety mattered most, given what's at stake. After incidents like a dealership AI assistant agreeing to sell a Tahoe for a dollar, we checked whether each tool's pricing and inventory answers are grounded in verifiable, current source content, rather than a general model guessing at what's sitting on your lot.

## 2. Dealer.com

[Dealer.com](https://dealer.com) is Cox Automotive's flagship dealership website platform, powering tens of thousands of rooftops with inventory-driven sites, digital retailing widgets, and integrated lead capture. For a marketing manager weighing a DealerOn switch, the pitch is consolidation: one vendor for the website, the CRM, the DMS, and the listings feed instead of four separate contracts. Dealer.com pairs tightly with VinSolutions CRM and Dealertrack DMS, so leads and inventory data move between systems without custom syncing. The tradeoff is that you're buying into Cox Automotive's ecosystem, not just a website vendor, and that decision tends to shape every renewal that follows.

**Best for:** Multi-rooftop dealer groups already standardized on VinSolutions, Dealertrack, and Autotrader who want their website, CRM, DMS, and listings on one vendor stack.

**Pricing:** Per-rooftop monthly subscription, tiered by site modules. Custom quote for dealer groups; bundled discounts with other Cox Automotive products.

**Pros:**
- Site architecture built for inventory, handling VDP and SRP volume at scale without custom development

**Cons:**
- Cox Automotive ecosystem lock-in (works tightly with VinSolutions, Dealertrack DMS, Autotrader)
- Templated site experience; deep customization requires Cox-managed services or premium tier
- Per-rooftop pricing scales fast for dealer groups

**Verdict:** Pick Dealer.com when you're already deep in the Cox stack and want the simplest path for the website layer. Skip it if avoiding further Cox lock-in is the priority.

## 3. DealerInspire

[DealerInspire](https://dealerinspire.com) is Cars.com's dealership website platform, built around a page-builder with conversational widgets and digital-retailing tools already built in. For a marketing manager who wants more design flexibility than a rigid templated site, that's the pitch: campaign pages and inventory landing pages go up faster, and the conversational and financing widgets are part of the core product instead of a separate vendor to onboard. The bigger draw is what sits behind it. If your rooftop already feeds inventory to Cars.com and buys leads back from it, putting the website on the same account means one renewal conversation instead of three. The tradeoff is that you're buying into that ecosystem at a premium price point, not shopping a website vendor on its own merits.

**Best for:** Marketing managers at dealerships already running on Cars.com's inventory feed and lead-management tools who want their website in the same vendor relationship.

**Pricing:** Per-rooftop monthly subscription, tiered. Premium positioning; custom quote.

**Pros:**
- Native ties to Cars.com's inventory feed and lead-management tools cut down on duplicate data entry

**Cons:**
- Cars.com Group ecosystem lock-in (paired with Cars.com inventory and lead-management)
- Premium positioning; pricing higher than entry-level platforms
- Conversational AI features still maturing relative to dedicated AI-first vendors

**Verdict:** Pick DealerInspire when you're already on Cars.com's inventory and lead-management stack and want your website in that same relationship. Skip it if you want an AI answer layer that's further along than a still-maturing feature set.

## Frequently asked questions

**Does adding an AI assistant mean replacing our current website platform?**

No. Nobi and similar answer-layer tools sit on top of the site you already run instead of replacing it. The query overrides and citation pills from the previous section work the same way here: they ground answers in what's already published on your VDPs and policy pages, without touching your CMS, your DMS integration, or your inventory feed.

**How do these tools prevent a bot from quoting a sold car or the wrong price?**

Query overrides let you pin an exact, dealer-approved answer to a specific question, like financing terms or a pricing policy, so it fires the same way every time with no AI paraphrasing. Everything else routes through a grounded answer pipeline that cites the VDP or spec sheet it pulled from, so a wrong number is traceable to a source instead of invented by a model guessing at what's on the lot.

**Will an AI assistant hurt our CSI score?**

The risk comes from answers a shopper can't verify, or a bot that loops without resolving anything, not from AI itself. Citations back to the source and a clear handoff to a person cut down on both failure modes.

**Do we still need a website platform if we add an AI assistant?**

Yes. The assistant answers questions and captures leads. It doesn't host your inventory feed, run your CMS, or manage your DMS integration, so it pairs with your existing platform rather than standing in for it.
