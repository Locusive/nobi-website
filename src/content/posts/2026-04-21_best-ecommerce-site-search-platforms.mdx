## What should ecommerce teams prioritize when choosing a site search platform today?

Your shortlist should rank four things: semantic understanding (does the engine handle the words shoppers actually type, not just the words in your product titles), how much manual merchandising work it demands from your team, total cost at your real query volume rather than the sticker price, and how many weeks pass before implementation produces a measurable CVR lift. Platforms that ace the first two but ignore the last two tend to demo beautifully and underwhelm in production.

Semantic matters most for catalogs with editorial naming. Fashion, beauty, and home goods shoppers type "brown boots" when the product is cataloged as the Cappuccino Boot. Nobi's color-normalization layer maps brand vocabulary to shopper vocabulary, so a Kilte shopper searching "brown boots" surfaces the Cappuccino Boot even though the word "brown" never appears in the product record.

Merchandising rules are real headcount. AI-native ranking shrinks that work without eliminating it. Pricing models (flat, per-query, per-order, revenue share) vary wildly, so model your annual volume before signing. Treat personalization as a line item, not a footnote.

## Which site search platforms should mid-market ecommerce brands evaluate?

The platforms most often shortlisted by mid-market ecommerce teams are Nobi, Algolia, Klevu, Searchspring, Bloomreach, Constructor, and Fast Simon. Each sits in a distinct slot, AI-native versus developer-first versus enterprise versus low-cost Shopify app, and the right pick depends more on your team shape and budget model than on raw feature comparisons.

Nobi is the AI-native option. Results learn from shopper behavior without manual rule-tuning, and the same platform handles conversational discovery and [lead capture](https://nobi.ai/lead-capture), so you aren't stitching three vendors together. Pricing is usage-based: $25/month covers 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages, then $0.01 per extra search and $0.10 per extra message. A brand running 50,000 searches a month (and staying within the included 250 messages) pays the $25 base plus $475 in search overage, roughly $500/month on searches alone, still well below enterprise contracts. Built for fast Shopify and Shopify Plus rollouts measured in days rather than months.

Algolia is the most flexible developer platform. Pick it if you have engineers who want to own the search experience end to end and don't mind wiring relevance, ranking, and merchandising themselves. Algolia publishes pricing per 1,000 search requests and per 1,000 records, which scales predictably but adds up at mid-market query volumes.

Klevu has strong AI search for Shopify catalogs, though category merchandising is sold as a paid add-on rather than included, and Klevu doesn't publish pricing publicly.

Searchspring is the merchandising-first pick. Its workflows are mature for category managers running active campaigns, though the underlying relevance leans more on rules than on AI inference.

Bloomreach is an enterprise suite combining search with content management and a CDP. Built for larger brands with complex catalogs, pricing is quote-only and based on customers served plus catalog size.

Constructor is AI-first for enterprise product discovery, sold on a revenue-share model that scales with GMV rather than query volume.

Fast Simon is the affordable Shopify-focused pick, best fit for smaller catalogs and teams that want quick install over deep customization.

## How does pricing compare across site search platforms?

Pricing varies more by billing model than by sticker price, and the model you pick matters more than the headline number. [Nobi starts at $25/month](https://nobi.ai/pricing) with 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages included, then charges $0.01 per additional search and $0.10 per additional message. Algolia's Grow plan bills $0.50 per 1,000 search requests above the 10,000-request free tier, with Grow Plus at $1.75 per 1,000. Klevu and Searchspring both quote on request and tier by catalog size and order volume, with Klevu typically starting in the low-to-mid double digits per month and Searchspring aimed at mid-market budgets. Bloomreach uses custom pricing built from a module fee plus a usage fee tied to customers served, catalog size, and event volume, and Fast Simon proposes implementation pricing based on tech stack and scope. Constructor runs on a revenue-share model, so costs scale with attributed GMV.

Each model fails in a different way. Per-query pricing looks cheap on an average month and stings on a spike, so model your worst month, not your median. Revenue-share sounds aligned until conversion actually improves and the bill climbs with it. Enterprise platforms like Bloomreach and Constructor rarely publish list pricing, and real quotes tend to land north of $50k/year once you account for minimums.

Honest note on Nobi: the $25 base is accurate for small stores, but it stops being the relevant number at real mid-market volume. A brand running 100,000 searches and 10,000 conversational messages a month is closer to $1,000/month once overages are in. That is still predictable and published, which is the point, but run your actual volume against the per-search and per-message rates before you treat Nobi as the cheap option on the shortlist.

## When is Algolia or Bloomreach the better choice than Nobi?

Algolia is the right call when you have a dedicated search or platform engineering team that wants full control of the indexing pipeline, ranking signals, and frontend UI, and is happy to own the ongoing relevance tuning. Bloomreach is the right call when you're a true enterprise brand that needs search, content management, and customer data unified in one suite, and you have the budget and timeline (months, not weeks) for a services-led implementation. Nobi is not the better fit in either of those scenarios.

Pick Algolia if your engineers want to write search logic in code rather than configure it through an admin UI. Algolia's API-first model gives developers direct access to index settings, ranking rules, and query parameters, which is exactly what you want if you've staffed a search team to own relevance. It's also the better pick if your catalog is very large or structurally unusual (multi-entity indexes, heavy faceting, non-product records like articles and locations mixed into results) and needs bespoke indexing logic that a configuration-driven product won't cover.

Pick Bloomreach if you're consolidating search, content, and customer data onto a single enterprise vendor. Bloomreach sells Discovery alongside Experience Manager and Engagement, and the pitch only pays off if you're actually buying the suite. It's also the safer pick if your procurement team requires deep enterprise references, negotiated SLAs, and a services-led rollout. Pricing there is custom and module-based, which means contracts, not a checkout page.

One more honest cut: Nobi isn't built for stores under roughly $1M GMV. At that size, a Shopify-native app like Fast Simon will get you 80% of the way for a fraction of the integration effort, and you won't have the search volume to justify a dedicated platform yet. Nobi earns its keep once you're past that threshold and relevance actually moves revenue.

## What kind of conversion lift can a better site search platform actually deliver?

Real, measured A/B test lifts from swapping in a better site search platform usually land in the 15% to 25% conversion rate range, as long as the new platform actually understands the words shoppers type. That's the honest benchmark. Anything much higher than that usually means a very bad starting point or a cherry-picked window.

UNTUCKit ran the clearest test we have. Over two months, they split traffic between Nobi and their prior search tool. Conversion rate moved from 15.0% to 17.6%, a 17.1% lift. Revenue per searcher went from $32.30 to $39.17, up 21.3%. AOV ticked up from $215 to $222. After the test, they moved 100% of traffic to Nobi. They also ran a separate head-to-head against their paid incumbent, Findify, and Nobi came in at 17.4% CVR versus Findify's 16.5%. A real margin, not a landslide. Findify is a credible AI search and merchandising product for Shopify brands, so the fact that the gap was modest is worth stating plainly.

Other brands have seen bigger swings when the starting point was worse. Kilte, a DTC fashion brand, lifted CVR 21.7% against Shopify's default search. The mechanism was narrow and concrete: shoppers typing "brown boots" started finding the "Cappuccino Boot" because Nobi maps brand color vocabulary to what people actually type. Faherty ran a head-to-head against another AI search tool and measured 12.3% search CVR against the competitor's 10.1%. Lucchese attributed over $1M in incremental year-one revenue and a 39x ROI.

The pattern: the lift is bigger when you're replacing default keyword search, smaller when you're replacing a competent AI rival, but measurable in both cases.

## How long does a site search migration typically take?

Implementation timelines vary by an order of magnitude across this category. Shopify-native AI search tools like Nobi, Klevu, and Fast Simon typically go live in days to a couple of weeks. Algolia takes weeks to months depending on engineering bandwidth and how much custom UI you build. Bloomreach, Constructor, and Coveo are enterprise rollouts that usually run three to six months with a services engagement attached.

For Shopify and Shopify Plus brands, Nobi installs from the app store without engineering work. Most brands are live within a week, and the heavier lift is reviewing search behavior in the first few weeks and tuning merchandising, not standing up the integration itself. Klevu and Fast Simon follow a similar shape: app-store install, configuration in the admin, and a short period of catalog syncing and tuning before going live. Fast Simon notes that implementation pricing scales with the level of involvement from their team, so timelines stretch if you want hands-on setup help.

Algolia is a different shape of project. You're wiring up indexing pipelines, configuring ranking and rules, and building or customizing the search UI in your storefront code. Plan four to twelve weeks minimum, longer if you're swapping out an existing search stack or building a custom autocomplete experience.

Bloomreach, Constructor, and Coveo all come with implementation services baked in, and three to six months is a realistic window for go-live. That timeline reflects catalog modeling, integration with your CDP and merchandising workflows, and training for the people who'll run it day-to-day.

Why this matters: conversion lift compounds. If a better search experience adds three to seven percent to revenue (the range from the prior section), every week of delay is lift you didn't capture. For mid-market Shopify brands, the days-to-weeks options usually pencil out faster than a six-month rollout, even if the enterprise platforms eventually land in a similar place.

## How should a Head of Ecommerce actually pick a site search platform?

Start by writing down three numbers: your monthly search volume, your engineering bandwidth in person-weeks for the next quarter, and the CVR lift you'd need to justify the spend. Those three numbers eliminate most of the shortlist on their own. From whatever's left, run a head-to-head A/B test on real traffic for at least four weeks before committing. Every credible vendor in this category will agree to that, and the ones that resist are telling you something.

Model total annual cost at your actual query volume, not the sticker price. A brand doing 50,000 searches a month on Nobi pays the $25 base plus about $475 in overage, roughly $6,000 a year. Plug your own numbers into each vendor's pricing page before you get attached to a demo.

Match the engineering lift to the bandwidth you actually have this quarter, not the bandwidth you're hoping to hire into.

Insist on a live A/B test against your incumbent. UNTUCKit did this and saw Nobi hit 17.4% CVR versus Findify's 16.5%. Faherty ran Nobi against another AI search tool and measured 12.3% versus 10.1%. Kilte tested against Shopify's default and got a 21.7% lift. Ask any vendor on your shortlist for [three customers willing to share numbers like those](https://nobi.ai/customers). Vague "lift" claims without figures are a red flag.

Finally, decide upfront whether you want search only or search plus conversational AI. Bolting chat on a year later means a second integration, a second contract, and a second data model. Buying both bundled is cheaper and cleaner if you know you'll want it.
