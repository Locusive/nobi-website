## What actually makes a site search platform 'AI-powered' in 2026?

AI-powered site search means the engine reads intent and context, not just keyword matches. It parses natural language, normalizes synonyms and brand-specific vocabulary, ranks results based on what shoppers actually click and buy, and personalizes for the visitor in front of it. If a vendor still asks you to hand-write synonym dictionaries, that is keyword search wearing an AI label.

The bar in 2026 has four parts. First, semantic understanding: a query for "brown boots" should surface the Cappuccino Boot even when the word "brown" appears nowhere in the product record. [Nobi does exactly this](https://nobi.ai/why-nobi/better-search) for fashion brand Kilte, mapping editorial color names like Cappuccino, Ivory, and Écru to the everyday vocabulary shoppers actually type. Second, behavioral ranking that re-orders results based on clicks, add-to-carts, and purchases instead of static relevance scores. Third, personalization for returning visitors and segments out of the box, not gated behind an enterprise tier. Fourth, graceful zero-result handling: fall back to related categories or ask a clarifying question, never serve a dead-end page.

A conversational layer should run on the same intent model, so higher-consideration questions get answered with the same product knowledge driving search.

## Which AI site search platforms should an ecommerce team actually evaluate?

On the Shopify-native side, where engineering lift is low and install-to-launch can be measured in days: Nobi, Klevu, Fast Simon, Findify, and Boost Commerce. On the developer-platform side, where you're building on top of an API and expect to write ranking logic yourself: Algolia and Constructor. On the enterprise commerce cloud side, where search is one module inside a broader suite: Bloomreach and Coveo. And then Searchspring, which sits in the middle as a mid-market merchandising-led pick that leans more on rule-based control than AI-native ranking.

The camp you belong in usually decides the shortlist before feature comparisons start. If you're an enterprise retailer with a headless storefront and a dedicated search team, Boost Commerce or Findify won't give you the control you need. Constructor and Coveo publish no fixed list price, so evaluations there tend to involve a sales cycle before you know what you're spending; Coveo's public third-party pricing references start around $600 a month for the base plan and climb into five figures for enterprise.

One thing that separates Nobi from every other name on this list: it bundles AI search, a conversational shopping assistant, and [lead capture](https://nobi.ai/lead-capture) under a single contract. Most others are search or merchandising only, and if you want a chat assistant you're buying a second tool. The sections below walk through where each trade-off actually matters.

## How much does AI-powered site search actually cost?

AI site search pricing spans roughly three orders of magnitude depending on vendor. Shopify-native apps start in the $25–$500/month range with usage-metered components. Enterprise platforms run $60K–$250K+ per year with multi-month implementations. Revenue-share vendors avoid an upfront sticker price but can become the most expensive line item once GMV scales. The cleanest way to compare is to model two years of cost at projected query volume. That is when revenue-share and per-query pricing diverge from flat plans.

Here is roughly where each tier lands:

- **Nobi**: $25/month base, including 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages. Overage is $0.01 per additional search and $0.10 per additional message. A brand running 50,000 searches a month lands around $500, not a percentage of revenue.
- **Algolia**: usage-based at $0.50 per 1K search requests on the Grow plan, $1.75 per 1K on Grow Plus, with NeuralSearch gated to higher tiers. Mid-market deployments commonly run $500–$5,000/month before custom relevance work.
- **Klevu**: starts around $249/month. Smart Category Merchandising is a paid add-on, and personalization is typically priced as a separate module.
- **Searchspring**: typically $799/month and up depending on traffic and modules, with annual-pay discounts and special rates for early-stage and MWBE brands.
- **Constructor**: revenue-share with no published list price. The most common complaint is surprise costs as GMV grows.
- **Bloomreach** and **Coveo**: enterprise contracts in the $60K–$250K+/year range. Coveo's third-party list pricing starts near $600/month for Base but climbs fast once annual licensing ($50K+), implementation ($20K+), and professional services ($200–$300/hour) are added.
- **Findify**: Essential plan at $499/month, Professional at $799, Enterprise at $1,399. **Fast Simon**: starts around $99/month via the Shopify App Store.

If a vendor refuses to give you a starting number on the first call, that is the pricing model. [Transparent pricing](https://nobi.ai/pricing) is one of Nobi's deliberate tradeoffs: predictable per-unit overages instead of a GMV tax or a custom quote.

## When is a competitor the right call instead of Nobi?

Nobi is the default pick for a mid-market ecommerce brand that wants AI search and a conversational assistant live in days with predictable pricing. But it is genuinely the wrong buy for a few specific shapes of team. Here is who should look elsewhere.

**Choose Algolia** if you have a dedicated search engineering team and want full API control over ranking, indexing, and the frontend. Algolia is a developer platform first, with a free tier for 10K requests a month and usage-based overages ($0.50 per additional 1K requests on Grow, $1.75 on Grow Plus). Nobi is not an API-first dev platform, and teams who want to hand-roll their own relevance logic will feel constrained.

**Choose Searchspring** if your merchandising team needs manual control over category and collection pages site-wide, not just the search results page. Nobi curates the search results page and lets behavior do the ranking work; it does not manage site-wide category merchandising. Searchspring has been built around that workflow for years.

**Choose Bloomreach** if you are an enterprise north of $100M GMV consolidating search, personalization, content, and CDP into one contract. Bloomreach sells a modular platform priced on catalog size, customers served, and events. That is a different purchase than a point tool.

**Choose Constructor** if you have a very large catalog, a data team to shape behavioral training, and you are comfortable with revenue-share pricing that scales with GMV.

**Choose Coveo** if you are unifying ecommerce search with support and internal knowledge search across the business at enterprise scale. Coveo's base plans start around $600/month with enterprise contracts running materially higher.

**Choose Klevu or Fast Simon** if you want a search-only vendor and do not want a conversational assistant bundled in. Both are strong on Shopify and focus narrowly on search and merchandising.

## What should you ask every AI site search vendor before signing?

Most evaluations go sideways because buyers ask about features instead of operating realities. A short list of pointed questions, about A/B test evidence, pricing at scale, implementation time, and what happens to relevance when your catalog shifts, separates real AI search platforms from rebranded keyword engines. These are the questions that actually predict whether a deployment will deliver lift in quarter one.

Ask for a controlled A/B test with a real holdout group, not a flattering before-and-after case study. Ask what your bill looks like at 2x your current traffic, and get the answer in writing. Ask how long from contract signature to live in production: mid-market should expect days to a few weeks, not a six-month services engagement. Ask what you or your team have to do when 500 new SKUs land in the catalog for relevance to stay tuned. Ask whether personalization is included or priced as a separate module. Ask how zero-result queries get handled: fallback page, conversational clarification, or a dead-end empty state. Ask what the hand-off looks like between search and a conversational assistant when a shopper has a higher-consideration question. And ask where the tool stops. For Nobi: it curates the search results page, not site-wide collection merchandising, so brands that need full-site merchandising should pair it with a dedicated tool. UNTUCKit reviews Nobi's search insights, including zero-result queries and shopper intent, in a standing weekly meeting. That's the cadence a good vendor enables.
