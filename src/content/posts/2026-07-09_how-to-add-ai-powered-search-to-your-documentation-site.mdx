## How do you add AI-powered search to a documentation site?

A visitor lands on your docs with a real question, types it into the search bar in their own words, and gets nothing back. The page they needed is right there, it's just titled differently than they phrased it. That mismatch is the actual problem: keyword search only matches the words a writer chose, not the words a visitor types, so close variations turn into null results and, eventually, support tickets or a visitor who just leaves. Add AI-powered search by connecting your help articles, FAQs, and PDFs to a grounded layer that matches on meaning instead of exact keywords, with a citation on every answer so visitors can check it themselves. Here's what that setup looks like, and what it costs to run.

## Why does keyword search fail on documentation and help-center sites?

Keyword search fails on documentation because visitors phrase questions in their own words, not the exact terms your docs use. A visitor searching "do I need to reset after a return" won't match a doc titled "Exchange Policy," even though that page has the answer. That's the vocabulary mismatch problem: the words a person types rarely line up with the words a writer chose months earlier, and [keyword matching has no way to bridge the two](https://nobi.ai/blog/ai-vs-traditional-site-search). Every close variation that doesn't match returns a null result, and the visitor leaves.

That zero-result rate compounds as your docs library grows. More pages means more ways to phrase the same question, and more chances for a real query to miss every one of them. Search abandonment on a docs site doesn't show up as a bounce metric either. It shows up as an unnecessary support ticket, or as a visitor who just leaves without filing anything at all.

That second case is the one worth worrying about. It's the invisible churn problem: the people turned off by a bad search experience never contact you, they just go. And if you patch a null-result search with a bot that guesses instead of admitting it doesn't know, you can make it worse.

## What is Nobi, and how does it make documentation search understand plain-language questions?

That gap between a confident wrong answer and a real one is exactly what Nobi is built to close. Nobi is a conversational website assistant that reads your existing documentation, help articles, FAQ routes, PDFs, policy pages, and answers visitor questions in plain language with an inline citation on every claim. [Instead of matching keywords, it matches meaning](https://nobi.ai/blog/how-ai-site-search-works), so a question phrased any way still routes to the right source, and the visitor can check the answer against the exact document it came from without leaving the page.

Building the knowledge base doesn't require re-entering anything. Connect a URL or upload a PDF, and that content becomes part of what the assistant can answer. There's no manual copy-paste into a separate system, and no rewriting your docs to fit a search tool's format.

Every answer also carries a numbered citation pill. Hover over it and you see the source document's name, its date, and the exact excerpt the answer was built from. A visitor who wants to double-check a claim about a return window or a setup step doesn't have to take the assistant's word for it. They can trace it back themselves, in the same view.

Under the hood, Nobi routes each query automatically. A short lookup, like a product name or a single term, takes a fast instant-search path built for speed. A full question, the kind that needs a real answer rather than a list of pages, gets a grounded conversational response instead. The visitor never has to know which path they're on.

Pricing runs $25 a month as a base, which includes 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages. Beyond that, additional searches run $0.01 each and additional messages run $0.10 each.

## How do I connect my documentation content to Nobi's search?

Connect Nobi to your documentation the same way you'd point someone to it: give it the URL. Nobi indexes existing help-center pages, FAQ routes, product pages, or uploaded PDFs and policy docs, and that content becomes searchable and answerable within hours. There's no re-entry, no separate content pipeline, and no rewriting your docs to fit a new format.

That matters because most docs already live somewhere. If your help center is a set of published articles, connect those URLs. If policies live in PDFs (a warranty document, a shipping policy, a terms page), upload the file directly. If FAQ answers sit on a dedicated route, connect that too. Nobi doesn't require any of it to be reformatted or moved first. It reads what you already have.

Once connected, that content becomes one knowledge base, not two. The same source material powers the fast instant-search bar, which returns ranked results for a short query, and the conversational assistant, which answers a full question with a citation back to the exact document it came from. You're not maintaining a separate index for search and a separate one for the assistant. Add a new help article or update a policy page, and both surfaces draw from the update at once.

## How do I stop the search assistant from giving wrong or fabricated answers?

Stop fabricated answers with two layers. Query overrides pin an exact, merchant-approved response to any high-stakes question, so a return policy or warranty question comes back word-for-word with no LLM paraphrasing. An optional second AI review then re-checks every other draft answer against the cited source before it ships. Together, they target the "confident lie" failure mode operators worry about most: the bot stating a policy or feature that doesn't actually exist.

Overrides work for the questions you already know are risky. If your return window changed last month, or your warranty terms have edge cases a general answer would get wrong, you lock the exact wording in advance. When a visitor's question matches, that locked answer fires every time. Nothing gets reworded, summarized, or generated fresh. Everything else routes through the standard grounded pipeline, where Nobi answers from your connected sources with an inline citation.

The fact-check pass covers what overrides don't: the long tail of questions you can't pre-write answers for. Before an answer sends, a second AI call reads the raw content from the source it was built on and is asked to find inaccuracies. Only answers that clear that check go out. This is on by default for high-consideration content and can be turned off per merchant if it doesn't fit your setup.

Even when an answer isn't overridden or specifically checked, it still isn't a black box. Every response carries a citation pill a visitor can hover over to see the source document, its date, and the exact excerpt it came from. That citation is what turns "trust the bot" into "check the bot," which matters more than any single accuracy safeguard.

## How do I keep documentation answers accurate as content changes?

Citations tell a visitor whether an answer is still right, but the faster fix is making sure it doesn't go stale in the first place. Nobi's connected knowledge sources refresh twice a day, so a documentation update, a changed policy, a new feature, a corrected spec, lands in the assistant's answers within hours instead of waiting on a stale, retrained model. That real-time sync is what prevents the other common failure mode operators worry about: a bot confidently repeating information that was true last quarter but isn't anymore.

There's no separate step to trigger. You edit the doc page the way you always do, publish it, and Nobi picks up the change on its next refresh cycle. No retraining a model, no manual reindex, no waiting on an engineering ticket to push an update live. That matters most for the content that changes without warning: a return window that shifts for a holiday season, a feature that ships mid-quarter, a spec correction after a support escalation. Static, retrained search tools carry that lag as a matter of course.

The refresh applies to the whole knowledge base, not just one surface. The same twice-daily sync updates both the instant-search index and the conversational assistant's answers, since they read from the same connected sources. Update a policy page once, and both the search bar and the assistant reflect it by the next cycle.

## How do I measure whether AI-powered documentation search is actually working?

Track it the way you'd track any other change to the site: zero-result rate on search queries, ticket volume for the questions the assistant now answers directly, and, if the docs sit on a commerce storefront, downstream conversion. A falling zero-result rate is the earliest signal. Lower ticket volume and conversion lift follow once visitors start trusting the answers they get.

Zero-result rate matters most because it's the most direct read on the vocabulary mismatch problem from earlier. As semantic matching closes the gap between how visitors phrase a question and how your docs are titled, fewer searches should come back empty. That number should trend down within the first few weeks of connecting your content, and it's worth checking before you look at anything downstream.

Ticket volume is the second signal, and it moves on a similar timeline. Watch the categories that generate the most tickets today, usually policy questions and product specs, and see whether that volume drops once the assistant is answering those questions directly on the page. A visitor who gets a cited answer to "what's your return window on sale items" doesn't need to file a ticket to ask the same thing.

If your documentation lives on a commerce storefront, tie search sessions to revenue per visit and conversion, not just support metrics. A visitor who finds the answer to a pre-purchase question is a visitor who's more likely to buy, and that's a number worth tracking alongside ticket counts.

Some teams fold this into a recurring habit rather than a one-time check. That turns search data into an ongoing input for content planning instead of a report nobody revisits.

## When should I use a dedicated search platform like Algolia or Coveo instead of Nobi?

Pick [Algolia](https://algolia.com) when you have a search engineer who wants full API control over ranking in code and is prepared to build a separate answer layer on top for visitor questions. Pick [Coveo](https://coveo.com) when you're unifying documentation search with support portals and internal knowledge under one enterprise ML-relevance engine, and you have the budget and sales cycle for it. [Nobi fits teams who want a grounded, citation-backed answer layer live in hours, without an engineering project or an enterprise contract](https://nobi.ai/blog/best-ai-search-for-developer-documentation).

Algolia is a search API. It returns fast, relevant results once your team configures the ranking, but it doesn't include an answer layer out of the box. A visitor asking "do I need to reset after a return" still needs something built on top of Algolia's API to turn that question into an answer, and that's engineering work your team owns.

Coveo is built for a bigger job: pulling documentation, support-ticket history, and internal knowledge bases into one relevance engine. That's the right scope if you're consolidating search across several properties at once. It's usually out of reach otherwise, since Coveo sells through a sales-led process with services costs attached.

Neither is the right fit if the actual gap is somewhere else. Nobi doesn't run a ticket workflow, so if you need agent-side case routing and macros, pair it with a helpdesk like [Zendesk](https://zendesk.com) or [Gorgias](https://gorgias.com). And Nobi answers in web chat only, with no email channel, so a team whose main support line is email needs a different tool for that piece.
