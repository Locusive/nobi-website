## What is AI semantic search for ecommerce?

AI semantic search uses language models and vector embeddings to match shopper queries to products [by meaning, not by keyword overlap](https://nobi.ai/blog/ai-vs-traditional-site-search) with the product record. A shopper typing "something warm for skiing" surfaces insulated jackets even when the word "ski" never appears in the catalog. A search for "brown boots" returns a product the brand calls the Cappuccino Boot. The result is more relevant results, fewer zero-result queries, and a direct lift to conversion rate on search-led sessions.

The mechanism is straightforward. Each product gets encoded as a high-dimensional vector that captures its meaning, not just its tokens. Each query gets encoded the same way. The engine ranks products by vector similarity, so "warm jacket for the slopes" lands near "insulated ski parka" even with zero shared words. Most production systems run hybrid retrieval - semantic vectors blended with classic BM25 keyword scoring - so exact SKU lookups and brand-name searches still hit the right product instantly.

The unlock for ecommerce shows up on the long tail: evocative product names, editorial color palettes, and natural-language queries the keyword index returns zero or wrong results for. [Kilte](/customers/kilte) saw this directly - Nobi's color-normalization layer maps "brown boots" to the Cappuccino Boot without a merchandiser hand-pinning the rule.

## How much can AI semantic search actually move conversion rate?

Real ecommerce A/B tests put the lift in the high single digits to low twenties when measured against an incumbent search tool. [UNTUCKit](/customers/untuckit) ran a two-month split test against their prior search tool and recorded a 17.1 percent conversion rate lift (17.6 percent versus 15.0 percent), 21.3 percent higher revenue per searcher ($39.17 versus $32.30), and 3.3 percent higher AOV ($222 versus $215). After two months, UNTUCKit dropped the split and moved 100 percent of search traffic to the new engine. DTC fashion brand [Kilte](/customers/kilte) saw a 21.7 percent conversion rate lift on a similar test, this time against Shopify's default search - their brand-voice color names (Cappuccino, Ivory, Midnight, Écru) weren't matching the words shoppers actually typed (brown, white, black) until semantic matching closed that gap.

A few things to keep in mind when you read those numbers and try to model your own.

The lift concentrates on [long-tail and natural-language queries](https://nobi.ai/blog/handling-long-tail-queries). Head terms like "white shirt" or "running shoes" already work fine on most engines because the keyword overlap is dense. The wins show up on queries like "shirt for a beach wedding" or "soft cotton tee for hot weather" - the queries where shoppers describe a need rather than name a SKU. If your search logs are dominated by two-word brand-and-category queries, expect a smaller lift than UNTUCKit's.

Catalogs with editorial or brand-voice naming see the largest semantic wins. Kilte is the textbook case: when your color is "Cappuccino" and the shopper types "brown," keyword search returns zero. Semantic matching turns that zero-result page into a converting one. Fashion, beauty, and home goods catalogs tend to fit this profile.

Use conversion rate as the primary metric, but track revenue per searcher alongside it - revenue per searcher catches AOV effects that CVR alone misses, which is how UNTUCKit surfaced the $7 AOV bump. Run the test for at least four weeks to clear weekday and seasonality noise. Two weeks is too short to trust.

## How do you know if your store actually needs semantic search?

Three signals tell you it is worth the project: your catalog uses brand-voice, editorial, or evocative product names that do not match the words shoppers type; your zero-result rate in your search tool is above 5 percent; or search-led sessions are a meaningful share of revenue, typically 15 percent and up. Stores with small catalogs of obvious-name products often get less lift, because keyword matching already returns the right product and the math leans toward fixing other parts of the funnel first.

Start with the zero-result rate. Pull it from your current search tool's analytics dashboard - every modern search tool surfaces this metric. Above 5 percent means a meaningful slice of shoppers are typing things your index can't match, and most of those sessions end in a back button rather than a refined query. That number alone usually justifies the project for a catalog over a few hundred SKUs.

Then audit your top 100 search queries by hand against the catalog. For each query, ask whether there's a product that should obviously rank but doesn't. If 20 or 30 of the top 100 have a "right answer" your keyword search is missing, semantic matching will recover those sessions. This is the most concrete version of the diagnostic - a spreadsheet, not a vibe.

Compare [search-led conversion rate](https://nobi.ai/blog/increase-ecommerce-conversion-rate-search) against non-search-led. Shoppers who use the search bar typically convert at 2x to 5x the site average because they're high intent. If your search-led CVR is flat or only slightly above your sitewide number, the bar is leaking the intent. That gap is revenue you're already paying to acquire.

The clearest tell is naming convention. Brands using color names like Cappuccino, Midnight, or Écru get an outsized win from semantic normalization. [Kilte](/customers/kilte) is the canonical case: shoppers search "brown boots" and surface the Cappuccino Boot even though "brown" never appears in the product record. If your catalog reads like a poem, semantic search is the fix.

## What are the steps to implement AI semantic search on your store?

Five steps, in this order: clean and structure your catalog, pick a vendor, install the widget or snippets, configure your business rules, and A/B test for at least four weeks before flipping all traffic. The install itself is days for most vendors. The real time goes into catalog hygiene up front and the test window at the end.

Catalog cleanup comes first because the model embeds whatever text you give it. Wrong color attributes, missing material fields, or marketing-speak titles get encoded straight into the vector and produce confident-but-wrong results. Run a pass on your top 500 SKUs by revenue: confirm titles describe the product, structured attributes are populated, and descriptions read like product information rather than ad copy.

Pick the vendor with the second-question test. Open trials with two or three options, load a sample of your catalog, and run identical follow-up queries: "show me the same in navy", "under $80", "for cold weather". The vendor whose semantic layer holds context past the first turn is the one to take to pilot.

Install paths vary widely. [Algolia](https://www.algolia.com/) is API-first and assumes a few engineering days of frontend work to wire up a custom search experience. Other vendors ship as Shopify apps that install with a click plus some theme configuration. Nobi drops two JavaScript snippets into your theme, one site-wide and one on the search and assistant surfaces, which works on Shopify, BigCommerce, Magento, and Salesforce Commerce Cloud without an app listing.

Configuration stays your job. The model handles meaning; synonyms for your brand-specific words, redirects from common misspellings, and zero-result fallbacks all need rules. [Kilte](/customers/kilte) wired the same semantic layer into three surfaces (search bar, collection filters, product discovery), which is worth planning for upfront if you want one underlying index powering everywhere shoppers look.

Then A/B test for four weeks minimum. Shorter runs get fooled by a single weekend or holiday spike. Flip to 100 percent only when CVR and revenue per searcher both move in the new variant.

## How should you choose between AI semantic search vendors?

Compare four things vendor by vendor: how the embedding model handles your catalog's edge cases, how pricing scales with your traffic, time-to-first-install, and whether non-technical merchandisers can run it day to day without filing an engineering ticket. The only honest model-quality test is the identical-query test - run the same five or ten weird queries from your real search logs against trial accounts and compare the top results yourself. Vendor demo queries are cherry-picked; your queries are not.

Pricing models split the field. [Algolia](https://www.algolia.com) charges per query, which scales linearly with traffic and rewards engineering-led teams that want full API control over ranking and indexing. [Coveo](https://www.coveo.com) sits in the same lane on the higher-end enterprise side, with third-party reviews citing entry plans around $600/month and enterprise contracts north of $50k/year plus implementation fees. Pick either when you have a dedicated search engineering team and a budget for it.

[Constructor](https://constructor.io) runs on revenue-share, which can produce surprise costs as GMV grows but aligns the vendor with your conversion outcomes. It fits when behavioral personalization across category and browse pages matters as much as the search box itself. [Bloomreach](https://www.bloomreach.com) prices custom by catalog size and event volume; it's the right pick when search is one piece of a search-plus-CMS-plus-CDP consolidation, and overkill when you only need standalone search.

[Findify](https://findify.io) starts at $499/month on tiered subscription and is Shopify-focused. [Klevu](https://www.klevu.com) doesn't publish pricing publicly and is also Shopify-strong. One thing to know: Klevu and [Searchspring](https://searchspring.com) are both divisions of Athos Commerce now, so shortlisting both is picking between two products of the same parent. [Fast Simon](https://www.fastsimon.com) lives in the Shopify App Store tiered-pricing lane.

Nobi runs at $25/month base (2,500 searches and 250 messages included), then $0.01 per additional search and $0.10 per additional message - a brand running 50,000 searches and 5,000 messages a month lands around $995. [UNTUCKit](/customers/untuckit) ran Nobi against the paid incumbent they were already using and saw 17.4% CVR vs. 16.5%. The tradeoffs to know up front: web chat surface only (no SMS or WhatsApp) and a smaller integration marketplace than Algolia.

## How do you measure AI semantic search performance after launch?

Track four metrics weekly: search-led conversion rate, revenue per searcher, zero-result rate, and click-through rate on the first three results. Run a 50/50 A/B test against your previous search for at least four weeks before flipping to 100 percent of traffic, and review zero-result queries weekly to catch catalog gaps the model cannot fix on its own.

Search-led conversion rate is the headline number, not site-wide CVR. Site-wide CVR moves with paid traffic mix, homepage tests, and email campaigns; isolating shoppers who used search tells you whether the new model is actually doing its job. Pair it with revenue per searcher so you catch AOV and basket-size effects - a model that surfaces better product matches often lifts basket size without changing the conversion percentage at all. If conversion holds flat but revenue per searcher jumps 15 percent, the new search is working.

Zero-result rate is your health metric. Aim to push it under 2 percent within the first eight weeks. Anything higher means shoppers are typing queries the model cannot resolve, and most of those misses are catalog problems (missing synonyms, thin product descriptions, gaps in your assortment) rather than model failures. Top-3 click-through rate is the quality metric: it tells you whether the right product is landing in the first three slots. If CTR on the top three is below 40 percent, the ranking is putting the wrong items first even when the recall is decent.

Use the zero-result list as a weekly working document, not a dashboard you glance at. [UNTUCKit](/customers/untuckit) reviews their Nobi search insights in a standing weekly meeting where search trends, zero-result queries, and shopper intent patterns become input to merchandising and catalog decisions, not just a report the search owner reads alone. That meeting is what turns a search tool into a feedback loop on the catalog itself.

## Frequently asked questions about AI semantic search

Common buyer questions cover how semantic search handles existing merchandising rules, how long implementation actually takes, what pricing surprises to plan for, and whether semantic search replaces full site merchandising. Short answers below.

**Does semantic search replace merchandising rules?** No. The model handles relevance; you still need policy controls like pinning, boosts, and seasonal promotions. Most vendors expose both, so you keep your merchandising playbook and let semantics handle long-tail queries.

**How long does implementation take?** Install is a few days for app-based vendors and several weeks for enterprise platforms like Bloomreach or Coveo. Catalog cleanup and the A/B test add four to eight weeks before you flip 100 percent of traffic.

**What pricing surprises should I plan for?** Revenue-share models scale with GMV, usage-based models spike during traffic surges, and tiered apps jump fast at the next tier. Ask each vendor for a 12-month projection at your current and 2x volume so the next tier or overage doesn't blindside you.

**Does semantic search work on small catalogs?** Lift is smaller when shoppers can already find products by typing the exact name. Catalogs of 500+ SKUs with editorial naming (descriptive titles, attribute-rich descriptions) see the biggest wins.

**Can semantic search cover category and browse pages too?** Some vendors merchandise across the full site - Constructor and Bloomreach do this. Pure search vendors like Algolia, Klevu, and Nobi curate the search results page; pair them with a category merchandising tool if you need site-wide coverage.
