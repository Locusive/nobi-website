Buy the wrong one and you're still sitting with the same bottleneck six months later. If ticket volume is your problem, a helpdesk AI won't move that number. If agent resolution time is the issue, a storefront AI assistant doesn't help either. Here's how they actually differ and when each one is the right call.

- **Nobi** - conversational website assistant that answers shopper questions on-site before a ticket is ever filed; $25/mo base (2,500 searches + 250 messages included). Pick it when the goal is cutting inbound ticket volume by answering pre-purchase and post-purchase questions at the point of need.

Quote-only; platform fee plus usage on AI-resolved and routed ticket volume. Pick it when your helpdesk is staying put and the bottleneck is how long each ticket takes to sort and close.

Neither replaces the other - they work at different stages of the funnel.

## What do Nobi and Forethought each do?

[Forethought](https://forethought.ai) and Nobi solve different parts of the ecommerce support problem. Forethought works inside your helpdesk, processing tickets after they arrive. Nobi sits on your storefront, answering shopper questions before a ticket gets filed.

Forethought integrates with 70+ helpdesks - Zendesk, Salesforce, Intercom, Freshdesk, Gorgias among others - and layers on top rather than replacing them. It auto-resolves the tickets it can and helps agents close the rest faster through suggested replies, routing, and summarization.

Nobi is a conversational website assistant that deploys as a chat widget on your storefront. It answers sizing queries, WISMO lookups, return policy questions, and questions about discount policies and active promotions from your connected product pages and policy docs - with inline citations on every answer.

The two tools aren't substitutes. A brand on Gorgias could run Nobi to cut ticket volume and Forethought inside the helpdesk to speed up what still arrives.

## Where does each tool fit in the ecommerce support funnel?

That split - one tool on the website, one inside the helpdesk - becomes the clearest buying signal once you map where your biggest support cost actually sits. Sizing questions, return policy lookups, and WISMO requests are the highest-volume ticket category for most brands. Those questions can be answered on the product page or the order status page before a shopper ever opens the contact form. Agent hours spent sorting, routing, and writing replies to tickets that have already arrived is a separate problem - and it lives entirely inside the helpdesk.

Forethought works at the helpdesk layer. It doesn't touch the website or anything that happens before a ticket arrives; its work begins the moment a ticket lands in the queue. The Solve module handles autonomous resolution for tickets it can close without an agent. Triage routes the rest to the right team. Assist gives agents suggested replies, summaries, and context so they close what remains faster. Forethought fits teams that already run a helpdesk with real volume and want AI compressing how long each ticket takes.

Nobi sits earlier in the funnel, on your storefront where shoppers are still browsing. It answers questions from the sources you connect: product pages, return policy docs, FAQ routes, shipping information. A shopper asking about fabric content, return windows, or order status gets an answer immediately - with an inline citation showing exactly which page it came from. The ticket they would have filed doesn't get filed. That upstream impact is where Nobi has the most effect on support costs: it shrinks the volume arriving at your helpdesk rather than speeding up what's already there.

Running both in parallel is a valid architecture if your support cost falls in two places - high question volume on the site AND slow resolution time once tickets land.

## How do Nobi and Forethought handle AI accuracy and hallucination risk?

The funnel position each tool occupies shapes where the hallucination risk lands. Nobi's answers go directly to shoppers, which makes accuracy tooling the critical piece. Forethought's AI surfaces in the agent's inbox first - a suggested reply that contains an error reaches a human who can catch it before it goes out, not a shopper who may act on it immediately.

Every Nobi answer carries numbered citation pills. Hovering over a pill shows the source document name, date, and the exact excerpt the answer came from, so a shopper can check any claim against your official content without leaving the chat. For high-stakes questions - return policies, warranty terms - query overrides let merchants pin an exact approved response to specific prompts. No LLM paraphrasing, no variation; the override fires every time a shopper asks that question, and everything else routes through the standard grounded pipeline. Nobi also runs a second AI review on each draft answer before it sends: that second check reads the draft against the raw content from the cited sources and flags anything that doesn't hold up. It's toggleable per merchant and on by default for high-consideration industries. Connected knowledge sources refresh twice daily, so a pricing or policy change lands in customer answers within hours.

Forethought's AI works inside the agent's inbox. Suggested replies and ticket summaries appear to the human agent, who decides whether to send, edit, or discard before anything reaches the shopper. That human checkpoint is where Forethought's accuracy assurance sits - errors get caught before they go out, not after.

## How does pricing compare between Nobi and Forethought?

That advice to ask about platform independence before signing runs into a structural problem with Forethought: you can't know what you're signing until you're already in a sales conversation.

Nobi's pricing is public before you talk to anyone. The base plan is $25 per month, which includes 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages. Beyond that, additional searches run $0.01 each and additional messages cost $0.10 each. There's no revenue-share, no per-seat fees, and no tiered AI gating. You can build a budget model and run it by your finance team before you commit to anything - no discovery call required.

Forethought's pricing is quote-only. The structure is a platform fee plus committed usage on AI-resolved ticket volume and ticket volume routed to agents - Basic, Professional, and Enterprise tiers all require a sales call before you see a number. There's no public figure to anchor against before that process starts. The usage-based model could work in your favor if your AI resolution rate is high: costs scale with volume, so a tool that closes a large share of tickets on its own can justify the line item. The problem is you can't run that math until you're already mid-conversation with their sales team. Forethought's acquisition by Zendesk completed in March 2026, and before signing anything multi-year, it's worth asking explicitly whether pricing tiers, platform access for non-Zendesk customers, and the product roadmap are changing.

For a marketing manager who needs to justify the expense to a CFO before committing, Nobi is the only one of the two where that math can be done in advance.

## When is Forethought the better choice over Nobi?

The harder pricing conversation is worth having when Forethought addresses the problem you actually need to solve. Forethought is the stronger pick when your support bottleneck is inside the helpdesk, not upstream of it. If your team already runs Zendesk, Salesforce, Gorgias, or any of the 70+ platforms it connects to, and the real problem is how long each ticket takes to sort, route, and close - not how many tickets arrive - Forethought adds AI to that workflow without requiring a platform change, a migration, or retraining your team on a new tool.

Forethought works best when a human needs to review responses before they reach shoppers. The Assist module surfaces suggested replies and ticket summaries in what the agent sees, so a human can approve, edit, or discard before anything goes out. The Triage module handles sorting and routing automatically. If your team's bottleneck is that agents spend too long on each ticket rather than that too many arrive, that's exactly what Forethought is built to compress. Its 70+ helpdesk integrations mean it fits into what your team already uses without retooling.

Nobi has none of the helpdesk-side tooling Forethought offers. No ticket routing, no agent reply suggestions, no macro management. Brands whose primary need is structured workflow inside the helpdesk will find Nobi doesn't address that problem at all. Pick Forethought when agents spend too long on each ticket; skip it if the bigger problem is the volume of questions arriving before a ticket is ever filed.

## When is Nobi the better choice over Forethought?

If the bigger problem is the volume of questions arriving before a ticket is ever filed, Nobi is the better pick. Forethought compresses how long each ticket takes; Nobi works earlier - on the storefront, before a shopper opens the contact form. Sizing queries, return policy lookups, WISMO requests: Nobi answers those from your connected pages and docs the moment a shopper asks, regardless of which helpdesk you run.

Nobi also combines product search and automatic shopper Q&A in one session. A shopper asking whether you carry something in a specific variant gets a search result and a policy answer in the same chat without switching tools.

Every answer carries inline citation pills so shoppers can verify claims against your official content. For high-stakes questions like return policy or warranty terms, query overrides let merchants pin an exact approved response so the same answer fires every time with no LLM paraphrasing.

Nobi's honest limits: it doesn't manage tickets, route cases, or run macros. If your support model depends on a human joining an AI session mid-thread, Nobi doesn't do that. Brands whose primary need is helpdesk tooling should pair Nobi with their existing setup rather than replace it. But if the main cost is ticket volume from questions shoppers could get answered on your website - Forethought doesn't address that. It only makes each ticket faster once it has already arrived.
