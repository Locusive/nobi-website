## How do you make site search adapt to returning visitors without requiring them to log in every time?

A returning shopper already knows your store. They came back on purpose, which puts them closer to buying than any first-time visitor you'll see today. But most site search treats every session like a first visit - same blank search bar, same generic prompts, no memory of what they browsed or asked before. The parkas they spent 20 minutes on last Tuesday, the sizing question they put in chat, the category they kept circling back to: all of it resets when the browser closes. When they hit a zero-context experience on their third visit, they don't re-explain what they want. They leave.

## Why do returning shoppers keep starting from scratch every time they visit your site?

Session-based signals vanish when the browser closes, and because most shoppers don't log in, there's no account history for the search layer to draw from.

That gap hurts more than it might look. Returning visitors arrive with higher purchase intent than first-timers, but their browsing history, prior searches, and any chat they started are gone. A shopper on their fifth visit in two weeks still starts from zero.

On mobile it compounds. Short sessions spread across multiple days produce multiple zero-context experiences, making it harder for the shopper to pick up where they left off.

## What is Nobi, and how does it build memory that carries across returning visitor sessions?

Nobi is a conversational website assistant that closes that gap. It combines product search and automated shopper Q&A in one platform - and for returning visitors, it builds a persistent memory layer that carries forward across sessions without requiring a login.

The memory starts at first visit. Nobi assigns each visitor a unique identifier stored in their browser automatically - no account, no third-party tracker, no configuration required. As a shopper browses, Nobi records the pages they visit, products they view, queries they type, and topics they cover in chat. When they return, that context is already there.

Instead of a generic placeholder, the search bar reflects what this shopper has already shown interest in. Suggestion pills - LLM-generated prompts scoped to both the current page and the visitor's cross-session history - show different starting points than a first-timer on the same page would see. The assistant's opening message can acknowledge what they browsed or asked before, so re-entry feels like picking up a conversation rather than starting one.

Merchants can also pass context they already know. Before the chat starts, a call to `window.Nobi.addVisitorContext()` lets you supply a plain-English description of the visitor's situation - loyalty status, recent purchase category, anything relevant. Each entry is date-stamped and ages out after 12 months.

```js
window.Nobi.addVisitorContext()
```

Nobi's knowledge base - connected to your product pages, policy docs, FAQ routes, and PDFs - refreshes twice daily, so answers stay grounded in current inventory and pricing as the memory layer builds up.

## What does Nobi's visitor memory actually track, and how long does it persist?

The memory layer that drives those personalized placeholders and starter messages runs entirely in the visitor's browser - first-party storage, no login required, nothing shared with ad networks. Three signals feed it: product detail pages the visitor has viewed, search queries they typed into the Nobi assistant, and topics or questions from prior chat sessions. All three flow into the same store, which is why a product category a shopper browsed last week can shape the suggestion pills they see this visit.

That data sits in local browser storage, the same mechanism any site uses for a saved cart or a language preference. It persists as long as the browser retains that data - typically until the visitor clears their history or cache, or until the browser's own retention window expires. Clearing clears everything: there's no settings page to find, no account to delete, no brand to contact. That reset path is implicit and entirely visitor-controlled.

Nobi doesn't ask for an account or an email address for cross-session memory to work. It tracks what this browser has done, not who this person is. A shopper can return five times over two weeks, see a progressively more relevant experience each visit, and still never have shared their name or contact information with the brand.

## How do you turn a returning visitor's browsing history into more relevant search results and recommendations?

The cross-session memory layer shapes the search experience before a returning visitor types a single character.

The most visible change is placeholder text. A returning shopper who spent time on running shoes doesn't see "search our store" in the assistant input - they see a prompt that reflects what they've already browsed. Suggestion pills adjust the same way. A returning visitor on a product detail page sees different starting prompts than a first-timer on the same page, because the pills are generated from both the current page context and the visitor's prior sessions.

[Kilte](/customers/kilte) runs Nobi across three entry points at once - a search bar, collection filters, and product discovery. Because all three draw from the same persistent memory layer, a visitor who browsed one spot sees that context reflected across the others.

One honest scope note: what Nobi personalizes today is the entry experience - placeholders, pills, and opening messages. The search results list itself doesn't reorder based on individual click and purchase history. That's behavioral reranking, and it's where [Constructor](https://constructor.io) and [Klevu](https://www.klevu.com)'s Expert tier play. Both are built to shift result rankings based on what each shopper has clicked and bought.

## How do you personalize the chat and recommendation experience for shoppers who have visited before?

Inside the assistant, their prior context carries through the whole conversation - what Nobi surfaces in chat reflects what this shopper has already seen.

A returning visitor who asked about jacket sizing doesn't get a blank prompt. The assistant opens with starting points relevant to topics from earlier sessions, so a shopper picking up a research thread doesn't have to re-explain what they're looking for. Products the assistant recommends can draw on browsing from prior visits, not just what's happening in the current session.

For answers a returning shopper is likely to ask more than once - return policy, warranty terms, shipping cutoffs - query overrides lock an exact verbatim response. A visitor who asked about your return window last Tuesday gets the same merchant-approved answer today, so the wording of the answer stays consistent from visit to visit.

Inline citation pills let returning visitors verify what they half-remember. Hovering a pill shows the exact source document, date, and excerpt the answer drew from - useful when a shopper recalls a product detail from a prior session but wants to confirm it before buying.

All of this works without asking the visitor to log in. A returning shopper who browsed the same category three times and never created an account still gets the personalized experience - because the memory layer tracks what they did on your site, not who they are.

## How do you personalize the returning-visitor experience without making shoppers feel surveilled?

Fresh answers and cited sources build trust in what Nobi says - but shoppers also have to trust what it knows about them. The line between "this site remembers me helpfully" and "this site is watching me" comes down to what signals you use and how visibly you surface them. Nobi's memory runs on first-party browser signals from your own site only - no cross-site tracking, no demographic inferences, no off-site behavior. Personalization shows up as relevant prompts and contextual suggestions, not explicit "we saw you browsing X last week" messaging that can read as intrusive.

The data relationship is simple: it's between the shopper and your site. Nobi records what visitors actively did on your domain - queries they typed, pages they viewed, topics they covered in chat. Nothing comes from advertising networks or third-party data brokers. Because the personalization reflects choices the shopper made here, the experience reads as helpful context rather than profiling.

That setup fits inside your existing consent framework. Standard cookie consent already covers it - there's no separate consent flag to configure and no new legal category to sort out. If a visitor has already consented to functional cookies on your site, the memory layer runs within that consent.

## How do you measure whether returning-visitor personalization is actually moving conversion?

The primary metric is returning visitor CVR: the percentage of prior visitors who complete a purchase per session. Segment new vs. returning in Google Analytics and run a clean 30-60 day window before enabling cross-session personalization, then a matching window after.

Nobi's analytics dashboard surfaces what returning visitors searched, clicked, and bought through the assistant - covering search-to-cart rate and CVR from the personalized entry points. Two secondary signals are worth watching alongside those: zero-result rate for returning visitors (a drop means the personalized entry points are landing closer to intent) and repeat assistant engagement rate.

The business-level summary is revenue per visit for returning visitors. If personalization is working, returning visitor RPV should widen its gap above new visitor RPV over time.

Nobi's analytics covers search behavior and CVR from the assistant - it doesn't show where in the session visitors drop off or why they leave without buying. Brands that need abandonment-reason analysis as a primary lens will find [Dynamic Yield](https://www.dynamicyield.com)'s analytics more targeted for that specific job.

Returning visitor sessions also accumulate more slowly than new visitor sessions, which affects timing. Establish your 30-day baseline before making any changes, and run the measurement window long enough to reach statistical significance on that smaller population.
