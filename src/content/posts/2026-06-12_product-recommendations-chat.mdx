## How do AI product recommendations work in ecommerce search and chat?

You already have the product they want. Most of the time, that's not the problem. The problem is your store never surfaces it - a search returns close-but-wrong results, the chat doesn't connect the question to the right SKU, and the shopper leaves before you get a second chance. Once they're gone, they're usually gone for good. This is how to wire AI product recommendations into your search results and chat widget so the right product reaches the shopper at the moment they're ready to buy.

## Why do shoppers leave empty-handed even when the right product is already in your catalog?

Most product discovery failures happen quietly. A shopper types a query, gets results that don't match what they meant, and leaves - never surfacing an item that was a few clicks away. The gap isn't the catalog; it's the bridge between shopper intent and what the store surfaces at that moment.

[Zero-result and near-miss pages](/blog/fix-zero-result-searches) are where that gap shows up most clearly. A shopper who hits a dead end declared intent - they told you exactly what they wanted - and the store failed to answer. Those pages convert at a fraction of normal PLPs.

Vocabulary mismatch drives most dead ends. Shoppers describe products in plain language. Catalog titles use internal shorthand. Keyword search doesn't bridge that gap without synonym lists someone has to write and maintain.

The harder problem is the churn you can't measure. Most shoppers who hit near-miss results don't bounce to a trackable exit page - they just leave. A shopper who gets a bad recommendation rarely comes back for a second try. The stakes go beyond one lost sale.

The fix isn't more synonym groups. It's surfacing the right alternative or add-on the moment the primary query stalls.

## What is Nobi, and how does it generate product recommendations across search and chat?

Nobi closes that gap. It's an AI platform that combines site search, a shopping assistant, and product discovery in one system - and the recommendation logic runs from the same grounded catalog layer that powers every search result and every shopper question answer. That means every suggestion points to a real, currently available SKU, not a guess from a model trained weeks ago.

That shared foundation matters. Most storefront setups run search and recommendations as separate systems. Inventory changes in one place and the other lags. Nobi doesn't have that problem - there's one grounded index, so a restocked item or updated product description lands in both search results and recommendations within hours of the twice-daily refresh.

How recommendations surface depends on where the shopper is and what they're doing. On any page - homepage, PDP, collection - contextual suggestion pills appear as tappable buttons with prompts scoped to that specific page. A shopper on a boot PDP might see "Does this run wide?" or "Is this available in size 11?" Clicking one opens the shopping assistant with the answer already loaded.

When a recommendation involves a sizing, materials, or compatibility question, inline citation pills on every answer show exactly which source document and excerpt grounded the response. Shoppers can verify the claim without leaving the chat - which matters more when the question is "will this fit" or "what are these made of" than when it's a simple product lookup.

## Where in the shopper journey should AI product recommendations appear?

Those questions - fit, materials, compatibility - cluster at three high-value moments: when a search query returns weak results, when a shopper is on a PDP weighing a single item, and when they ask a natural-language question in chat. Each is a moment of declared or near-declared intent - the shopper is ready to buy if the right product surfaces.

The highest-conversion opportunity is a search page that returns nothing useful. A shopper who hits a dead end told you what they wanted and got a blank state back. Surfacing a relevant alternative there rescues a sale that would otherwise disappear from your analytics - zero-result rates can run as high as 30% across storefronts, so there's no shortage of those moments to recover.

A shopper on a PDP is close to buying. They've narrowed to one item. A "frequently paired with" or "complete the look" row at that point raises AOV without requiring a retargeting touchpoint to get there. The same logic holds on the search results page - a compatible accessory or size variant alongside primary results can raise basket size without a separate campaign.

In chat, when a shopper types "do you have this in a wide fit?" the right response closes the loop in one step: answer and the right SKU. That ends the interaction with a purchase path, not another click.

[Kilte](/customers/kilte) wired Nobi into three places at once - search bar, collection filters, and product discovery - so the same semantic layer compounds across the shopper's whole visit rather than firing at a single touchpoint.

## How does Nobi generate recommendations from shopper signals without manual pinning or synonym rules?

All of that context awareness - the page-specific prompts - runs on in-session signals, not a merchandiser's rulebook. Nobi reads the queries typed, the pages visited, and how long a shopper lingers on a PDP, then uses that context to decide which products surface next. There's no synonym list to build and no pin logic to maintain.

The grounding is the same semantic index that powers search. When a shopper describes a product in plain language, Nobi matches it to what fits the description - not just the literal words in the title. Session context and catalog work together: the index covers what's available, the session signals narrow to what's relevant to this particular shopper right now.

Suggestion pills work the same way. The ones generated for a running shoe PDP differ from those on the homepage - not because a merchandiser wrote separate rules for each, but because Nobi reads the product context on that specific page and generates from there.

The same logic handles cross-sells. Nobi surfaces complementary items and catalog relationships from the AI layer - you don't need a cross-sell matrix someone keeps up to date. When a shopper is on a boot PDP, relevant alternatives and pairings come from the catalog, not from a manually maintained "also recommended" list.

Nobi's personalization works within a session. It reads what's happening in the current visit - queries, page views, dwell time - not a shopper's full click and purchase history across past visits. [Constructor](https://constructor.io) is purpose-built for that kind of behavioral reranking, where results reorder based on what each shopper has bought and browsed historically. If cross-session behavioral history is the primary signal your team needs, Constructor is the right comparison to make.

## How do AI-generated recommendations surface inside the chat widget and on the search results page?

Those in-session signals flow into two places at once. In the chat widget, a recommendation is part of the answer, not a separate widget below the fold. When a shopper asks "what's the best option for wide feet?", Nobi returns a grounded response with citation pills showing the source document, date, and exact excerpt. The product surfaces inline with the text - the shopper can verify the claim by hovering the pill before clicking.

On the search results page, the same semantic index that powers the primary results also surfaces complementary and alternative items in the same pass. There's no separate "you may also like" request that can lag or return stale inventory. The complementary items come from the same lookup.

Contextual suggestion pills appear as tappable buttons scoped to wherever the shopper is. A visitor browsing a jacket might see "Is this machine washable?" or "Does this come in tall sizes?" Clicking opens the assistant with the answer already loaded - a starting point for visitors who don't know what to type and would otherwise leave without asking anything.

For high-stakes questions - return policy, warranty terms, specific SKU availability - query overrides let merchants pin a verbatim approved response. The override fires whenever the question matches, with no AI paraphrasing. Everything else routes through the standard grounded pipeline.

Because search and chat draw from the same index, a product recommendation in the assistant and a result on the search page stay consistent - a restocked item or updated price lands in both places at the next refresh, without a separate sync step.

## How do you keep AI recommendations grounded and accurate - not hallucinated or stale?

That shared index compounds across every touchpoint, which means accuracy matters across all of them. The two failure modes operators fear most are confident lies - the assistant recommends a product with wrong features or a price that doesn't exist - and hallucinated inventory, where the bot surfaces an out-of-stock SKU as available. Nobi addresses both through live catalog grounding and a second AI review that checks each draft answer against the source before it reaches the shopper.

The grounding starts with the catalog refresh. Nobi's connected knowledge sources update twice a day. A restocked SKU, a price change, or an updated product description lands in the recommendation layer within hours. The stale-training-data problem - where a model confidently answers from data that's months old - doesn't apply here.

Recommendations also draw from your connected catalog at inference time, not from a snapshot taken at setup. An item discontinued two days ago doesn't surface as a live recommendation. A product back in stock this morning is available to recommend today.

The second layer is the AI review pass. Every draft answer goes to a second model that receives the raw content from the cited sources and checks the draft against it before the answer sends. If something looks off - a price that doesn't match the source, a feature the page doesn't mention - that pass catches it. The review is toggleable per merchant and on by default for high-consideration categories.

Inline citation pills give shoppers the same visibility. Each recommendation links to the specific source document, date, and excerpt that grounded it. A shopper can verify any claim without leaving the chat; merchants can audit what grounded a given recommendation after the fact.

## How do you measure whether AI product recommendations are lifting attach rate and AOV?

The grounded index gives you something beyond accurate answers: a measurement trail from the first query to the completed cart. The metrics that matter are attach rate (how often a recommended product gets added alongside a primary item), AOV in sessions where a shopper engaged with a recommendation versus matched sessions that didn't, and revenue per visit across both search results and the shopping assistant. Nobi's analytics connect those numbers to what shoppers actually searched, clicked, and bought.

Start with attach rate. Segment it by where the recommendation appeared - shopping assistant versus the search results page. If attach rate is strong in one place and flat in the other, you know where recommendations are pulling their weight and where to look.

AOV lift is the number a head of ecommerce or CMO asks for first. Compare average basket size for sessions that included a recommendation interaction against matched sessions that didn't. That delta is the core business case for AI recommendations.

CVR goes one level deeper. Measure add-to-cart rate for sessions where a shopper clicked a suggestion pill against sessions that only used traditional search. The gap tells you what the conversational layer adds to CVR beyond the search baseline.

Revenue per visit pulls both effects together. A session with recommendation engagement drives more items and bigger baskets. Benchmark those sessions against baseline to show whether the investment is paying back against existing traffic.

What Nobi doesn't provide is drop-off-reason analysis - why specific shoppers abandoned mid-session in behavioral terms.
