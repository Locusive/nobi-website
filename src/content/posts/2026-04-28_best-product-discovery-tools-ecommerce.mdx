## What counts as a product discovery tool in ecommerce?

A [product discovery tool](https://nobi.ai/blog/ecommerce-product-discovery-beyond-filters) is anything that helps a shopper find the right product faster than scrolling a category page: search bars, on-page shopping assistants, recommendation modules, and answer engines that handle product questions before checkout. The category overlaps with customer support because the same shopper question - "does this fit a 14-inch laptop?" - shows up in both pre-purchase chat and post-purchase tickets. Discovery, in practice, is the moment a shopper turns intent into a specific SKU.

That's why this list mixes purpose-built ecommerce tools with broader support and answer platforms. Nobi sits in the ecommerce-native lane: site search, conversational shopping, and automatic shopper Q&A in one platform, starting at $25/month base. [Re:amaze](https://www.reamaze.com) and [Intercom](https://www.intercom.com) come from the customer-support side and land here because shopper questions are the friction point between intent and conversion. [Ada](https://www.ada.cx) brings autonomous AI agents across web, app, email, and messaging. [Yext](https://www.yext.com) covers search and answers for multi-location brands, not ecommerce specifically.

## How did we evaluate these product discovery tools?

We compared each tool on five things a head of ecommerce actually cares about: how well it handles on-site product discovery, what its pricing model looks like at real volume, how long implementation takes, how it sources answers (catalog data, knowledge base, external syncs), and how honestly it reports what it can't do.

Job fit was scored against three discovery jobs rather than a feature checklist: [search relevance on the catalog](https://nobi.ai/blog/ai-vs-traditional-site-search), conversational shopper Q&A on product pages, and answering post-purchase questions before they turn into support email. A tool that wins one of those jobs and loses the other two gets that limit named in its writeup. Re:amaze, for instance, is a real option for the customer-support side of the equation but does not run search or product discovery on a storefront; that gets called out, not glossed over.

Pricing was compared at the entry tier and at realistic volume, not at the lowest published headline. Yext, Intercom, and Ada all sit behind enterprise quotes or "contact us" gates, so volume math was modeled from publicly disclosed seat or usage rates where they exist and flagged as unconfirmed where they don't.

Implementation was measured in time-to-first-shopper-answer rather than feature count: how long from contract to a real shopper asking a real question and getting a real answer. Knowledge freshness was the last lens. A tool whose answers reflect a pricing or policy change within hours is built for ecommerce; a tool whose knowledge base refreshes weekly is built for a slower problem.

## 2. Yext

[Yext](https://yext.com) is a knowledge graph and answers platform. The product turns business information into structured entities (locations, services, people, FAQs, products) and serves answers from that graph across owned surfaces like a brand's site search and across third-party publishers like Google, Apple Maps, and Alexa. Yext came up in multi-location search - franchise networks, dealer locators, healthcare provider directories - and has expanded into broader brand-answers and AI-visibility use cases.

**Best for:** Brands whose discovery problem is brand-level answers, locations, or services rather than a product catalog.

**Pricing:** Custom; quoted per deployment scope and number of entities. No public starter tier; expect a sales cycle and an annual commitment.

**Pros:**
- Mature knowledge graph model that handles structured entities cleanly across many surfaces
- Strong fit for multi-location and franchise discovery, where one entity update needs to fan out to dozens of listings
- Established publisher and answer-surface ecosystem (Google, Apple Maps, Alexa, Facebook) it can syndicate to from a single source of truth

**Cons:**
- Custom pricing with no public floor makes the cost story hard to model before a sales cycle, and implementation runs as a multi-quarter project
- Catalog and merchandising primitives sit at the edges of the product, not the center; the platform is built around brand and location answers

**Verdict:** Pick Yext when knowledge-graph answers across many surfaces is the job; the strongest fits are multi-location brands, franchise networks, and any team whose discovery surface spans Google, Apple Maps, and Alexa as much as their own website.

## 3. Intercom

[Intercom](https://intercom.com) is a customer messaging platform with the Fin AI Agent layered on top of an established helpdesk. Fin learns from native Help Center articles plus external sources: public URLs (up to 10 sources, 3,000 pages each), PDF and DOCX uploads, and direct syncs from Notion, Confluence, Guru, Salesforce, and Zendesk.

**Best for:** Support teams already running Intercom for ticketing who want Fin to resolve a meaningful share of conversations autonomously.

**Pricing:** Seat-based Intercom subscription plus per-resolution Fin pricing - check Intercom's site for current numbers.

**Pros:**
- Fin sits inside an established support platform, so handoffs to human agents and ticket history are already wired up
- Wide range of knowledge sources, including direct syncs from Notion, Confluence, Guru, Salesforce, and Zendesk
- Native Help Center content updates instantly when an article changes
- Mature analytics and inbox tooling for support teams

**Cons:**
- External sources (public URLs and the Notion / Confluence / Guru / Zendesk syncs) refresh weekly, so an off-platform policy change can take up to a week to land in answers
- Fin runs on top of seat-based Intercom plans, not as a standalone product, so cost climbs with support team headcount
- Built for SaaS support broadly, not ecommerce specifically; many features are unused by an ecom buyer

**Verdict:** Pick Intercom + Fin if you've already standardized on Intercom for support; if you don't run Intercom today, deploying it just to get Fin is a heavy lift.

## 4. Ada

[Ada](https://ada.cx) is an AI customer service platform built around autonomous agents that handle support across web, app, email, and messaging from a single layer. The pitch is one AI agent fronting every channel a shopper might use, resolving issues end-to-end rather than routing them to a human queue. For a head of ecommerce running real volume across multiple touchpoints, the appeal is consolidation: one agent, one set of integrations, one place to tune answers and measure resolution rate. Ada has a long enterprise track record, and the product is designed for autonomous resolution rather than agent-assist suggestions.

**Best for:** Brands with multi-channel support volume that want a single autonomous AI agent resolving requests across web, app, email, and messaging.

**Pricing:** Custom; enterprise quote. Public pricing isn't published, and Ada's docs route you to a sales conversation for tier and volume specifics.

**Pros:**
- Cross-channel coverage from a single agent across web, app, email, and messaging surfaces
- Built around end-to-end autonomous resolution rather than agent-assist suggestions, so resolution rate is the headline metric
- Mature track record running at enterprise scale and volume

**Cons:**
- Implementation involves data integration and tuning, so plan for an enterprise rollout rather than a few-day install
- Less ecommerce-specific tooling than ecommerce-native helpdesks; on-site product discovery is not the core use case

**Verdict:** Pick Ada when cross-channel autonomous resolution is the priority and you have the team to drive an enterprise rollout.

## 5. Re:amaze

[Re:amaze](https://reamaze.com) is a customer support platform built specifically for ecommerce brands that live on Shopify, BigCommerce, or WooCommerce. The product centers on the support agent's day: shared inboxes, macros, live chat, and order context surfaced inside the ticket view so a rep can answer without flipping tabs. It is helpdesk software with native ecommerce integrations and predictable per-agent pricing, designed for teams whose primary friction is reps moving through a ticket queue with order data at hand.

**Best for:** Ecommerce teams whose primary gap is agent-side ticket workflow and order-aware support.

**Pricing:** From $29 per agent per month on the entry tier.

**Pros:**
- Native Shopify, BigCommerce, and WooCommerce integrations - order data, customer history, and storefront context show up inside the ticket
- Predictable per-agent pricing without a long enterprise sales cycle
- Solid agent-day tooling: shared inboxes, macros, live chat, and customer history in one place

**Cons:**
- Per-agent pricing scales linearly with support headcount, so cost climbs as the team grows
- Less AI-forward than newer entrants; the AI features sit alongside the helpdesk rather than driving it

**Verdict:** Pick Re:amaze when the gap is ticket workflow on a Shopify or BigCommerce store and the team wants ecommerce-aware helpdesk tooling at predictable per-seat pricing.

## How should a head of ecommerce pick between these tools?

Start with the job, not the demo.

If shoppers can't find the right product on your search results page, or bounce off PDPs with unanswered questions about fit, sizing, or returns, Nobi is the most direct fix: site search, [conversational discovery](https://nobi.ai/blog/best-ai-shopping-assistants-for-ecommerce), and automatic shopper Q&A on a $25/month base, with $0.01 per additional search and $0.10 per additional message above the included quota. Nobi isn't a ticket workflow platform, so pair it with a helpdesk if agent-side case routing matters.

If your team already lives inside Intercom's inbox, or autonomous resolution across web, app, email, and messaging is the gap, Intercom's Fin or Ada are better fits even at higher entry cost. Both quote enterprise pricing, so model real volume against per-resolution rates before signing.

If the bottleneck is ecommerce-native helpdesk workflow rather than discovery, Re:amaze handles tickets, macros, and live chat from $29 per agent per month. Yext is the right answer when "discovery" means brand-level or multi-location answers across Google, Alexa, and Apple Maps, not a product catalog.

Two checks before you sign. Compare pricing at realistic volume, not the entry tier; per-seat, per-resolution, and per-message models behave very differently at scale. And check knowledge-freshness cadence against your real change rate - weekly external syncs are fine for static docs and a problem for frequent pricing or policy changes.
