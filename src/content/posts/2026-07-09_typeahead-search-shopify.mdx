## How do you add typeahead search to a Shopify store?

You've watched it happen: a shopper taps a few letters into your search bar, nothing happens, and after a beat of silence they either keep typing and hope or just leave. Shopify's default search only responds once someone finishes typing and hits enter - there's no feedback while they're mid-query. That blank gap reads as broken, even on a store that works fine everywhere else, and it's costing you shoppers before they ever submit a search. Nobi is an AI-powered conversational website assistant, and it closes that gap with a dropdown that fills in as they type - products, collections, and content pages, updating on every keystroke, well before enter gets pressed. It installs in a Shopify theme with code pasted into the theme editor: a snippet that loads Nobi with your merchant ID, and a search bar component placed where you want the dropdown to appear. Worth knowing going in: this instant layer is separate from semantic search, which reads a shopper's intent once they submit a full query - that's covered further down.

## Why do shoppers abandon search before they finish typing on Shopify?

Shopify's default storefront search waits for a shopper to type a full query and press enter before it returns anything, and that blank-box gap is where search abandonment happens. A shopper who doesn't see anything responding to their first few keystrokes assumes the search box isn't going to help and leaves the query unfinished. There's no error message, no empty-results page, nothing to point to. The shopper just closes the tab or clicks back to Google.

That makes this failure almost impossible to see in a standard funnel report. A finished search that returns nothing at least shows up as a zero-result event you can count and fix. An abandoned search never gets that far: there's no submitted query, no page view, no add-to-cart, just a session that ends early with no obvious cause. This is the invisible churn problem. The shoppers a slow or blank search box turns off don't appear as a metric anywhere; they just stop converting.

Nobi is an AI-powered conversational website assistant that combines instant product search, [automated shopper Q&A](https://nobi.ai/blog/pdp-product-questions-shopify-plus), and lead capture in one product. Its typeahead layer renders a dropdown of matching products, collections, and content that updates on every keystroke, so a shopper sees results forming before they finish typing or hit enter.

The same component that powers typeahead also runs Nobi's [conversational assistant](https://nobi.ai/blog/adding-ai-chatbot-to-ecommerce-site) and its lead capture. That matters for a store's tech stack, not just for shoppers. A store isn't running one tool for instant search results, a second for a shopping assistant, and a third to capture high-intent visitors before they leave. It's one component reading the same catalog and content, doing all three jobs from a single install.

Behind the dropdown, Nobi's index refreshes against the live catalog twice a day. A product added this morning, a price change, a SKU that just sold out: those changes show up in the typeahead dropdown within hours, not after some overnight batch job or a manual reindex a merchandiser has to remember to trigger. For a store adding or updating products regularly, that's the difference between a dropdown that reflects what's actually in stock and one that's already stale by the time a shopper types.

## How do you install Nobi's typeahead search on Shopify?

Nobi's search component installs with code pasted into the Shopify theme editor, replacing or augmenting the default search bar without a developer sprint. Most Shopify stores are live with a working typeahead dropdown the same day they install it, and the twice-daily catalog refresh mentioned above starts working right after, with nothing to trigger by hand.

Installation is a quick theme drop-in, pasted directly into your theme editor:

1. Add this near the top of your theme's layout file, swapping in the merchant ID from the Installation page of your Nobi dashboard:

html
<script async src="https://assistant-script.nobi.ai/nobi.bundle.js" onload="window.Nobi.initialize({ accountId: 'YOUR_MERCHANT_ID' });">
</script>
2. Then place the search bar wherever you want the typeahead dropdown to appear:

html
<nobi-search-bar
  default-mode="ai"
  size="regular"
  show-mode-toggle="false"
  show-hint-row="false">
</nobi-search-bar>
Reload the storefront, type a few characters into the search bar, and confirm the dropdown starts returning matching products.

There's no separate indexing pipeline to stand up first. Once the theme edit is live, Nobi reads your existing product catalog automatically, no manual re-entry, no export/import step, no waiting on a batch job to finish before typeahead starts working.

[Algolia](https://algolia.com) is a search API built for engineering teams: typeahead there means writing code against the API, then building and maintaining the ranking and rendering logic yourself. That buys granular control, but it's a different path than Nobi's - it means an engineering sprint before a shopper sees a single result, not a same-day theme edit.

New products don't need a second install either. Add a product in Shopify and it shows up in the typeahead dropdown on the next catalog refresh, no code change, no redeploy, no ticket to a developer to re-index anything.

## What shows up in the dropdown as a shopper types?

As a shopper types, the dropdown fills with matching products, matching collections, and matching content pages like FAQ or policy pages, ranked by how closely they match the characters typed so far. Results update on every keystroke instead of waiting for a full page reload or a submitted query.

Products show up with a thumbnail and a price, so a shopper can see enough to act without leaving the dropdown. That's different from a results page that only shows product names. A shopper typing "canvas tote" can see three options and their prices before they've finished the word, and click straight into the one they want.

Collections surface too, when a partial query matches a category name. A shopper who types "wint" and means "winter" sees a Winter Collection link alongside individual products, which is useful for anyone browsing by category rather than hunting for one specific item.

Content pages join the same dropdown. If a shopper types "return" and your store has a returns policy page, that page can appear next to product results, giving them a shortcut to the answer instead of a list of items that don't match what they're actually asking.

All of this runs on a fast, literal keystroke match: it's comparing the characters typed so far against product titles, collection names, and page titles. It's not yet reading intent or meaning. A shopper who types "warm jacket for hiking" and means something more specific than the literal words gets handled by a different layer, the semantic matching that kicks in once they finish typing and submit the full query, covered next.

## Does Nobi's typeahead dropdown use semantic search?

No. The dropdown you just saw filling in with products, collections, and pages runs on a fast, literal keystroke match, not the semantic layer this guide covers next. Typeahead's job is speed: catch the characters a shopper has typed so far and show something responding before they decide the search box isn't going to help. Semantic matching, which reads intent and resolves vocabulary mismatch, only kicks in once a shopper finishes typing and submits a full query.

Keeping those two apart matters more than it sounds like it should. Typeahead has milliseconds to work with and no way to know what a shopper means yet, only what they've typed. If it tried to guess intent mid-keystroke, on three characters, it would be guessing wrong most of the time and showing shoppers results that don't match anything they're actually after. Its job is narrower and more reliable: match what's on the screen against product titles, collection names, and page titles, and update on every keystroke.

Semantic matching runs after that, once there's a complete query to interpret. A shopper who types "wint" and means "winter" is well served by typeahead, since a partial word against a collection name is exactly what a literal match is built to catch. A shopper who submits "waterproof jacket for hiking," when the catalog title says something like "trail shell," needs a different kind of matching. That's a full sentence expressing an intent, and resolving it against a catalog that uses different words is what the semantic layer is for, not typeahead.

Configuring or marketing typeahead as if it already solves vocabulary mismatch sets the wrong expectation. It's a separate capability, running at a separate point in the query lifecycle, and it fires only once a shopper submits.

## How do you measure the lift from adding typeahead search?

Nobi's search analytics track conversion rate, add-to-cart from search, and zero-result rate for a defined window before and after typeahead goes live, so the lift shows up as a specific, attributable number rather than a vague sense that search feels faster. [UNTUCKit](/customers/untuckit) isolated the typeahead dropdown from the rest of its search upgrade and ran it as its own test. Conversion rate rose 0.5 percentage points on an absolute basis from that component alone, separate from the broader semantic-search result covered earlier in this guide.

[Zero-result rate](https://nobi.ai/blog/fix-zero-result-searches) is worth watching alongside CVR, even though typeahead itself doesn't touch it directly. A drop in zero-result rate is a leading indicator: it tends to show up in revenue per visit a few weeks later, once enough shoppers have gone through a full session without hitting a dead end. If your zero-result rate is falling but revenue per visit hasn't moved yet, give it time before concluding the change didn't work.

The cleanest way to isolate typeahead's own effect, the way UNTUCKit did, is to compare CVR and add-to-cart-from-search rate for a matched window before and after rollout, ideally holding the rest of your search stack constant so you're not measuring two changes at once. That's harder to do cleanly if you roll out typeahead and semantic search in the same release. If you can stagger them, even by a couple of weeks, you get a clean read on the earlier layer's effect.

Query patterns shift with your catalog and with the season, so a one-time report after launch isn't enough. Reviewing search analytics in a standing weekly marketing meeting catches a real drop in zero-result rate before it's buried in a quarter's worth of noise, and it catches a regression fast if a catalog change or a new promotion breaks a query pattern that used to work fine.

## What are the limits of Nobi's typeahead search on Shopify?

Nobi's typeahead and search results live on the search bar and results page. It doesn't merchandise your category or collection pages the way a dedicated merchandising tool does, and personalization today is limited to placeholder text and starter messages rather than reordering results based on what a shopper has clicked or bought before. That second gap is worth naming plainly: [Klevu](https://klevu.com)'s Expert tier and [Constructor](https://constructor.io) both offer behavioral reranking, where results shift for a returning shopper based on their own click and purchase history. Nobi doesn't do that yet.

For most Shopify stores, that's not the bottleneck. The problem this guide has walked through, an abandoned search before the shopper even finishes typing, isn't a personalization problem or a category-page problem. It's a response-speed and match-quality problem, and typeahead plus semantic matching on submit closes that gap without needing a separate merchandising platform bolted on top.

Where it does matter is a store whose search is already fast and relevant, and whose remaining CVR bottleneck is elsewhere: a homepage that doesn't adapt to returning visitors, or collection pages that show the same grid to every shopper regardless of browsing history. That's a real, different problem, and it calls for a tool built around it. Klevu's Expert tier and Constructor both target that layer directly, with reranking and personalization as the core product rather than a secondary capability.
