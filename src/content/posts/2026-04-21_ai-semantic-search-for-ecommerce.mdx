## What is AI semantic search and how is it different from keyword search?

AI semantic search interprets the meaning behind a query instead of matching exact words. It converts products and queries into embeddings, numerical representations that capture meaning, so a shopper searching "brown boots" can surface a product literally named "Cappuccino Boot" because the model understands "brown" and "cappuccino" sit in similar semantic space. Traditional keyword search, the kind that ships by default with Elasticsearch or BM25 ranking, would return zero results on that query unless a merchandiser had hand-written the synonym.

That gap is not theoretical. Kilte, a DTC fashion brand, names colors in brand voice: Cappuccino, Écru, Midnight, Ivory. Shoppers type brown, off-white, black, white. [Nobi's color-normalization layer](https://nobi.ai/why-nobi/better-search) maps the editorial names to the words shoppers actually use, with no one writing synonym rules by hand. In an A/B test against Shopify's default search, Nobi lifted conversion rate by 21.7%.

Most production AI search systems are hybrid: semantic retrieval handles intent and vocabulary, keyword scoring holds onto exact matches like SKUs, and behavioral signals re-rank results based on what shoppers actually click and buy.

## Why should ecommerce teams prioritize AI semantic search now?

Search is one of the highest-intent surfaces on an ecommerce site. Shoppers who use internal search convert at roughly 2 to 3 times the rate of shoppers who only browse, which means the quality of the results ranking on your search page is doing more revenue work than almost any other template you own. Moving from keyword matching to AI semantic search goes after the three problems that quietly depress search CVR: zero-result queries, long-tail descriptive searches, and the "wrong product in position one" result that breaks trust on the very first tap.

The shift in shopper behavior is the forcing function. People now type full phrases like "waterproof hiking boots for wide feet" or "something like a linen shirt but warmer," and classic keyword engines struggle because there is no exact token to match. A semantic engine reads the intent, so the query resolves to relevant SKUs instead of a dead end. Zero-result rate is usually the fastest-moving KPI after a switch, because queries that previously returned nothing now find something useful.

The [public A/B data from Nobi customers](https://nobi.ai/customers) is worth taking seriously. UNTUCKit ran Nobi against their prior search tool for two months and saw conversion rate lift from 15.0% to 17.6%, a 17.1% relative gain, with revenue per searcher up 21.3% ($39.17 vs. $32.30) and AOV up 3.3% ($222 vs. $215). They ended the split and moved 100% of traffic to Nobi. Kilte, a DTC fashion brand whose color names ("Cappuccino," "Écru," "Midnight") weren't matching shoppers typing "brown," "white," or "black," saw a 21.7% CVR lift against Shopify's default search.

A caveat: these numbers come from brands with enough catalog depth and search volume for semantic matching to matter. If your catalog is small and your search traffic is thin, the lift ceiling is lower.

## What should you evaluate when choosing an AI semantic search platform?

Four factors separate the platforms worth shortlisting from the ones that look good in a demo and punish you six months in: engineering effort to install, how much manual merchandising the tool still expects, pricing predictability as traffic grows, and whether the relevance model actually improves from shopper behavior on its own. Different vendors make very different bets on each.

**Implementation timeline.** Shopify-native apps can be live in days. Klevu and Searchspring typically land in the weeks range once you factor in catalog sync and merchandising setup. Bloomreach, Coveo, and larger Constructor builds are multi-month projects, often with implementation fees and professional services attached. Coveo, for instance, is positioned as an enterprise platform and third-party coverage suggests annual licensing and implementation costs in the tens of thousands before you even weigh the monthly fee.

**Manual merchandising load.** Rule-based tools like Searchspring and Findify give merchandisers a lot of levers, which is great if you have a team to pull them and a problem if you don't. Behavior-learning platforms like Constructor, Algolia, and Nobi lean harder on automatic re-ranking from click, add-to-cart, and purchase signals, so the baseline ranking gets better without someone writing rules every week.

**Pricing model.** This is where buyers get surprised. Algolia publishes usage-based rates (for example, $0.50 per additional 1K search requests on Grow). Constructor uses revenue share, so costs scale with GMV. Bloomreach and Coveo are custom enterprise contracts. Klevu and Fast Simon sit in between, with session- or plan-based scaling and paid add-ons (Klevu's Smart Category Merchandising, for example, is a separate module). Nobi is flat-usage: $25/month covers 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages, then $0.01 per extra search and $0.10 per extra message, with no revenue share.

**Integration surface.** Confirm how the platform handles your PIM, variants, filters, and analytics stack before signing. And watch for personalization being sold as a separate module rather than included, which is common in the mid-market tier and quietly doubles the invoice.

## How do you implement AI semantic search on Shopify or a headless stack?

On Shopify, most AI search tools install as an app that syncs your product catalog, replaces the default search with a JavaScript widget, and takes over the search results page through their rendering layer. On a headless stack (Next.js, Hydrogen, or a custom frontend), you sync the catalog via API and call the vendor's search endpoint directly, rendering results in your own components. The heavy lifting in either path is catalog sync, filter mapping, and analytics instrumentation, not the search algorithm itself.

The sequence looks like this:

1. **Catalog sync.** Map product fields (title, description, variants, metafields, inventory state) to the provider's schema. Shopify apps like Klevu and Fast Simon handle this through the Shopify API and keep inventory in sync automatically. Headless teams write a sync job that pushes product updates to the vendor on webhook events.
2. **Install the widget or wire the API.** A Shopify app install is usually a day of work. A headless integration is typically 1 to 2 weeks with a frontend developer, depending on how custom your results page is.
3. **Configure facets.** Decide which attributes (size, color, price band, material) become filters. Get this wrong and shoppers will drop off even with great relevance.
4. **Synonyms and redirects.** Cover the handful of edge cases the model gets wrong. A good semantic model reduces the synonym list from thousands of entries to dozens.
5. **Analytics.** Wire search events (query, result click, add-to-cart, purchase) into GA4 or your warehouse so you can measure lift against the baseline.

Enterprise platforms like Bloomreach and Coveo follow a different shape: a services engagement, custom integration work, and typically 2 to 6 months before traffic cuts over. Algolia sits in between, faster to install but with more configuration on ranking rules once you scale.

Nobi's Hooks API, shaped by UNTUCKit's request for developer control over how products render, gives headless teams hook points without forking the widget.

## How do you measure whether AI semantic search is actually working?

Run a split test, not a before/after comparison. Seasonality, promotions, and paid campaign shifts will make a sequential test lie to you every time. Route 50% of search traffic to the new engine and 50% to the incumbent, let it run for 4–8 weeks, and compare the two cohorts head-to-head. A real win looks like a double-digit CVR lift that holds across product categories, not a blended average that's mostly one promo-driven SKU.

Your primary metric is search CVR: of the sessions that touch the search bar, what percentage place an order. That's the number the test lives or dies on. Track a handful of secondaries alongside it: revenue per searcher, AOV among searchers, zero-result rate, and search-to-PDP click-through. Revenue per searcher catches cases where CVR is flat but basket size moves. Zero-result rate tells you whether the new engine actually understands descriptive queries, which is the whole point of going semantic in the first place.

Run the test long enough to cover at least one full weekend/weekday cycle and ideally a paid campaign flight, since paid traffic skews query mix toward branded and category terms. Then segment the results by query type. One-word brand queries ("patagonia") behave nothing like natural-language queries ("waterproof jacket for hiking in the rain"), and a new engine can win on one while losing on the other. If a vendor shows you a single blended CVR number with no segmentation, push back.

For reference on what honest head-to-head numbers look like: UNTUCKit tested Nobi against the paid search tool they were already running (Findify) and saw 17.4% search CVR vs. 16.5% on the same traffic. Faherty ran Nobi against a different AI search tool and saw 12.3% vs. 10.1%. That's the level of specificity you should expect from any vendor pitching you lift numbers. If the deck says "up to 30%" with no split-test methodology behind it, assume the real number is closer to zero.

## What does AI semantic search actually cost?

Pricing ranges from about $25 a month for Shopify-scale flat-rate plans to mid-six-figure annual contracts for enterprise suites. The pricing model matters as much as the headline number. Flat or usage-based pricing is predictable: Algolia's published Grow plan runs $0.50 per additional 1,000 search requests, and [Nobi's flat-usage pricing](https://nobi.ai/pricing) starts at $25/month with 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages included, then $0.01 per additional search and $0.10 per additional message. Revenue-share pricing (Constructor) and session-tier pricing (Klevu) can scale faster than your budget as traffic grows.

Here's a rough map of where each platform lands:

- **Nobi**: $25/month base, $0.01 per additional search, $0.10 per additional message. No revenue share, no per-seat fees. A brand running 50,000 searches a month pays roughly $500, not a percentage of GMV.
- **Algolia**: usage-based. Free tier covers 10K requests/month; Grow adds $0.50 per 1,000 requests, Grow Plus $1.75 per 1,000. Improve is custom for high-volume brands.
- **Klevu**: session-tier pricing on Shopify, starting in the low-$50s/month and climbing with catalog size and session count. Smart Category Merchandising is a paid add-on.
- **Findify**: published plans at $499, $799, and $1,399 a month for Essential, Professional, and Enterprise.
- **Searchspring and Fast Simon**: custom quotes, typically mid three to low four figures monthly. Fast Simon's implementation fee depends on your stack.
- **Bloomreach, Coveo, Constructor**: enterprise contracts, often $100K+ a year. Bloomreach uses a module fee plus usage fee. Constructor layers a revenue share on top of the license.

Before signing anything, model the cost at 2x your current traffic. Percentage-of-revenue and session-tier deals look reasonable on a slow month and ugly on a good Q4. Honest caveat on Nobi: the $25 base is built for brands above roughly $1M GMV, and we have less name recognition than Algolia or Bloomreach. The trade is transparent math, no surprise revenue-share invoice in January.

## When is AI semantic search not the right investment, or when is a different tool the better call?

If your store does under ~$1M GMV or your catalog has fewer than a few hundred SKUs, the fixed cost of any AI search platform, Nobi included, is hard to justify. Default Shopify search is usually good enough at that scale, and the $25/month base on Nobi still turns into real money once you're paying $0.01 per search and $0.10 per conversational message at mid-market volume. Revisit the category when traffic gets heavier.

If you have a strong in-house engineering team that wants deep API control and is willing to do ongoing relevance tuning, Algolia is the better fit. Its API depth and customization are a genuine advantage over managed tools, and teams that like tuning their own ranking get more out of it.

If you're an enterprise retailer consolidating search, content, and CDP into one vendor, Bloomreach or Coveo are built for that scope. A focused search tool isn't going to replace a commerce experience cloud.

If your merchandising team wants to hand-curate rules for every category, Searchspring's rule-based UI is still the most mature in that lane.

Honest note on Nobi: we have less brand recognition than Algolia or Bloomreach, and a smaller third-party integration ecosystem. If you need dozens of prebuilt connectors out of the box, factor that in. Where Nobi does earn its keep is results like Lucchese's: $1M+ incremental revenue in year one and 39x ROI on Shopify Plus.
