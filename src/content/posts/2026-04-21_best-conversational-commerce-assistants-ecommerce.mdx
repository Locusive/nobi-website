## What is a conversational commerce assistant, and what problem does it solve for ecommerce teams?

A conversational commerce assistant is software that lets shoppers ask product questions, get guided recommendations, and complete purchases through a chat-style interface, on the site, inside messaging apps, or directly on the PDP. It sits between site search and live chat: more flexible than a search bar, more scalable than human reps. For ecommerce teams, the job it does is shortening the distance between a shopper's question and a confident add-to-cart.

The category splits cleanly from what came before it. Live chat is human-first and doesn't scale past your headcount. Old-school chatbots ran on scripted decision trees and broke the moment a shopper phrased something unexpectedly. Modern assistants are LLM-driven and catalog-aware, so they can handle open-ended product Q&A, guided discovery, size and fit questions, cart recovery, and post-purchase support without a rigid flow.

Why it matters now: paid traffic keeps getting more expensive, so every high-intent session has to convert harder. [Faherty's Shop with AI assistant](https://nobi.ai/customers) has posted a 6.3% conversion rate and a $315 AOV, well above typical site averages, which shows what a good assistant can do with the traffic you're already paying for.

## What should a head of ecommerce actually evaluate when picking a conversational commerce assistant?

Five criteria separate a tool that moves metrics from one that adds another dashboard to your Monday review: depth of product understanding, personalization that works on day one, predictable pricing, implementation speed, and vendor stability. Get any of these wrong and the assistant becomes shelfware your CX team apologizes for.

Start with catalog semantics. A shopper typing "something lightweight for travel" is not searching for the word "lightweight" in your product titles. They want the assistant to understand weight, packability, and use case, then return the right SKUs without your merchandising team writing 400 synonym rules. Ask any vendor to demo this on your actual catalog, not a sanitized sandbox. If they need a six-week tuning phase to handle natural language, that is the answer.

Personalization should be the same story. If standing up "right product for this visitor" requires a data engineer, a CDP integration, and a 90-day learning window, the tool is going to underperform for the first two quarters you own it. Look for assistants that begin personalizing from session signals out of the box and improve as behavioral data accumulates.

Pricing is where buyers get burned. Revenue-share models look cheap until you have a strong quarter, and opaque usage tiers make it impossible to forecast. A flat base with transparent per-unit overages, for instance [Nobi's $25 base covering 2,500 searches and 250 messages](https://nobi.ai/pricing), then $0.01 per additional search and $0.10 per additional message, lets you model costs against traffic. A mid-market brand running 50,000 searches and 5,000 messages a month should run that math before signing anything.

Finally, weigh implementation time and vendor risk. Days-to-launch beats months-to-launch when your roadmap is full. And an acquired or pivoting vendor can freeze your roadmap for a year while their product team reorganizes around the new owner's priorities.

## Which conversational commerce assistant fits a brand that needs search and conversation in one platform?

Nobi is built for brands where the shopping assistant can't live in a silo. Shoppers start in search, drop into conversation when they want a recommendation, and need the same catalog understanding across both surfaces. That's the core bet: one platform that indexes your products once, then serves that intelligence through a search bar, a conversational assistant, and [lead capture forms](https://nobi.ai/lead-capture) without three separate integrations and three separate data models.

Pricing is straightforward. $25/month covers 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages, with $0.01 per additional search and $0.10 per additional message after that. For a mid-market brand running 50,000 searches and 5,000 messages a month, the math works out to roughly $975 plus the base fee, which is predictable enough to forecast against traffic growth. No revenue share. No per-seat fees. No tier gates on the AI features.

The customer results back up the positioning. Faherty's Shop with AI deployment on Nobi converts at 6.3% with a $315 AOV, which is meaningful lift when stacked against typical ecommerce site averages. Lucchese, a luxury Western boot brand on Shopify Plus, attributes $1M+ in incremental first-year revenue to Nobi at a 39x ROI, with $3.46M in cumulative attributed revenue across search plus a cart assistant and PDP assistant. The two assistant surfaces drove incremental checkouts that search alone would have missed.

Personalization runs out of the box. Results and recommendations adjust based on shopper behavior, so merch teams aren't writing rules every time a new collection drops. For teams that need more control over presentation, the [Hooks API](https://nobi.ai/custom-actions), shaped by UNTUCKit's requests and now available to every Nobi customer, lets developers customize how products get rendered and ranked without leaving the platform.

Honest caveat: if you're under roughly $1M GMV, Nobi is probably overkill. The volume economics don't pencil out until search and conversation are doing real work.

## Which conversational commerce assistants are built specifically for the sales moment on Shopify?

Alby, Rep AI, and Octane AI all focus on converting browsers into buyers, but they each target a different sales moment. Alby answers product-specific questions at the PDP for Shopify brands. Rep AI proactively engages hesitant browsers with behavioral triggers meant to lift CVR on high-intent sessions. Octane AI anchors discovery in guided quizzes, which tends to work especially well in beauty and CPG. If you know exactly where your drop-off is, one of these will likely fit better than a broader platform.

**Alby** is the right pick if your problem is specifically product-question abandonment on the PDP. Shoppers ask "will this fit a queen bed?" or "is this cruelty-free?" and leave when no one answers. Alby fine-tunes a chat agent on your catalog and content so those answers show up inline. The caveat worth knowing: Bluecore acquired Alby in November 2024, and the post-acquisition roadmap and pricing haven't been clearly communicated since. It's also chatbot-only, so if you also want to fix site search, it won't get you there.

**Rep AI** positions itself as a proactive sales rep that watches behavior and engages shoppers at moments of hesitation, like long dwell time on a PDP or a filled cart that's stalled. If you believe your issue is that browsers need a nudge, not an answer, Rep AI is built for that specific job. The trade-off: it's a sales chatbot, not a discovery or search layer, and personalization depth is shallower than what unified platforms do.

**Octane AI** is the category leader for product quizzes on Shopify, with plans starting at $50/month. Beauty, skincare, and CPG brands use it because "help me find the right routine" translates cleanly into a quiz. Outside those verticals, the quiz-first shape is less flexible for open-ended conversation.

All three are Shopify-native, which keeps install work light if that's your stack.

## What if customer support, not sales, is your real bottleneck?

If most of your conversational volume is order status, returns, and "where's my refund," a support-first platform will outperform a discovery-first one every time. Gorgias is the strongest pick when you're ready to commit to a full ecommerce helpdesk with AI-assisted responses. Tidio is the lighter alternative for smaller merchants who want live chat plus simple bots without taking on a full ticketing system.

Gorgias is the right call over Nobi when ticketing is the job. The helpdesk workflows, the macros, the omnichannel inbox, the agent assignment rules, are the product, not a side feature bolted onto a chat widget. Plans start at $10/month for 50 tickets on Starter, then step up to $50/month (300 tickets), $300/month (2,000 tickets), and $750/month (5,000 tickets), per Gorgias's published pricing. AI Agent resolutions are billed separately at $0.90 per resolved conversation. Realistically, you're looking at $60+ per month before AI automation does meaningful work for you, and even then Gorgias is solving support volume, not product discovery. Shoppers who want help finding the right SKU still need a different tool.

Tidio sits at the other end. Its Lyro AI agent runs on a per-conversation model (around $0.50 per conversation, with 50 free to start), and it's built around small to medium Shopify merchants who need a chat widget plus light automation without a helpdesk commitment. Setup is fast. The tradeoff is depth: Lyro answers questions from your support content well, but it isn't doing semantic product understanding the way a real discovery tool does, so it won't replace Nobi or Algolia at any meaningful catalog size.

Pick by where the volume actually lives. Tickets go to Gorgias. Product questions stay with a discovery-first assistant.

## When is a competitor the better pick over Nobi?

Nobi is not the right fit for every brand. If you're under roughly $1M GMV, the search and personalization layer is over-scoped for your volume. Tidio's free tier, which includes 50 Lyro conversations at no cost, will carry you until traffic justifies a paid discovery layer. At that scale, the $25/month Nobi base plus per-search and per-message overages isn't expensive, it's just more machinery than a sub-$1M store needs to run.

If your bottleneck is ticketing and omnichannel support depth, Gorgias is the better buy. Their workflow is built around meaningful shopper conversations with unlimited users, and the ticket-based plans scale from Starter at $10/month through Advanced at $750/month, with AI Agent resolutions priced at $0.90 per resolved conversation. A support org that lives in a shared inbox and needs macros, SLAs, and multi-channel routing will get more out of Gorgias than it will out of a discovery-first assistant like Nobi. The flip side, which third-party reviewers have flagged, is double-charging for AI on top of ticket fees, so model the cost at your actual volume.

If your go-to-market is quiz-led merchandising, especially in beauty and CPG, Octane AI's quiz builder is more specialized than anything Nobi does natively. Their Basic plan starts at $50/month for 400 credits, and the product finder, routine builder, and personality quiz templates plug into Klaviyo for segmented follow-up. That's a purpose-built flow Nobi doesn't try to replicate.

One last honest note: Nobi has less name recognition than Algolia or Bloomreach. If your procurement process treats analyst coverage as a gate, that's a real consideration, not a hand-wave. Run the pilot anyway and let the numbers argue.

## How should a mid-market ecommerce team budget and roll out a conversational commerce assistant?

Budget for two cost layers: platform pricing and implementation time. Platform fees range from free entry tiers (Tidio) and $25/month (Nobi, which includes 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages, then $0.01 per additional search and $0.10 per additional message) up to custom enterprise quotes for Alby post-Bluecore acquisition. Watch for revenue-share clauses and tier jumps that can swing annual spend 3-5x once traffic grows. Do the per-unit math before signing: a mid-market brand running 50,000 searches and 10,000 conversational messages a month pays far more than the $25 base, but it is still forecastable line by line.

Implementation is the bigger hidden cost. Enterprise search platforms can absorb months of engineering work on indexing, relevance tuning, and frontend integration. Purpose-built assistants typically deploy in days.

Start narrow. Pick one surface, search, PDP, or cart, and one success metric, CVR or revenue per session. Run a 60-day pilot against a holdout group so you can attribute lift cleanly. Lucchese took this shape: search first, then a cart assistant and a PDP assistant layered on, reaching $1M+ in incremental revenue in year one and $3.46M cumulative.

One more line item: vendor stability. Acquisitions like Alby into Bluecore can stall roadmaps for quarters, so factor re-platforming risk into any multi-year contract.
