# Nobi vs Klevu: Which Site Search and Shopping Assistant Should You Choose?

## What Nobi and Klevu Each Do

Both tools sit in the same product discovery category, but their approaches could not be more different. Klevu is built around keyword matching and structured catalog logic, which works well until your shoppers start typing the way they actually think. Nobi starts from intent, not vocabulary. That gap matters more than most teams realize, especially when a significant chunk of your highest-value queries return zero results or the wrong ones.

**Klevu** is built for merchandising teams that want hands-on control. It gives you a dashboard for campaign testing and scheduling, supports Shopify Markets and Hydrogen storefronts, and exposes developer APIs and SDKs for teams that need custom configuration. Klevu holds a 4.8-star rating across 13 reviews on G2, with most praise pointing to its merchandising controls and flexibility. If your team has a dedicated search specialist, that control surface is genuinely useful.

**Nobi** combines AI site search with a conversational shopping assistant. The design assumption is different: instead of giving operators levers to pull, Nobi learns from shopper behavior and adjusts on its own. Brands like UNTUCKit and Lucchese use it to handle search and product discovery without building an internal merchandising function around it.

The practical difference comes down to your team's makeup. A larger brand with someone dedicated to search configuration will find Klevu's toolset worth the investment. A mid-market operator running a lean team, without time to actively manage search campaigns, will find Nobi's self-tuning approach more realistic to maintain. Neither is wrong. They're built for different operating models.

## Search Intelligence: Behavior-Learning AI vs Manual Rule Configuration

Vague queries drive some of the highest purchase intent in ecommerce. A shopper typing "gift for a 40-year-old runner" or "shoes for wide feet that look dressy" is close to buying, but their search doesn't map cleanly to product attributes. How a search tool handles that gap separates the two platforms clearly.

**Klevu** processes queries by matching keyword patterns. It has no native generative AI search capability, which means it can't interpret what a shopper actually means, only what they literally typed. When the words don't align with your catalog's indexed terms, results suffer. G2 and Capterra reviewers note that Klevu's personalization feels rule-based in practice: relevance boosting, manual synonym rules, and pinned results require your team to configure them upfront and keep them updated as your catalog evolves.

That maintenance burden compounds over time. Every new product line, seasonal push, or catalog restructure can degrade result quality unless someone actively re-tunes the rules. For brands without a dedicated search analyst, that's a problem. The tool stays accurate only as long as someone is actively managing it.

**Nobi** takes a different approach. Rather than asking your team to define relevance manually, it learns from shopper behavior: which results get clicked, which items get added to cart after a specific query, and where shoppers hit dead ends with zero results. Those signals feed back into ranking automatically. No rules to write. No synonyms to maintain.

The practical difference shows up on the searches that matter most. Long-tail and conversational queries, the ones that indicate real purchase intent but resist keyword matching, are where Nobi's behavior-learning model has an edge. Brands like UNTUCKit have seen this play out in measurable conversion lift, not because the rules were configured correctly, but because the system adapted to how their shoppers actually search.

For operators who can't staff a search program, that distinction is the whole ballgame.

## Implementation Time: Days vs Months

Getting to go-live is a financial decision, not just a technical one. Every week your search is broken or mediocre is a week customers leave empty-handed.

**Klevu's setup depends heavily on developer support.** According to G2 reviews, users consistently flag manual configurations as a bottleneck, often requiring engineering time to get relevance logic and scoring models working correctly. For a lean team without dedicated dev resources, that timeline stretches. You're paying a monthly subscription while your engineers are still building the thing.

**Nobi is designed so a non-technical team member can complete setup.** There's no custom relevance logic to write from scratch, no scoring models to calibrate before the first search query runs. Most merchants are live within days. That's not a marketing claim, it's a structural difference in how the product was built.

For a direct-to-consumer brand doing $5M to $50M in revenue, two extra months on implementation isn't a scheduling inconvenience. It's two months of continued revenue loss from poor search results, dead-end queries, and shoppers who give up.

There's also a support problem worth naming. G2 reviewers report that Klevu's support team closes tickets before issues are fully resolved. That's a compounding risk during onboarding, when configuration questions are most frequent and most consequential.

Before committing to either platform, ask two specific questions: What is the realistic go-live timeline against your actual tech stack, whether that's Shopify, BigCommerce, or headless? And how does support handle a failed catalog sync or a theme conflict on day one?

The answers will tell you more than any demo.

## Personalization, Conversational Search, and Lead Capture

Klevu's merchandising dashboard gives teams fine-grained control over product rankings and campaign scheduling. That control comes with a cost: G2 reviewers from merchandising and marketing teams consistently flag the UI as complicated to navigate day-to-day, which means the tool meant to save time often creates its own administrative overhead.

Nobi takes a different approach to personalization. It reads behavioral signals from each visit, surfacing different products for different shoppers from the moment the search bar loads. There is no segment-building phase, no configuration window before it starts adapting. A returning customer who has browsed outerwear gets a different result set than a first-time visitor searching the same term.

The bigger gap shows up when shoppers use natural language. Type "what jacket works for a spring wedding" into a Klevu-powered search bar and you get keyword-matched results, because Klevu does not include a native conversational shopping assistant. The query gets parsed for words, not for intent. Nobi's assistant interprets what the shopper is actually trying to accomplish and guides them toward the right product, which reduces bounce rates on ambiguous searches. Faherty saw this difference play out in the numbers: after switching, they achieved a 12.3% search conversion rate with Nobi, compared to 10.1% with a prior AI search tool.

Beyond search results, Nobi extends what the assistant can do during the shopping experience. Operators can collect emails, trigger promotions, or route shoppers to specific landing pages, all within the search interaction itself. Klevu does not offer this functionality. For brands that run email acquisition campaigns or want to capture intent signals at the moment a shopper is most engaged, that gap matters.

The practical difference is this: Klevu gives merchandisers a control panel. Nobi gives shoppers a guide, and gives operators a way to act on that interaction while it is happening.

## Pricing: Transparent Model vs Premium Complexity

Klevu sits at a premium price point, and multiple operators report that costs climb unexpectedly as traffic and catalog size grow. That unpredictability is an operational problem, not just a budget annoyance. If a promotional spike doubles your search bill mid-season, you are now evaluating a vendor contract during your busiest period instead of focusing on sales.

Nobi targets mid-market operators, roughly $1M to $50M GMV, with pricing designed to stay consistent as the store grows. The number you agree to at signup should reflect what you actually pay as query volume increases, without surprise tier jumps.

Before signing with either tool, ask the vendor one specific question in writing: what triggers a price increase? Catalog size, monthly query volume, API call counts, and assistant conversation volume may each be metered separately. If the answer is vague or requires a follow-up call with a sales engineer, treat that as a signal.

Klevu's premium structure suits large enterprise teams with dedicated procurement resources. Competitors like AddSearch have positioned themselves explicitly on price as a contrast to Klevu's cost model, which suggests the gap is wide enough that buyers notice it consistently.

For a mid-market operator without a multi-month negotiation runway, pricing clarity is not a nice-to-have. It directly affects whether you can plan a fiscal year with confidence.

## Where Each Tool Has Real Limits

No tool is the right fit for every store, and both Nobi and Klevu have real gaps worth naming.

**Nobi works best for mid-market stores**, generally above $1M GMV. Below that threshold, the pricing and feature depth may be more than your operation currently needs. A lighter tool may serve you better at that stage. Also worth confirming before you commit: Nobi's third-party integration ecosystem is smaller than Algolia's or Bloomreach's. If your tech stack has a specific dependency, check compatibility directly rather than assuming it's covered. Nobi is also a newer brand with less visibility in analyst reports and enterprise RFPs. If vendor longevity or third-party analyst coverage matters to your procurement process, ask Nobi directly about roadmap and funding.

**Klevu's clearest strength** is its merchandising dashboard. Teams with a dedicated merchandiser who needs daily control over search rankings, pinned products, and campaign scheduling will find that Klevu delivers exactly that. If that level of manual control is a non-negotiable part of your workflow, it's a genuine differentiator.

The honest evaluation criteria aren't feature lists. They're team size, technical resources, catalog complexity, and how much ongoing search maintenance your team has the bandwidth to own.
