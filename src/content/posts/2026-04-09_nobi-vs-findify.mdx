# Nobi vs Findify: A Direct Comparison for Ecommerce Site Search

## What Nobi and Findify Actually Do

Shopify's native search just isn't built for large catalogs. That's the problem both tools are trying to solve. Enterprise platforms like Algolia or Bloomreach have the power, but most mid-market operators don't have the budget or the engineering team to run them.

**Findify** is a search and discovery platform built primarily for Shopify. It uses behavioral signals, including clicks, purchases, and dwell time, to rerank results and personalize product listings. Its core products cover smart search, recommendations, and category merchandising. The personalization layer is Findify's main differentiator: the platform learns from shopper behavior and adjusts what surfaces first.

**Nobi** combines AI site search with a conversational shopping assistant. A shopper can type "gifts for a runner under $75" and get filtered, relevant results rather than a keyword-match dump. Nobi interprets intent before matching products to your catalog.

That structural difference matters most when a visitor searches for something that does not map cleanly to a product title or tag. Findify personalizes within a keyword-search model, so if the keyword match fails, the personalization layer has nothing useful to work with. Nobi handles ambiguous queries differently: it reads the query as a request, not a lookup.

Both platforms target the same buyer. The choice comes down to which search model fits your shoppers.

## AI Search and Personalization: How the Two Approaches Differ

Both tools call themselves AI-powered, but they use different mechanisms, and the distinction matters depending on who is actually landing on your store.

**Findify's personalization engine** reranks search results based on each shopper's behavioral history. If someone has clicked on blue sneakers before, Findify surfaces blue sneakers higher the next time they search. That works reasonably well for returning visitors, but first-time visitors have no behavioral history to draw on. For most ecommerce stores, new visitors represent a significant share of daily traffic, which means a large chunk of searches get little benefit from behavioral reranking.

**Nobi uses large language model inference** to parse the intent behind a query, not just its words. A search like "slim-fit pants for a tall guy" returns results filtered by fit and inseam length, not a broad dump of every product containing the words "slim," "fit," "pants," and "tall." Because this approach works from meaning rather than history, it performs on first-session queries just as well as on returning ones. That matters in practice.

Nobi also includes a **conversational shopping assistant**. A shopper can type "do any of these come in navy?" as a follow-up and get context-aware results instead of a fresh, context-free search. Findify does not offer a comparable back-and-forth interface.

The impact of intent-based search over behavioral reranking shows up in the numbers. Faherty measured a **12.3% search conversion rate** with Nobi, compared to 10.1% from the AI search tool it previously used. That 2.2 percentage-point improvement came specifically from switching to an approach that reads query intent rather than relying on prior click data.

For stores with high new-visitor traffic or complex, descriptive search queries, that difference is material.

## Implementation Time and Technical Requirements

Getting Findify live is not a plug-and-play process. The platform requires developer involvement to configure relevance ranking, build out merchandising rules, and customize the search UI to match your storefront. For stores without an in-house engineering team, that means either hiring a Shopify developer or carving out sprint time. Realistically, implementations run several weeks to a couple of months depending on how much customization you need.

**Nobi is designed to go live in days.** The onboarding process is guided, and the AI handles relevance out of the box, which removes the manual ranking configuration step that consumes most of the time in a Findify deployment. You don't need a developer to get up and running.

That gap matters most if you're in a seasonal business. If it's October and you need better search before the holiday rush, a multi-week implementation schedule isn't just inconvenient, it's a real operational constraint. Missing a peak season because setup ran long is a concrete cost.

Both platforms support **Shopify**. Nobi also integrates with other major ecommerce platforms, which matters if you're running a multi-storefront setup or planning a platform migration. Before signing with either vendor, confirm native support for your specific platform version and any third-party tools in your stack: your review platform, loyalty program, inventory system. Post-contract integration surprises are common and expensive to fix.

The short version: if you have developer resources and time to configure, Findify gives you a lot of control. If you need speed, or you're running lean, Nobi's faster path to production is a real operational advantage.

## Pricing and What It Costs at Scale

Findify does not publish pricing on its website. That alone should prompt a question: why not? Vendors that withhold public pricing almost always negotiate rates based on GMV or order volume. Your bill grows as your store grows, regardless of how much additional value search is actually generating for you. You pay more because your business is succeeding, not because the tool is doing more.

Nobi uses flat, transparent pricing with no revenue-share component and no usage-based overages. You can model your annual cost before signing anything, and that number does not automatically increase as your GMV scales.

The math matters. For a store doing $5M in annual revenue, a 0.5% GMV-based fee adds up to $25,000 per year. At $10M, that doubles to $50,000. The gap between a flat fee and a percentage-of-revenue model widens significantly over a multi-year contract. If you are evaluating Findify, ask them for a cost projection at your expected GMV growth rate over three years, not just what you will pay in month one.

Total cost of ownership also includes implementation. Developer time costs money, whether that means agency fees or internal engineering hours. Findify's setup typically requires more technical involvement upfront and ongoing maintenance as your catalog or theme changes. Nobi's lighter implementation footprint means fewer billable hours in year one, which can partially or fully offset any price difference between the two platforms.

The comparison is not just sticker price versus sticker price. It is full-year cost, including what you pay when search is working exactly as intended.

## Real Results from Stores That Have Used Both

UNTUCKit ran Findify before switching to Nobi. After the switch, its search conversion rate moved from 15.0% to 17.4%. A 2.4 percentage-point lift sounds modest, but on a high-traffic store it compounds across thousands of sessions every month. That additional revenue comes without spending more on paid acquisition.

Lucchese, the western boots and leather goods brand, deployed Nobi and generated more than $1M in incremental revenue during its first year on the platform. The reported ROI was 39x, meaning every $1 spent returned $39. Results at that ratio are rare enough that they deserve scrutiny, but the figure is what Lucchese reported after a full year of measurement, not a short promotional window.

Faherty, a lifestyle apparel brand, measured a **12.3% search conversion rate** with Nobi, compared to **10.1%** with the AI search tool it had been using before. That 2.2 percentage-point difference held across multiple measurement periods. A result that only appears in a single A/B test window could be noise. One that repeats is harder to dismiss.

Three data points are not a broad sample. All three brands sit in the mid-market fashion and lifestyle segment. If you run a home goods store, a sporting equipment shop, or an electronics retailer, these numbers tell you something directionally useful, but they are not a direct prediction of what you will see.

Before committing to either platform, ask both vendors for case studies from stores in your specific category and at your traffic volume. Ask how conversion was measured, over what period, and whether the test controlled for seasonal variation. Any vendor worth working with should be able to answer those questions without hesitation.

## Where Findify May Be the Better Fit

Findify has been in market longer than Nobi and carries a larger publicly visible customer base. For procurement teams that weight vendor stability and reference customers heavily, that track record is a legitimate factor, not just a preference.

Migration cost is the other honest consideration. If your team has spent months building out Findify's merchandising rules, synonym libraries, and category boosts, that configuration has real value. It does not transfer automatically. Budget four to eight weeks of reduced optimization while you rebuild those rules on any new platform, and factor that into the ROI calculation before you switch purely on conversion potential.

Catalog scale matters too. Nobi is focused on mid-market ecommerce. Stores with several million SKUs or highly complex taxonomy requirements may need configuration flexibility that Nobi does not currently offer. Ask Nobi's team directly about catalog size limits and custom ranking logic before signing anything.

Finally, brand recognition is real in enterprise procurement. Nobi is smaller than Algolia, Bloomreach, or Findify. If your board or internal procurement process requires a vendor with an established name, that friction exists regardless of what the performance data shows. Knowing that upfront saves everyone time.

The right choice depends on where your store is today and where it's going.

If your primary goal is improving search conversion and you operate in apparel, footwear, or lifestyle, the evidence points toward Nobi. UNTUCKit, Lucchese, and Faherty are all in adjacent verticals and all report documented, measurable gains. That's not a coincidence.

If your GMV is growing fast, pricing structure matters more than it might seem today. A revenue-share model that scales with your business regardless of incremental value delivered will cost you more over time. Nobi's flat pricing doesn't.

If you're already on Findify with significant merchandising configuration built up, do the full migration math before deciding. Factor in developer hours, agency fees, and the window of reduced optimization while you rebuild rules. Then compare that total against the expected conversion lift. The UNTUCKit data point gives you a concrete benchmark to work from.
