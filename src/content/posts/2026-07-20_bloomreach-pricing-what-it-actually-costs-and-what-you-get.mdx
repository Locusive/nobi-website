## What does Bloomreach charge for, and how is the contract structured?

Bloomreach prices on three dimensions: catalog size (SKUs indexed), customers served (monthly unique visitors or profiles), and event volume - page views, clicks, searches, and impressions processed. Those three variables go into an annual contract negotiated through a sales conversation. There is no public calculator and no self-serve signup. Enterprise contracts commonly run six figures per year.

The base license covers Bloomreach's full commerce experience platform: search, merchandising, content management, and customer data modules. Most brands won't use all of that on day one. You're paying for the entire platform whether you need it or not.

Implementation adds substantially to first-year spend. Getting Bloomreach fully live is a services engagement measured in quarters, not weeks. That means a data integration phase, a configuration phase, and a tuning phase before the platform is doing what you bought it for - all of it billed on top of the license.

The upshot for a brand evaluating Bloomreach: budget for a six-figure annual contract, add a services line item for implementation, and plan for several months before the platform is live. If your primary need is site search and you don't need the CDP and content modules, you're buying more than the job requires.

## What does Bloomreach Discovery include, and what costs extra?

That six-figure annual contract buys you a module, not a product. Bloomreach Discovery covers AI-powered product search, category page merchandising, product recommendations, and SEO-optimized pages. It's a capable module for brands that need search to understand shopper intent and serve relevant results across a large catalog.

What it doesn't cover: answering shoppers' questions before they buy.

Conversational Shopping - Bloomreach's Loomi AI layer that handles grounded pre-purchase Q&A - is a separate module with its own line item. So is Engagement, which covers email, SMS, and omnichannel marketing campaigns. And Content, the CMS and landing-page authoring module, is separate again. A brand that wants search AND a way to answer shoppers asking about sizing, materials, or return policies needs at minimum Discovery plus Conversational Shopping - two contracts, not one bundled product.

This matters because the modules are designed to compound. The CDP layer that runs underneath Bloomreach ties cross-channel data together and powers personalization across search, recommendations, and campaigns. Without real behavioral data flowing in - clicks, purchases, browsing sessions - the personalization promise stays theoretical. Discovery works on its own, but it works much better when Engagement and the CDP are feeding it real behavioral signals.

The practical upshot: a brand evaluating Bloomreach for search alone is buying into a platform architecture whose full value depends on multiple modules running together. The more of those modules you add, the more the annual contract grows - and the more months the implementation runs before everything is doing what you bought it for.

For merchants who need search plus the ability to answer shopper questions on the same bill, that modular structure is the first thing worth understanding about Bloomreach's pricing.

## What implementation and services costs should I budget beyond the Bloomreach license?

That modular structure the previous section described has a direct cost consequence: more modules means more implementation work, and Bloomreach implementations run months, not days. A typical rollout involves connecting your catalog feed, wiring up behavioral event tracking, integrating the search front end, letting the personalization model train on real traffic, and migrating your content schema. Each of those is a real engineering project, not a configuration checkbox.

Most brands bring in a Bloomreach implementation partner or engage Bloomreach professional services to manage the rollout. Either way, that services engagement is a separate line item on top of the annual license fee. The first-year total reflects both.

After go-live, the platform's personalization capabilities depend on behavioral data that accumulates over time. The ranker sharpens as it sees real click signals, purchase history, and browse behavior from live traffic. That means the full value of what you bought takes additional weeks or months to materialize after the platform is live - not from day one.

Ongoing maintenance adds to the resource picture too. Feed updates, model monitoring, and merchandising rule management all require internal attention after the implementation partner hands things off. This isn't a set-it-and-forget-it platform; it rewards teams with the capacity to keep it tuned.

Coveo carries a similar profile. It's also sales-led, with substantial services costs on top of licensing and an implementation engagement that typically requires a dedicated project.

For merchants evaluating Bloomreach primarily for search, this multi-quarter runway is worth pricing explicitly. The license buys access to the platform; the implementation and ongoing maintenance determine whether it delivers on the personalization promise. Budget a realistic first-year cost that includes both, and plan for a meaningful ramp period before the behavioral data is rich enough to do what the platform is designed to do.

## How does Bloomreach pricing compare to other ecommerce search platforms?

That first-year all-in cost puts Bloomreach at the top of the ecommerce search market. [Coveo](https://www.coveo.com) is the closest in price: sales-led with no published rate, though third-party sources consistently report a base plan around $600/month. Annual licensing commonly runs $50K+, and first-year totals often reach $100K+ when services are added to the bill.

[Constructor](https://www.constructor.io) prices on a revenue-share model tied to GMV. There's no published rate and no floor, so costs scale with every dollar of revenue growth. For a brand that's scaling quickly, that unpredictability is the main trade-off.

[Algolia](https://www.algolia.com) is the most accessible on entry pricing: a free Build tier covers 10,000 searches per month, and pay-as-you-go tiers above that run well under $500/month for typical mid-market volumes. One catch: NeuralSearch, the semantic matching layer, is only available on the top-tier Elevate plan. Lower tiers run keyword ranking only. Stores using instant-search can also see usage climb faster than expected, since each keystroke generates a billed query.

[Klevu](https://athoscommerce.com), now a division of Athos Commerce, doesn't publish dollar figures on its pricing page. Third-party sources put the range at roughly $499–$1,598/month. Personalization features are in the Expert tier only; Essential and Advanced tiers don't include them.

Searchspring, also under Athos Commerce and quote-only, is built around a manual rule-based merchandising model. Your team manages the relevance logic rather than an AI model doing it by default.

Nobi publishes its rates: $25/month base, including 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages. Above that, $0.01 per additional search and $0.10 per additional message. No revenue share, no per-seat fees, no AI features held behind a higher tier.

## When does Bloomreach's price make sense, and when should you look at alternatives?

Bloomreach's price makes sense when an omnichannel retailer is consolidating search, CMS, CDP, and marketing automation into a single platform contract and has the internal team for a multi-quarter rollout. The platform earns its cost when all those signals - search behavior, email engagement, content interactions, lifecycle data - sit on the same unified profile and inform each other. Brands that only need better site search, or a grounded way to answer pre-purchase shopper questions, are paying for platform infrastructure they won't use. Those specific needs are served by tools that cost a fraction of a Bloomreach contract.

**Pick Bloomreach when:** your brand operates across multiple channels and surfaces, you're ready to commit to a multi-quarter implementation, and you need the CDP layer to be the connective tissue across search, marketing, and content. The platform is built for that scope. Without the full investment - in time, in budget, in internal team capacity - you're running Discovery as a standalone search tool inside a platform built to do far more.

**Skip Bloomreach when:** the job is search relevance and answering shopper questions before they abandon. A mid-market brand with a DTC Shopify store and a team of five doesn't need a CDP and a content module to cut its zero-result rate or answer sizing questions on a PDP.

For teams in that position, the realistic alternatives depend on what you actually need.

[Constructor](https://www.constructor.io) is the closest enterprise alternative if full-site behavioral personalization is the goal - search, browse, recommendations, and category pages all reranking from shopper signals. The trade-off is a revenue-share pricing model tied to GMV, which scales with every dollar of growth and is hard to model upfront.

[Algolia](https://www.algolia.com) fits when an engineering team wants API control over ranking and can own the configuration long-term. The infrastructure is there; the work is yours to do. Usage costs scale with query volume - stores using instant-search generate a billed query on every keystroke, which can add up faster than expected at higher traffic volumes.

[Klevu](https://athoscommerce.com) and Searchspring - both now divisions of Athos Commerce - are mid-market ecommerce search platforms built for teams that want faster implementations and no enterprise contract. Both are quote-only, but implementations run days rather than months. Worth noting: shortlisting both means comparing two products from the same parent company.

Nobi fits when the job is fast semantic search plus grounded answers to shopper questions - sizing, materials, return policy - without a platform contract or an implementation project. [UNTUCKit](/customers/untuckit) ran a two-month A/B test and saw a 17.1% conversion rate lift over their prior search tool; [Kilte](/customers/kilte) saw a 21.7% lift over Shopify's default search. Both results came from better search relevance, not a six-month rollout. Pricing is $25/month base, with $0.01 per additional search and $0.10 per additional conversational message above the included quota - no revenue share, no per-seat fees.

The honest weakness: Nobi doesn't offer behavioral reranking today. If results reordering based on individual shopper click and purchase history is the primary requirement, Constructor or Klevu's Expert tier addresses that. Nobi's personalization is limited to contextual starter messages and placeholder text. For teams whose bottleneck is relevance and pre-purchase Q&A rather than deep personalization, that trade-off rarely comes up.

## Frequently asked questions about Bloomreach pricing

A few questions come up repeatedly when buyers are trying to scope a Bloomreach evaluation - especially around what's included, whether lighter-weight options exist, and how the contract evolves over time. The prior section laid out when Bloomreach's price makes sense and when it doesn't; these questions tend to follow from that decision point.

**Does Bloomreach offer a free trial?**

Bloomreach has no published free tier or trial period. Getting access to the platform requires a sales conversation and a demo request. There is no self-serve way to explore the product before that conversation.

**Can I buy just Bloomreach Discovery without the full platform?**

Discovery is available as a module, but it's still sold as part of an enterprise contract. There is no lightweight self-serve Discovery subscription. Buyers who want only search are still buying into the enterprise contract structure.

**Is Bloomreach pricing publicly available?**

No. All pricing is quoted through the sales team. Bloomreach.com shows no tiers, no calculators, and no starting figures. You won't know the number until you're in a sales conversation.

**What's a typical contract length?**

Annual contracts are standard. At enterprise scale, multi-year commitments with volume tiers are common.

**Does adding Loomi AI (Conversational Shopping) significantly change the contract?**

It adds a second line item. Loomi AI is a separate module from Discovery, so combining both affects the overall contract value during negotiation. Buyers evaluating search plus pre-purchase Q&A in one place are negotiating a multi-module scope from the start.
