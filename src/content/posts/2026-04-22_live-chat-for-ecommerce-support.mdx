## What does 'support deflection' actually mean in ecommerce live chat?

Support deflection is the share of customer questions resolved through self-service (chat, help center, or an AI assistant) before a human agent ever sees the ticket. In ecommerce, the honest way to measure it is self-service resolution rate per intent, not aggregate chat volume. A good pilot targets 30–50% deflection on a single intent before expanding, because "total tickets down" without intent segmentation hides where the automation is actually pulling its weight and where it's silently failing.

Two numbers matter, and teams constantly conflate them. Containment means the chat never escalates to a human. Resolution means the shopper's problem actually got solved. A conversation can be contained and unresolved (the shopper gave up), which looks great on a dashboard and terrible in churn.

Pair deflection with CSAT on deflected conversations as your guardrail. A 60% deflection rate at 2.8 CSAT is worse than 35% at 4.6. And know your mix: roughly 40–50% of ecommerce tickets are WISMO, 15–20% returns, 15–20% product and sizing, the rest long-tail. Pick the fattest intent first.

## Which support questions should live chat deflect first?

Start with the highest-volume, lowest-risk intent: product and policy questions. Sizing, materials, care instructions, shipping timeframes, and return windows are [answerable from a fixed knowledge base](https://nobi.ai/knowledge-base), carry little downside if the bot defers to a human, and are where shoppers silently drop off before they ever open a ticket. These are the questions a confident answer converts, and a wrong answer costs you a follow-up email at worst. Order-status (WISMO) and returns are higher-leverage but require live order data, so sequence them second and third.

Tier 1 is pre-purchase: product attributes, sizing guidance, stock availability, shipping policy, and returns policy. Ground the bot in your PDP data plus a single static policy doc and you cover the majority of pre-checkout friction without any backend integration. This is also the tier where a shopping assistant pays for itself twice: deflected tickets on one side, recovered carts on the other. Nobi sits here by design, answering pre-purchase questions from your catalog and content so the shopper decides without bouncing.

Tier 2 is WISMO. "Where is my order?" is the single biggest ticket driver for most ecommerce brands, but answering it means pulling live order data from Shopify or your helpdesk. Plan for an integration, not a prompt tweak.

Tier 3 is the write-back layer: returns initiation, refund requests, order edits, address changes. These need authenticated writes to Shopify or your ERP and usually sit inside a helpdesk like Gorgias or Zendesk. Nobi doesn't process returns or refunds inside the chat, so pair it with your helpdesk for this tier.

Leave account disputes, payment failures, and damaged-item claims to humans. The CSAT hit from a wrong automated answer on an angry ticket outweighs any deflection math you'd model.

## How do you prevent an AI chatbot from hallucinating wrong answers to shoppers?

Hallucinations happen when the model generates an answer that isn't grounded in your actual product catalog, policy pages, or order data. The defense is structural, not stylistic. Retrieve every answer from a constrained source: your product feed, your help center articles, your order API. Refuse to answer questions that fall outside those sources. Escalate to a human when confidence drops. Never let the bot invent shipping windows, return policies, or stock status. Either the data is live or the bot stays quiet.

Start with retrieval-grounded generation. Every response the assistant produces should trace back to a specific product record, a specific policy doc, or a specific order lookup. If the retrieval step returns nothing relevant, the bot should say it doesn't know and offer a handoff, not improvise a plausible-sounding answer. This is the difference between an assistant that quotes your 30-day return window from the actual policy page and one that confidently makes up "60 days" because that sounds reasonable.

Set hard guardrails on scope. Medical advice, legal questions, pricing negotiations, competitor comparisons, anything that isn't "help this shopper find or buy a product, or look up their order" should hit a refusal, not a generated answer. A narrow scope is a feature.

Use confidence thresholds for handoff. If the model's certainty about a retrieved answer falls below your bar, route to a human or to a "let me get someone who can confirm that" message. Guessing is worse than waiting.

Audit the logs. Sample 5% of deflected conversations every week and read them. Hallucinations decay CSAT silently, the shopper doesn't always file a ticket, they just don't come back. Watch for the classic failure mode: the bot cheerfully confirms "free returns" when your policy actually charges a $7 restocking fee. That's the kind of error that surfaces as a chargeback three weeks later, not a complaint you can fix in real time.

## What does a realistic live chat deflection rollout look like in 30 days?

A credible rollout is staged, scoped to one intent, and measured against a pre-change baseline. You don't flip the bot on across every page and hope the numbers move. You pick one intent, run it against a real baseline, and expand only when the quality holds.

**Week 1: baseline.** Pull the last 30 days of tickets and chats. Tag each one by intent: pre-purchase product question, shipping status, returns, order modification, account issue, everything else. Record the current deflection rate and CSAT per intent. Without this, you can't tell whether week four is actually better or just busier.

**Week 2: narrow launch.** Deploy on a single intent, typically pre-purchase product questions, to 25% of traffic. Write the escalation rule before the bot goes live, not after the first complaint. A reasonable default: escalate on any message mentioning refund, damaged, wrong item, cancel, or where the shopper explicitly asks for a human. Lock refusals for anything outside the grounded catalog.

**Weeks 3–4: audit and expand.** Sample 50 transcripts per day. Read them. Flag any answer that isn't supported by your product data or policy docs, tune the refusal behavior, and strip unsupported claims from the system prompt. Only after CSAT on deflected sessions holds steady against the week 1 baseline should you add a second intent, usually shipping status or sizing.

Measure three numbers, per intent: deflection rate, CSAT on deflected sessions, and ticket volume before versus after. Do not measure total chats. Chat volume almost always goes up when the chat experience gets better because more shoppers engage with it. Tickets are the number that reflects real support load. If tickets on your launched intent drop and CSAT is flat or higher, the rollout is working. If tickets drop but CSAT falls, you're deflecting by frustrating people, which shows up as churn a month later.
