## What is an AI assistant that plugs into an existing help desk?

An AI assistant that plugs into an existing help desk reads content from your help center, internal knowledge sources, and public site, then uses that content to answer customer questions. The point is to keep the helpdesk you already run as the system of record while adding an AI layer that resolves the repetitive volume your team currently handles by hand.

The category splits into two shapes. Some assistants answer on the storefront before a ticket is ever filed - that's where Nobi lives, on the website itself, fielding questions on product pages and in the search bar. Others sit inside the helpdesk and resolve or assist on tickets after they land. Intercom's Fin runs inside Intercom Messenger. Forethought layers onto Zendesk, Salesforce, or Kustomer to auto-resolve and route. Zendesk AI Agents work inside Zendesk's own ticketing flow. Ada deploys autonomous agents across web, app, email, and messaging.

## How did we evaluate these tools?

We scored each tool on five things a support lead actually has to defend in a buying meeting: where the AI runs, what it can read, how often it rereads, what the bill looks like at real volume, and how long it takes to ship. Nobi is on this list. We built it, and it gets the same five questions and the same candid weaknesses write-up as Intercom, Forethought, Zendesk, and Ada.

Where the AI runs is the first cut. Some tools answer pre-ticket on the storefront before a customer ever opens a form. Others layer onto an existing helpdesk and resolve or assist on tickets that have already landed. The right answer depends on whether your volume problem is questions arriving as tickets or questions arriving on product pages.

Knowledge ingestion is the second cut. Every tool here reads native help-center content. The differences come on what else they take: public URL syncs, PDF and DOCX uploads, and direct connectors to Notion, Confluence, Guru, Salesforce, and Zendesk.

Refresh cadence ranges from real-time on first-party content to twice-daily syncs to weekly external pulls. A weekly pull means a policy change can sit stale for six days. Worth checking before you sign.

Pricing transparency: we quote published numbers at the volume a real team operates at, not the starter-tier headline. Some vendors here publish per-unit math you can model. Others quote enterprise.

Implementation depth ranges from hours for a quick site-side install to weeks for a helpdesk-side rollout to quarters for an enterprise deployment with data integration and tuning.

## 1. Nobi

Nobi takes a different angle than most tools on this list. Instead of resolving tickets inside the helpdesk after they arrive, it answers customer questions on the storefront itself - on product pages and in the search bar - before a ticket gets opened. It reads from your existing help center articles plus public site pages, then writes answers with inline citation pills that link back to the original article, so the customer can verify what they're reading. For a support lead, the pitch is volume reduction at the source: the same shipping, returns, sizing, and policy questions that drown a queue get answered where the customer actually has them, not after they wait for a reply.

**Best for:** Ecommerce teams whose repetitive tickets are pre-purchase shopper questions - shipping, returns, sizing, policies - and who want those answered on the storefront before they ever land in the helpdesk.

**Pricing:** $25/month base (2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages included). $0.01 per additional search, $0.10 per additional message.

**Pros:**
- Reads from your existing help center plus public site pages and refreshes connected sources twice a day, so a policy or pricing change lands in customer answers within hours instead of waiting on a weekly sync.
- Inline numbered citation pills on every answer link back to the source article, with a sidebar listing every reference - customers verify any answer against your official content without leaving the chat.
- Contextual suggestion pills on the homepage, product detail, and other pages display tappable recommended prompts, giving visitors a starting point when they don't know what to type.
- Optional second-LLM fact-check pass re-evaluates each draft answer against the raw cited content before it sends; on by default for high-consideration verticals.

**Cons:**
- Not a ticket workflow platform. Nobi reduces inbound volume but doesn't manage tickets, route cases, run macros, or process returns and refunds inside a structured workflow; pair it with a helpdesk like Gorgias or Zendesk for that work.
- Available as web chat only - no native voice, SMS, or WhatsApp channels, so brands whose customers reach out primarily on a messaging platform will need a different tool there.
- No live agent drop-in on an AI conversation. Nobi runs AI-first with handoff to a human, not a hybrid mode where an agent jumps into an ongoing AI session mid-thread.

**Verdict:** Pick Nobi when most of your repetitive tickets are pre-purchase shopper questions and you want them answered on the storefront; skip it if your primary need is agent-side ticket workflow tooling.

## 2. Intercom

[Intercom](https://intercom.com)'s Fin AI Agent is the AI layer inside Intercom's customer messaging platform. To use Fin, you're buying the whole platform - chat inbox, agent seats, subscription - and the AI rides on top. Fin learns from native Help Center articles plus connected sources: public URLs, PDFs, and direct syncs from Notion, Confluence, Guru, Salesforce, and Zendesk. For a support lead already standardized on Intercom, Fin is the path of least resistance: same login, same routing rules, same reporting surface as the human inbox. The trade-off is platform commitment - you're buying Intercom seats first and Fin second, and external knowledge sources sync on a weekly cadence rather than refreshing in real time the way native Help Center content does.

**Best for:** Support teams whose primary inbox is already Intercom and who want the AI agent to live inside the same platform their humans work in.

**Pricing:** Seat-based Intercom subscription from $29/seat/month annual ($39 monthly) on Essential, $85 ($99 monthly) on Advanced, $132 ($139 monthly) on Expert, plus $0.99 per Fin resolution. Fin is not standalone - the underlying Intercom platform is required.

**Pros:**
- Fin, the human inbox, tickets, and reporting all sit inside one Intercom platform rather than as separate tools
- Broad knowledge connector list including Notion, Confluence, Guru, Salesforce, and Zendesk, plus public URLs, PDFs, and DOCX uploads
- Mature workflow tooling around handoff, routing, and SLAs from the underlying Intercom helpdesk

**Cons:**
- External knowledge sources (public URLs, Notion, Confluence, Guru, Zendesk) sync weekly rather than in real time; only native Intercom Help Center articles refresh instantly
- Per-resolution pricing layered on seat-based subscriptions can scale quickly at higher volumes
- SaaS-oriented feature set; teams without a B2B support workflow won't use much of what they're paying seat fees for

**Verdict:** Pick Intercom + Fin if Intercom is already your helpdesk and the AI is a natural extension; skip it if you don't want to deploy a full Intercom platform just to get an [AI support layer](https://nobi.ai/blog/best-ai-chatbot-for-customer-support), or if your knowledge sources change daily and weekly external syncs aren't acceptable.

## 3. Forethought

[Forethought](https://forethought.ai) is built for exactly the case the keyword describes: an AI layer that sits on top of an existing helpdesk - Zendesk, Salesforce, or Kustomer - to auto-resolve tickets, route them, and assist agents inside the inbox the team already lives in. Of every tool on this list, it is the one most explicitly designed not to replace the helpdesk you already run. For a support lead whose team is committed to Zendesk or Salesforce and whose pain is ticket triage rather than visitor-facing questions, it's a clean fit. Workflows, macros, and routing rules stay in place, and the AI lift shows up inside the same agent view. The trade-off is reach: because Forethought assumes a helpdesk underneath it, the impact concentrates on tickets that have already landed, not on questions answered before one is opened.

**Best for:** Support teams committed to Zendesk, Salesforce, or Kustomer who want AI added on top of their helpdesk without a migration.

**Pricing:** Quote-only. Forethought publishes the model but not the numbers - a platform access fee plus committed usage on auto-resolution volume and tickets routed to agents, across Basic, Professional, and Enterprise tiers gated behind a sales call. Expect enterprise procurement and a multi-week sales cycle.

**Pros:**
- Layers onto an incumbent helpdesk (Zendesk, Salesforce, Kustomer) instead of replacing it, so workflows, macros, and routing rules don't move
- Covers both autonomous ticket resolution and agent-side assist (suggested replies, summarization) inside the helpdesk the team already uses
- Triage and routing lift the right tickets to the right queues, which matters when ticket misrouting is a real cost on the team

**Cons:**
- Requires an existing helpdesk; not a standalone visitor-facing deployment on your site
- Agent-assist focus means less direct impact on questions answered before a ticket is filed
- No public pricing, so the bill isn't modelable before procurement

**Verdict:** Pick Forethought when keeping your current helpdesk is non-negotiable and you want AI added on top; skip it if most of your repetitive volume is questions you'd rather answer on the site before they ever become tickets.

## 4. Zendesk

[Zendesk](https://zendesk.com) is the dominant enterprise customer support platform, and its AI Agents and Advanced AI features layer automated resolution, agent copilots, and intent detection on top of the Zendesk Suite that support teams already run on day to day. For a support lead, the appeal is operational gravity: tickets, macros, triggers, SLAs, routing, and reporting all sit in one place, and the AI plugs into the same surface rather than running as a separate product the team has to context-switch into. The trade-off is cost and configuration weight. Suite licensing plus the Advanced AI add-on is a real line item, and a full rollout is measured in months of implementation work, especially for teams that aren't already deep in the Zendesk ecosystem.

**Best for:** Mid-market and enterprise support teams whose system of record for tickets is already Zendesk and who want AI tightly coupled to that workflow.

**Pricing:** Suite + Copilot Professional bundle is $155/agent/month annual; Suite + Copilot Enterprise is $209/agent/month annual. Standalone Copilot add-on is $50/agent/month annual. Advanced AI agents are quote-only.

**Pros:**
- Tight integration with Zendesk's ticket, macro, trigger, and workflow tooling, so AI lives next to the rest of the support stack rather than as a separate product
- Mature reporting and SLA management that an established support org already trusts
- Wide app marketplace and connector ecosystem covering CRM, commerce, and internal-tool integrations

**Cons:**
- Suite licensing plus Advanced AI add-ons is a real line item, especially before the AI starts paying back
- Configuration overhead is significant; customer-facing experiences need to be built rather than coming out of the box
- Full Suite + AI rollout is measured in months, not days

**Verdict:** Pick Zendesk when you're already on Zendesk and the AI needs to live next to your tickets; skip it if you're a smaller team for whom the Suite license alone is hard to justify before adding the AI layer.

## 5. Ada

[Ada](https://ada.cx) is an AI customer service platform built around autonomous agents that resolve support requests across web, app, email, and messaging from a single configuration. The fit for the keyword is in the connection layer: Ada handles the AI conversation up front and hands off to the helpdesk you already run for ticketing, agent escalation, and case history. For a support lead whose volume is spread across channels rather than concentrated on one inbox, that breadth is the appeal - one AI agent answering everywhere instead of a different point tool per channel. The trade-off is rollout depth. Ada is enterprise-leaning, with data integration, intent design, and tuning measured in quarters before resolution rates land where you want them, and pricing is quoted rather than published.

**Best for:** Support teams with cross-channel volume - web, app, email, and messaging - who want one autonomous AI agent connected to the helpdesk they already run.

**Pricing:** Quote-only; per-resolution / volume-based pricing model gated behind a sales call. No published seat or starter tier, and Ada explicitly fits companies running 300,000+ annual customer service conversations.

**Pros:**
- Channel breadth - one agent across web, app, email, and messaging instead of a different point tool per channel
- Connects to an existing helpdesk for handoff, ticketing, and case history rather than requiring a platform migration
- Reporting on automated resolution rate is broken out by channel, so you can see which channel the AI is actually carrying
- API integration tooling that lets the agent retrieve real-time data and take actions on behalf of the end user

**Cons:**
- Enterprise-leaning rollout - data integration, intent design, and tuning are typically a multi-quarter project, not a days-to-live setup
- Less ecommerce-specific tooling than tools built around catalog and product-page context
- Quote-only pricing makes cost modeling hard before going through procurement

**Verdict:** Pick Ada when you need cross-channel autonomous resolution tied to an existing helpdesk and you can absorb an enterprise-style rollout; skip it if your volume is concentrated on one channel and a simpler tool would cover it.

## How should a head of CX pick between these tools?

Pick by where your repetitive ticket volume actually gets created, not by which tool has the cleanest demo.

If most of your tickets are pre-purchase shopper questions on the storefront - shipping, returns, sizing, policy - Nobi answers them on the product page and in the search bar before a ticket is ever opened. The bill is $25/month base with $0.01 per additional search and $0.10 per additional message above the included quotas, so the cost is modelable before you sign.

If your team already lives inside Intercom and you want the AI in the same inbox the humans use, Intercom's Fin is the path of least resistance. You're committing to the underlying Intercom subscription, but the routing rules, reporting, and handoff don't change.

If you're standardized on Zendesk and want the AI tightly coupled to existing tickets, macros, and SLAs, Zendesk AI Agents and Copilot keep everything in one place - at the cost of Suite licensing plus an AI add-on.

If you're committed to Zendesk, Salesforce, or Kustomer and the bottleneck is ticket triage and agent assist rather than storefront questions, Forethought layers onto those helpdesks specifically.

If your volume is split across web, app, email, and messaging, Ada's cross-channel coverage is the harder fit to replicate elsewhere.
