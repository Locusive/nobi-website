## How much does Yext cost?

Yext does not publish prices on its website. Every contract goes through a sales process and is quoted based on two variables: how many entities you manage in the knowledge graph (locations, products, or listings) and which modules you enable (Answers, Listings, Reviews, Pages). The more entities you have and the more modules you turn on, the higher your annual bill.

Third-party procurement data from Vendr and buyer-reported G2 contracts consistently puts enterprise agreements at $50,000 or more per year for multi-location companies. Entry-level contracts for a single company with limited entities come in lower, but Yext's platform is built and priced for multi-location enterprise use - it's where the product does its best work, and the pricing reflects that.

Implementation costs sit on top of licensing. Knowledge-graph setup, taxonomy mapping, and integration work are typically scoped and billed separately as professional services. Budget for that work separately when sizing your first-year cost.

## What does a Yext subscription actually include?

Yext is a modular platform built around a knowledge graph - a centralized database of your company's facts (locations, hours, FAQs, product specs, policy answers) that every part of the platform draws from. That architecture explains why the contract works the way the previous section describes: pricing scales with entities and modules because the knowledge graph IS the asset, and each module is a different way to publish from it.

The core modules break down like this:

**Listings** syndicates your knowledge-graph data to Google, Bing, Apple Maps, Yelp, and 200+ other directories. One update in Yext propagates everywhere, so you're not manually correcting hours or addresses across a dozen platforms.

**Answers** is an AI-powered search widget for your own site. Instead of returning a raw list of links, it surfaces structured answers drawn directly from the knowledge graph. For a company whose visitors are searching for policy details, FAQs, or location-specific information, this is the module most similar to what a dedicated site-search tool does.

**Reviews** aggregates feedback from across directories into a single monitoring and response interface. **Pages** generates templated location or landing pages at scale from knowledge-graph data - useful when you operate many locations and need each one to have its own consistent, SEO-friendly page.

If site search is your only goal, you can scope the contract to the Answers module alone. But a few things are worth knowing before you do: the full contract still runs through Yext's sales team (no self-serve option), the platform is designed around a broader knowledge-graph use case, and you'll be paying for the infrastructure and support model of an enterprise platform even if you only activate one piece of it. Buyers who need a pure [site-search tool](https://nobi.ai/blog/the-top-ecommerce-platforms-for-native-ai-search-and-product-discovery) without the surrounding architecture often find they're overbuying on contract scope, even at the lower end of Yext's range.

## What costs do Yext buyers commonly miss in the initial quote?

Yext's per-entity, per-module pricing model means costs can grow in ways that are easy to underestimate at the quoting stage. Three categories consistently surface in buyer post-mortems: entity-count overages, professional services for onboarding, and mid-contract module additions.

**Entity-count overages** are the most common surprise. Contracts set a cap on how many entities you manage in the knowledge graph. As your location count, FAQ library, or product catalog grows, you run up against that ceiling. Overage fees kick in above it, and they're typically not visible in the initial quote because the sales conversation anchors to your current entity count, not your projected one twelve months out.

**Professional services** are almost always a separate engagement. Knowledge-graph setup, taxonomy mapping, and connecting Yext to your existing systems require configuration work that Yext's own team typically scopes and bills separately from the license. For a company new to the platform, that's a meaningful additional line item to plan for before the project starts, not after.

**Module scope creep** catches buyers who quote one piece and then discover they need another. A company that buys Listings for directory syndication often realizes mid-implementation that Answers is the module doing the site-search job they actually needed. Adding a module mid-contract typically means going back through the sales process - there's no self-serve way to activate additional modules on an existing agreement.

Integration engineering adds another layer. Connecting Yext to a custom CMS, PIM, or existing data infrastructure is billed apart from the base license. Complex setups can add tens of thousands to first-year cost, and that work is often scoped only after the license is signed.

One more item worth surfacing explicitly in any contract review: annual renewal escalators. Enterprise agreements often include built-in price increases that aren't prominently disclosed at signing. Reading the escalation clause before you sign is worth the time - a multi-year commitment at an unclear escalation rate can materially change the total cost of the relationship.

## How does Yext's pricing model compare to Algolia, Coveo, Hawk Search, and Nobi?

Every tool in this category uses a different pricing model, and the model often determines how costs scale as much as the starting number does. Yext is per-entity and per-module, with no published rates. [Algolia](https://www.algolia.com) is per-query and per-record, with a free tier. [Coveo](https://www.coveo.com) is flat annual licensing with services on top. [Hawk Search](https://www.hawksearch.com) is tiered, with enterprise pricing above that. Nobi publishes a full rate card: $25/month base with per-use overages.

**Yext** is quote-only. Costs are set by entity count and module scope. It's also the only tool here built primarily for syndicating structured data to external AI platforms and search engines beyond your own site.

**Algolia** has a free Build tier (10,000 searches/month, 1 million indexed records). Grow and Grow Plus bill per search request and per indexed record above the plan baseline. NeuralSearch, Algolia's semantic matching layer, is only on the top-tier Elevate plan - it's not in Build, Grow, or Grow Plus.

**Coveo** doesn't publish pricing. Third-party procurement data puts the base around $600/month, with all-in first-year costs commonly reaching $100,000 or more when implementation and services are included. It's built for unifying search across multiple digital properties, not standalone site search.

**Hawk Search** publishes two tiers: Core at $500/month and Premium at $850/month. Enterprise contracts are quoted per catalog size. Implementation typically runs months.

**Nobi** publishes its [full pricing](https://nobi.ai/blog/ai-search-pricing-ecommerce-cost-per-month): $25/month base includes 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages. Above that, $0.01 per additional search and $0.10 per additional message. No quote required.

Yext and Coveo are enterprise platforms where site search is one piece of a larger system. Algolia and Nobi are site-search-first. Hawk Search focuses on catalog navigation for complex product catalogs and B2B buying workflows.

## When is Yext worth the price - and when should you evaluate alternatives?

Yext earns its enterprise price when your primary job is keeping structured facts consistent across external AI surfaces and directories - Google AI Overviews, Yelp, Apple Maps, voice assistants - at scale across multiple locations. If your primary job is site search, grounded Q&A on your own site, or lead capture, you're paying for knowledge-syndication infrastructure and missing the capabilities that actually move conversion.

**Pick Yext** when you manage multiple locations whose hours, policies, and FAQs need to be accurate everywhere a customer might find you, and when you have the budget and team for a sales-led, services-heavy onboarding.

**Pick [Algolia](https://www.algolia.com)** when a dedicated search engineering team wants full API control over how product attributes factor into ranking and has the bandwidth to own configuration and relevance tuning over time.

**Pick [Coveo](https://www.coveo.com)** when you're unifying [developer docs](https://nobi.ai/blog/best-ai-search-for-developer-documentation), customer support portals, and internal knowledge at enterprise scale under one relevance engine - and you have the appetite for a first-year commitment that commonly runs $100,000 or more once licensing and services are included.

**Pick [Hawk Search](https://www.hawksearch.com)** when your catalog has hundreds of attributes per product and filter-driven navigation is how customers find what they want. B2B sellers on BigCommerce or Optimizely are the natural fit.

**Pick Nobi** when you need [semantic site search](https://nobi.ai/blog/best-semantic-search-ecommerce), grounded conversational answers with inline citations, and lead capture working together from day one - with published pricing, no sales process, and a quick site-side install rather than an implementation sprint before anything goes live.

Two real limits worth naming: Nobi does not syndicate answers to external AI surfaces or directories the way Yext does. Its personalization today covers personalized placeholder text and starter messages - it does not do behavioral reranking based on individual visitor click or purchase history the way enterprise-tier search platforms do.

## Frequently asked questions about Yext pricing

**Does Yext offer a free trial?**
Yext does not publish a self-serve free trial. Evaluations run as sales-led proof-of-concept engagements, so you're working with the sales team before you see the product in your environment.

**Is Yext pricing per user or per location?**
Yext's primary pricing unit is the entity in the knowledge graph - locations, products, or listings - not per seat. More entities means a higher contract; adding users does not.

**Can I buy just the Yext Answers module for site search?**
Yes, Yext Answers can be scoped standalone, but the contract still runs through sales. Buyers who only need site search often find the platform oversized for that use case alone.

**How does Nobi compare to Yext Answers?**
Both surface answers from your own content. Yext Answers pulls from a structured knowledge graph you maintain; Nobi's assistant is grounded in any connected content - product pages, PDFs, help-center articles, policy docs - with inline citations visitors can verify. Nobi's pricing starts at $25/month and is fully published; Yext Answers pricing is quoted.

**What is Yext's contract length?**
Yext contracts are typically annual. Multi-year agreements can reduce per-entity costs but require upfront commitment and reduce flexibility to descope.
