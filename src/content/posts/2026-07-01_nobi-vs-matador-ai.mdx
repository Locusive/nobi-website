## What's the core difference between Nobi and Matador AI for a dealership?

Nobi is a website AI assistant that answers shopper questions on the VDP and SRP with cited sources and routes leads into your CRM. Matador AI is a [conversational platform](https://nobi.ai/blog/best-conversational-ai-used-car-dealerships) that works a lead across phone, text, email, social, and web chat once they're already in your funnel. The two solve different leaks in the same funnel.

Nobi lives on the dealership website. Its job is stopping the bounce: a shopper reading a VDP at night who can't find an answer and leaves instead of setting an appointment. It answers from your inventory feed and policy pages, with a citation back to the source, and hands qualified interest to your BDC instead of guessing.

Matador AI lives across channels, not the site. Once a shopper becomes an internet up or phone up, Matador keeps the lead warm with automated follow-up across text, email, and social, backed by TCPA compliance tooling built for dealerships. It plugs into your existing CRM rather than replacing it, and inventory-aware discovery is thin by comparison.

Nobi's rate card lets a dealership marketing manager pencil out cost before a sales call ever happens: $25/month covers 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages, with overage at $0.01 per search and $0.10 per message. That's a fixed number to plug into next year's budget, not a figure a BDC has to chase down from a sales rep. Matador AI works differently. Its pricing is tiered per dealership and quoted directly, and that reflects what Matador actually covers: phone, text, email, social, and web chat, all wrapped into one conversational platform with TCPA compliance built in for SMS. A rooftop buying Matador is buying coverage across five channels, not one, so a published flat rate would undersell what's actually being priced.

Neither vendor charges a revenue-share or a per-lead fee. A BDC manager budgeting against a monthly number doesn't want that number moving with volume, and a variable cost tied to how many leads convert makes forecasting a guessing game. Nobi's model stays inside a published rate card; Matador's stays inside a quoted contract. Both are flat in that sense, just arrived at differently: one you can calculate yourself, the other you get from a sales conversation scaled to your rooftop's channel footprint.

For a dealership comparing the two, the real question isn't which number is smaller. It's whether the coverage matches the spend. A single-channel site assistant priced by usage and a multi-channel BDC platform priced by contract are answering different budgeting questions, and the pricing model each vendor picked follows directly from what it's selling.

## Which channels does each one actually cover?

Matador AI covers phone, text, email, social, and web chat from one platform, the wider footprint of the two. Nobi covers the dealership website only, the [search bar and chat widget on the VDP and SRP](https://nobi.ai/blog/best-ai-search-for-car-dealerships), with no phone, SMS, or email channel.

Where the coverage gap actually shows up depends on where the shopper is when they go quiet. If a phone up doesn't get a same-day callback, or an internet lead goes cold on SMS three days after the initial email, that's a Matador problem, not a Nobi one. Nobi has no phone or SMS presence at all, so it can't touch a lead once it's left the website and moved into your CRM as a name and a number.

If the leak looks different, a shopper reading a VDP at night, unable to get a straight answer on trim or financing, and closing the tab instead of submitting the form, that's Nobi's lane. Matador doesn't sit on the page at that moment; its job starts once the lead already exists in the system.

Matador also plugs into your existing CRM instead of replacing it. Your BDC keeps its current workflow and tools, and Matador handles the follow-up volume a human team can't manually sustain across five channels at once. Nobi works the same way on the front end: it hands qualified interest to your CRM rather than trying to be one.

A rooftop with both leaks, website bounces and cold CRM leads, needs both channels covered, and neither tool substitutes for the other's lane.

## How do Nobi and Matador AI guard against a bot quoting a sold car or the wrong price?

Nobi's query overrides let a dealer lock an exact, dealer-approved answer to a specific question, a price, a warranty term, a return policy, so the assistant returns that verbatim text instead of letting an LLM paraphrase it. Every other answer routes through Nobi's grounded pipeline with inline citation pills showing the source document and excerpt, so a GM can verify what the assistant actually said and where it came from. That matters on the same website surface handling those late-night VDP questions from the prior section: the stakes go up when the question is "how much" instead of "does it have a sunroof."

The Chevy Watsonville incident, a bot agreeing to sell a Tahoe for $1, is the scenario query overrides exist to prevent. A dealership can't risk a website assistant agreeing to a price that isn't real, and query overrides give the GM control over the highest-stakes prompts instead of trusting an LLM to paraphrase correctly every time. Nobi also runs a second AI review, on by default for high-consideration industries: after the assistant drafts an answer, a second model checks it against the raw cited source before the answer ships, and only answers that pass go out. Nobi's connected knowledge sources refresh twice a day, so a changed price or an updated inventory feed reaches the assistant's answers within hours instead of sitting stale for days.

Matador AI's compliance depth runs in a different direction. It's built for TCPA consent on SMS campaigns, the rules around texting a lead who hasn't opted in, not for locking verbatim answers to inventory or pricing questions. That fits where Matador operates: once a lead exists in your CRM, the risk is a compliance violation on an outbound message, not a bot inventing a price on a vehicle page. A rooftop worried about a website assistant quoting a sold unit needs Nobi's override and citation layer; a rooftop worried about TCPA exposure on SMS follow-up needs Matador's compliance tooling. They're guarding against different mistakes.

## How fast can each one go live at a single rooftop?

Nobi implementation is measured in hours, not months. Connect the dealership's existing site content, VDPs, FAQ pages, policy docs, and the knowledge base builds itself with no manual re-entry. Matador AI's rollout centers on multi-channel setup: phone, text, email, social, and web chat, plus TCPA consent configuration for SMS campaigns. That's more setup surface than a single-channel website assistant.

For a rooftop testing an AI tool for the first time, that gap matters. A GM or marketing manager who wants to see something live this week has less to get wrong with a website-only install than with a platform that needs SMS consent flows, phone routing, and social integrations all working together before the first lead touches it. Nobi's knowledge base refreshes from connected content automatically, so there's no manual catalog entry to maintain once it's live, just VDPs, FAQ pages, and policy docs the dealership already publishes.

Matador's setup cost buys something real, though. A rooftop already running (or planning) multi-channel BDC outreach isn't choosing between five channels and one; it's choosing whether that outreach runs on one platform or gets stitched together from separate tools. For that dealership, the TCPA compliance configuration and cross-channel setup aren't overhead. They're the reason to buy Matador in the first place.

Neither vendor asks for a multi-month commitment. Nobi's install is a same-week project measured against a single website; Matador's is a heavier lift measured against five channels, but still a platform configuration, not a system migration. The right comparison for a rooftop isn't speed alone. It's speed against how much surface each tool actually has to cover.

## When is Matador AI the better choice over Nobi?

Matador AI is the better pick when the lead leak is on phone or SMS, aged and orphaned leads going cold in the CRM, or internet ups that never get a timely follow-up call. Its TCPA compliance engine also matters directly if the rooftop is running SMS broadcast re-engagement campaigns against a big list of aged leads.

A BDC manager staring at acres-of-diamonds aged leads sitting in the CRM has a follow-up problem, not a website problem. That's squarely what Matador is built for: [automated, persistent contact across text, email, and social](https://nobi.ai/blog/conversica-alternatives-car-dealerships) until an aged or orphaned lead either books an appointment or opts out. Coverage across five channels is exactly what pays off here. A shopper who submitted a form three weeks ago and went quiet doesn't need a better VDP experience. They need someone (or something) reaching back out on a schedule a human BDC team can't sustain across a database that size.

The TCPA piece raises the stakes beyond convenience. Texting a lead who hasn't given proper consent is a real legal exposure, and a dealership running SMS broadcast campaigns against thousands of aged records needs that compliance layer built into the platform, not bolted on after the fact. A website assistant has no way to touch this risk at all. Nobi never sends an SMS, so it can't create or solve a TCPA problem, but it also can't be the fix if TCPA is what's keeping a rooftop from running the re-engagement campaign it actually needs.

That's the test worth applying before buying either tool: is the website the leak, or is the CRM the leak? If shoppers are getting good answers on the VDP and still converting to appointments at a normal rate, but aged leads are piling up untouched three rows deep in the CRM, adding a website assistant won't move that number. Matador is built for the leak that's actually happening. Diagnosing which leak you have should come before either purchase.

## When is Nobi the better choice over Matador AI?

Nobi is the better pick when the diagnosis lands on the website: shoppers bouncing off the VDP or SRP because they can't get a straight, verifiable answer to a question, hours, financing terms, trade-in basics, inventory availability. Nobi answers from the dealership's own connected content with inline citations a shopper can check, and query overrides remove the risk of a paraphrased wrong answer on high-stakes questions.

A marketing manager watching a high bounce rate on VDPs that strong ad spend is driving traffic to has a website problem, not a follow-up problem. That's the gap Matador doesn't touch. Matador's job starts once a lead already exists in the CRM; it has no presence on the page itself, so a shopper who closes the tab at 10pm never becomes a Matador conversation in the first place. Nobi is honest about the flip side of that split too: it doesn't send a text, place a call, or send an email, so it isn't a fix for aged leads going cold in the CRM. That's still Matador's lane.

For a rooftop worried about CSI damage from a bad AI interaction, a cited, verifiable answer protects the score better than a bot that can't show its source. If a customer questions what the assistant told them, the dealership can point to the exact document and excerpt the answer came from instead of arguing over what the bot might have said.
