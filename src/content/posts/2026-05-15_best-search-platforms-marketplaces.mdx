## What should a marketplace look for in a search platform?

Marketplace search has to do three things at once that ordinary catalog search doesn't. It has to scale to millions of listings from third-party sellers without query latency creeping past a few hundred milliseconds. It has to hold relevance steady while the catalog churns, with sellers adding and pulling listings every hour. And it has to answer buyer and seller questions in different contexts without crossing the wires. Platforms that win marketplace deals get catalog scale and freshness right first. What separates them is ranking control, how cleanly they handle buyer and seller contexts separately, and whether they add AI assistant features on top.

A few things matter more than the feature checklist. Per-query latency under load is the one shoppers feel first - mobile buyers drop slow results pages fast. Buyer search and seller admin are different jobs, and a good platform keeps those two result sets separate. Total cost of ownership is rarely just the license; usage-based pricing, professional services, and engineering hours all land in the year-one bill.

Total cost of ownership is where vendor pitches and reality often diverge. The license is rarely the whole bill - usage tiers, professional services, and the engineering hours to keep the index tuned all show up by year-end. Ranking and merchandising control is the last one: who actually changes a result order, and do they need to file a ticket with engineering to do it.

## 2. Algolia

[Algolia](https://algolia.com) is a search API built for engineering teams. Rules, ranking, synonyms, and merchandising are all configured in code, with sub-50 ms response times and NeuralSearch on higher tiers when keyword relevance alone falls short. For a marketplace running into the millions of listings with engineers on staff, that API model is the appeal - you own how seller rating, ships-fast, and in-stock attributes get weighted, and you can build the exact buyer-facing UX you want. Custom ranking and the storefront search experience scale with the engineering time you put in, not with the size of the deal you signed.

**Best for:** Marketplace engineering teams that want full API control over ranking, indexing, and the storefront search UX at multi-million-listing scale.

**Pricing:** Usage-based, tied to monthly search volume. Mid-market deployments commonly run $2K+/month before custom relevance engineering work; the enterprise tier is quoted through sales and is not publicly listed.

**Pros:**
- Sub-50 ms search response times and fast indexing at catalog scale, which matters when sellers are adding and pulling listings constantly
- Large ecosystem of libraries, InstantSearch widgets, and integrations for every major frontend stack
- NeuralSearch adds semantic matching on top of keyword relevance for long multi-attribute queries that keyword-only search drops
- Granular ranking and indexing control lets engineering teams weight specific attributes - seller rating, ships-fast, in-stock - however they choose

**Cons:**
- Requires developers to implement and maintain; custom ranking and bespoke UX scale with engineering hours, not contract size
- Usage-based pricing can produce surprise bills during traffic spikes - a flash sale or holiday weekend changes the monthly bill materially
- Configuration complexity is real; non-technical merchandisers cannot drive relevance work alone

**Verdict:** Pick Algolia when a dedicated search engineering team owns ranking at multi-million-listing scale; skip it when non-engineering staff need to drive relevance and you want a flat-rate monthly bill.

## 3. Coveo

[Coveo](https://coveo.com) runs one ML ranking engine across commerce search, customer support portals, and internal knowledge bases. The ranker weights results by session signals, catalog attributes, and cross-shopper behavioral history, and the same engine runs on every property you point it at. For a marketplace that also runs a buyer-support portal and a separate seller-support knowledge base, that cross-surface unification is the reason to buy. One engine ranks listings on the storefront, answers in the buyer portal, and policy lookups for sellers, with user signals flowing across all three. For a team whose actual job is marketplace listing search and nothing else, Coveo is a lot of platform - and a lot of sales cycle - for one job.

**Best for:** Marketplaces unifying AI relevance across their commerce site, buyer-support portal, and seller-support knowledge base under one engine.

**Pricing:** Around $600/month base per third-party reports; real all-in deployments commonly run $100K+ per year once annual licensing (around $50K+), implementation (around $20K+), and professional services ($200-$300/hour) are added. No public price list - quoted through sales.

**Pros:**
- Machine-learning relevance that spans commerce, support, and workplace search on one engine
- Cross-surface personalization driven by unified user signals across every touchpoint, useful when buyer search and seller-portal search share infrastructure
- Mature analytics and reporting tooling built for enterprise governance and audit needs

**Cons:**
- Sales-led 6-9 month rollouts; professional services fees routinely double the license bill in year one before the engine is live
- Overkill when standalone marketplace listing search is the actual job
- Not built for Shopify-native workflows or smaller engineering teams

**Verdict:** Pick Coveo when unifying search across the marketplace site, a buyer-support portal, and a seller knowledge base at enterprise scale; skip it when standalone listing search is the actual job and you do not have the budget or runway for an enterprise services engagement.

## 4. Hawk Search

[Hawk Search](https://hawksearch.com) is enterprise site search built for catalogs with hundreds of attributes per listing, where faceted navigation - category, brand, dimension, fitment - is the dominant way buyers find what they want. The product has a mature footprint with B2B sellers and native integrations into BigCommerce and Optimizely's B2B and B2C platforms, where most of its reference customers live. For a marketplace whose buyers refine by filter rather than free-text search, and whose sellers list against deep attribute schemas, Hawk Search has spent years solving exactly that problem. Implementation is sales-led and services-heavy, so the gap between contract and live search reads more like a quarter than a sprint.

**Best for:** Marketplaces and large-catalog B2B sellers whose buyers discover primarily through filters across hundreds of attributes, not free-text search.

**Pricing:** Enterprise tiered, quoted per catalog size and contract scope through a sales-led process. Public starter pricing is not published.

**Pros:**
- Mature faceted navigation built for catalogs with hundreds of attributes per listing
- Strong fit for B2B marketplaces and large-catalog sellers where filter-driven refinement is the dominant buyer behavior
- Configurable merchandiser controls for category and refinement-level ranking rules
- Native integrations with BigCommerce and Optimizely B2B / B2C, plus support for Shopware and Adobe Commerce

**Cons:**
- Significant implementation overhead - rollouts run in months, not days
- No native Shopify connector, so Shopify teams pay for a custom integration build before search is live
- Pricing is not publicly published, so budget planning requires a sales conversation up front

**Verdict:** Pick Hawk Search when filter-driven discovery across hundreds of attributes on a B2B or large-catalog marketplace is the actual job and you have the implementation runway; skip it when free-text discovery or fast time-to-live on Shopify is the priority.

## Frequently asked questions

**How long does each platform take to go live on a marketplace catalog?** Time-to-live varies by an order of magnitude. Nobi is a quick site-side install - paste in a snippet, drop a placeholder where the search bar should appear, and you're live in hours. Algolia is fast on the indexing side but the storefront UX, ranking logic, and merchandising rules are yours to build, so a real production rollout typically runs weeks to a few months depending on engineering bandwidth. Coveo and Hawk Search are sales-led implementations that land in quarters, not weeks, with services budgets that often match first-year license.

**What does AI site search add to a marketplace beyond keyword matching?** [AI site search](https://nobi.ai/blog/the-top-ecommerce-platforms-for-native-ai-search-and-product-discovery) ranks results from the catalog and shopper behavior instead of from hand-written rules, so a query like "waterproof hiking boots size 10" surfaces the right listings without a merchandiser pinning each one by hand. On marketplaces with millions of listings from third-party sellers, that automatic ranking is the difference between a relevant results page and a wall of near-matches the buyer has to filter through. Nobi pairs that AI ranking with a buyer-facing assistant that answers questions about purchase protection, returns, and listing policies from connected content, with inline citation pills on every answer.

**Which platforms support Shopify natively?** Algolia has a native Shopify connector. Nobi goes live on Shopify through a snippet install - no custom integration build needed. Coveo and Hawk Search are not built for Shopify-native workflows; both require a custom integration build before search is live on Shopify.
