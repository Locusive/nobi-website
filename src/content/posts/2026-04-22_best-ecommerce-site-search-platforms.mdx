## What should a head of ecommerce actually evaluate in a site search platform?

Evaluate four things: relevance quality (does search surface the right products without constant rule-tuning?), total cost of ownership (licensing plus engineering time), merchandising control (who tunes results and how), and time to measurable conversion lift. Shoppers who use search convert at 2–3x the site average, so the platform you pick directly affects revenue, not just UX.

On relevance, the real question is whether the engine learns from shopper behavior or whether your team is forever writing synonym rules and redirects. Keyword matching alone will miss intent; semantic or behavior-trained ranking catches it.

Cost splits into three very different risk profiles. Flat fees are predictable but often padded. Query-based pricing rewards efficient sites and punishes spiky traffic. Revenue-share aligns the vendor with your growth but can get expensive fast once conversion lifts.

Implementation ranges from days (app-store installs) to weeks (mid-market tools) to months (enterprise deployments with custom indexing).

Merchandising matters too: can a merchandiser pin, boost, and drop banners without filing a dev ticket?

Finally, proof. Ask every vendor for a case study with CVR and revenue-per-search numbers, not traffic.

## Which site search platforms are worth considering in 2026?

The realistic shortlist for mid-market and enterprise ecommerce breaks into three groups. AI-native platforms that self-tune from behavior data: Nobi, Constructor, and Klevu. Developer-first infrastructure where you own the frontend and the relevance logic: Algolia. And enterprise suites that bundle search with personalization or a CDP: Bloomreach and Coveo. Shopify-focused apps round out the mid-market and SMB tiers, with Searchspring, Fast Simon, and Findify on the higher end and Boost Commerce and Doofinder on the lighter side.

The AI-native tier is where ranking improves as shoppers click, scroll, and buy, instead of from a merchandiser editing pinning rules every week. Nobi sits here, alongside Constructor (which positions itself as AI-first and connects to catalog plus real shopper behavior) and Klevu (Shopify-native, AI search with merchandising sold as paid add-ons). Constructor uses a revenue-share model, which scales with GMV and can produce surprise costs at higher volume. Klevu doesn't publish full pricing publicly.

Algolia is the developer's pick. It runs on a usage-based model (a free tier, then Grow at $0.50 per additional 1K search requests, Grow Plus at $1.75 per 1K, and a custom Improve tier), and it gives you maximum flexibility if your team wants to build the search UI and tune relevance themselves.

Bloomreach and Coveo are enterprise suites. Both bundle search into broader commerce or knowledge platforms, both use custom modular pricing, and both expect a longer sales cycle and implementation. Third-party reporting puts Coveo's commerce plans roughly in the $600 to $1,320+ per month range before custom enterprise tiers, with annual licenses often $50K and up.

On Shopify, Searchspring, Fast Simon, and Findify combine search with merchandising tooling for growth-stage brands, with Findify publishing tiers from $499 to $1,399 per month. Boost Commerce ($29 to $399 per month, scaled by GMV) and Doofinder cover the SMB end at lower price points, with lighter AI and a more keyword-driven core.

## How does each platform price site search, and where does the cost actually land?

Pricing models split into four shapes, and the shape matters more than the sticker price. Flat monthly fees, query-based metering, enterprise annual contracts, and revenue-share. Model a year of your actual traffic and GMV against each before you sign anything.

Flat-fee is the cleanest to forecast. [Nobi starts at $25/month](https://nobi.ai/pricing) including 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages, with $0.01 per additional search and $0.10 per additional message. That's predictable for a small or mid-sized catalog, but a brand doing 50,000 searches a month will pay closer to $500 once overages are counted, so do the math on your actual query volume. Boost Commerce starts at $29/month on the Launch plan (up to $50K GMV), moving to $239/month for Convert and $399/month for Accelerate, with a $2 overage per additional $1K in exceeded GMV. Fast Simon and Klevu don't publish headline pricing publicly on these pages, but both sit in the Shopify app tier range and scale by usage and features.

Query-based pricing is where high-traffic, low-AOV catalogs get punished. Algolia's Grow plan charges $0.50 per additional 1,000 search requests, Grow Plus charges $1.75 per 1,000. A brand with heavy search traffic but thin margins ends up paying for browsing, not buying.

Enterprise contracts come with a different shape entirely. Coveo's published tiers start at $600/month for Base and $1,320/month for Pro, but real enterprise deployments report annual licensing north of $50K plus $20K+ implementation and $200 to $300/hour for services. Bloomreach uses a customized module-plus-usage fee, also in the enterprise annual range.

Constructor uses revenue-share, which sounds aligned until your GMV grows. A $20M brand at 0.5 to 1% of attributed revenue is looking at $100K to $200K a year, uncapped. Growth is the thing that makes this model expensive.

Searchspring lands in ecommerce subscription range without publishing exact figures, priced per feature and catalog scale through sales.

The takeaway: pick a model that matches how your business scales. If queries grow faster than revenue, avoid query-based. If revenue grows faster than search cost, avoid revenue-share. If you want the bill to stay boring, flat-fee with transparent overages is the safest read.

## How much manual relevance tuning does each platform actually require?

Algolia, Searchspring, and most Shopify-native apps like Boost Commerce, Findify, and Doofinder expect merchandisers or developers to do the tuning work: write synonym lists, pin products for key queries, boost or bury SKUs, fix zero-result searches every week. AI-native platforms, Nobi, Constructor, and Klevu, learn from click and conversion signals and re-rank on their own. The practical difference shows up in where your merchandising team spends its Tuesday afternoons.

On a rule-based platform, the workflow is repetitive. A shopper searches "brown boots," gets zero results because the catalog says "Cappuccino." A merchandiser notices in the zero-result report, writes a synonym, pushes it live. Next week, a different editorial color name creates the same problem. Algolia gives you deep control over this, which is genuinely useful if you have a dev team that wants to own ranking logic. Searchspring wraps it in a merchandiser-friendly UI. But someone is still writing rules.

AI-native tools shift that time toward curation. Ranking adapts from what shoppers click and buy, so merchandisers can spend their hours on campaign moments and collection strategy rather than plumbing. Semantic understanding is where this gets concrete: Kilte, a DTC fashion brand whose catalog uses editorial color names like Cappuccino, Ivory, and Écru, saw a 21.7% conversion rate lift in an A/B test against Shopify's default search after switching to Nobi. [Nobi's color-normalization](https://nobi.ai/why-nobi/better-search) mapped "brown" to the Cappuccino Boot without anyone writing a synonym rule, the kind of gap any brand with evocative naming runs into constantly.

Two honest caveats. Self-tuning isn't instant, budget a two to four week learning window on any AI-native tool while it accumulates enough behavioral signal. And if your team actually wants to own custom ranking logic through an API, Algolia is built for that in a way Nobi isn't.

## What kind of conversion lift is realistic when you switch site search?

Published A/B tests from growth-stage brands cluster in the 10 to 25% CVR lift range when moving from keyword-based or default search to an AI-native tool. Anything well above that band is usually benchmarking against a broken starting point or overstating the result. The honest answer to a head of ecommerce asking "what will switching actually do for my numbers?" lives inside that band, and the numbers below are the ones worth anchoring against.

UNTUCKit ran Nobi in a two-month A/B test against the search tool they were already using. Conversion rate went from 15.0% to 17.6%, a 17.1% lift. Revenue per searcher moved from $32.30 to $39.17, up 21.3%. AOV nudged up 3.3%, from $215 to $222. After two months, UNTUCKit dropped the split and rolled Nobi to 100% of traffic. In a separate head-to-head, UNTUCKit also ran Nobi directly against Findify, their paid incumbent: 17.4% CVR on Nobi versus 16.5% on Findify. Close, but consistently in Nobi's favor.

Faherty measured 12.3% search CVR on Nobi versus 10.1% on a competing AI search tool, so the lift holds up even when the comparison is AI against AI, not AI against a stale keyword index. Kilte, a DTC fashion brand whose color names (Cappuccino, Écru, Midnight) didn't map to the words shoppers actually typed, saw a 21.7% CVR lift over Shopify default search. Lucchese reports $1M+ in incremental revenue in year one and a 39x ROI, though that figure covers Nobi's search plus a cart assistant and a PDP assistant, not search alone.

When you evaluate vendors, ask for CVR and revenue per searcher. Click-through and traffic are easy to move and hard to bank.

## When is a competitor a better pick than Nobi?

Nobi isn't the right call for every brand. A few scenarios where another platform wins:

**Choose Algolia if you have engineers and want to own the frontend.** Algolia is an API-first search platform, and teams building bespoke ranking logic or embedding search deep inside a custom storefront get more room to work with. Algolia's pricing scales with search requests (Grow at $0.50 per additional 1K searches, Grow Plus at $1.75), so the flexibility comes with usage costs, but if developer control matters more than out-of-box relevance, that's the right trade.

**Choose Bloomreach if you're enterprise and need search, CDP, and content personalization in one contract.** Bloomreach's pricing is custom and ties module fees to catalog size and event volume, which is heavy for a ecommerce brand but sensible if your procurement team already wants a single vendor across Experience Manager, CRM data, and commerce search.

**Choose Searchspring or Fast Simon if your merchandising team wants visual control over category and collection pages, not just search.** Both platforms run landing page and segmented merchandising campaigns across the site, which is exactly where Nobi has a real gap: Nobi curates the search results page, not site-wide category merchandising. For catalogs where the merchandiser's daily work is reordering collection grids, that matters.

**Choose Coveo if you need commerce search and internal or support search on the same platform.** Coveo unifies content across 30+ sources with ML-powered relevance, and third-party coverage puts pricing in the $600 to $1,320/month range for the lower tiers, with enterprise deals running $50K+ annually.

Two other Nobi limits worth naming honestly: it's web-chat only today (no SMS, WhatsApp, or voice), and the assistant helps shoppers decide but doesn't process returns or refunds. If post-purchase is the core job, pair with Gorgias.

## How long does implementation take on each platform?

Implementation falls into four rough buckets. Shopify app-store tools (Nobi, Boost Commerce, Fast Simon, Findify, Doofinder) install in hours to a few days. Mid-market platforms like Searchspring and Klevu typically take two to six weeks once you factor in merchandising setup. Algolia depends entirely on your engineering bandwidth, and a clean implementation with custom UI usually runs four to eight weeks. Bloomreach, Constructor, and Coveo run enterprise rollouts measured in months, often with required professional services engagements.

**Days.** Nobi, Boost Commerce, Doofinder, and most Shopify-native apps install from the app store, sync your catalog automatically, and start serving search the same day. Most teams are live with default relevance and a styled search results page inside a week. Faster implementation than enterprise alternatives is one of the practical reasons brands pick Nobi over Algolia or Bloomreach when they want results this quarter, not next.

**Weeks.** Klevu, Searchspring, Fast Simon, and Findify install quickly, but the real work is merchandising configuration, synonym setup, and category curation. Plan two to six weeks before you'd call the rollout finished. Fast Simon notes its implementation pricing scales with how much help its team provides, which is a fair signal that "install" and "live" aren't the same milestone.

**Weeks to months.** Algolia is a developer platform, so the timeline tracks your engineering capacity. A custom-built UI with relevance tuning, faceting, and analytics wiring typically lands in four to eight weeks of focused engineering work.

**Months.** Bloomreach, Constructor, and Coveo run enterprise scoping, data integration, and services engagements that stretch into multiple months. Coveo specifically lists implementation fees in the $20k+ range with professional services billed hourly.

A six-month rollout is six months without the lift. Factor that into the ROI math.

## How do I actually pick between these platforms?

Shortlist by pricing model and engineering capacity first, then run a real A/B test on the two or three finalists. If you have no engineers to spare, rule out Algolia and the enterprise suites. If revenue-share pricing makes your CFO twitch, cut Constructor and Bloomreach. That usually leaves you with three or four contenders worth piloting.

Demand a 30-to-60-day head-to-head test against your incumbent. Any vendor confident in their results will agree. One that won't is telling you something. Measure CVR and revenue per searcher, not click-through rate, because clicks don't pay the bills. Track zero-result rate too, since that's where lost revenue hides.

UNTUCKit ran exactly this process. Nobi hit 17.6% CVR against the prior tool's 15.0% over a two-month split, a 17.1% lift, and they moved Nobi to 100% of traffic once the data was in. The work didn't stop at launch. UNTUCKit now reviews search trends, zero-result queries, and shopper intent in a standing weekly meeting, which is how the catalog gets sharper over time.

One last check before you sign: if you'll need developer-controlled display logic later, ask about the customization path. UNTUCKit's requests on that front shaped [Nobi's Hooks API](https://nobi.ai/custom-actions), now available to every customer. The platforms that listen to operators tend to be the ones still working two years in.
