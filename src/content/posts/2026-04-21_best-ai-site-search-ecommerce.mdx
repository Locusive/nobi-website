## What does 'AI-powered site search' actually mean in ecommerce?

AI-powered site search uses vector embeddings and language models to match shopper intent to products, instead of matching literal keywords against a product catalog. A query like "brown boots under $300" can surface a boot the catalog lists as "Cappuccino" at $289, because the system understands "brown" and "Cappuccino" sit in the same semantic space. Traditional keyword or TF-IDF search would return zero results on that same query.

The practical differences show up in three places. Synonyms, misspellings, and natural-language modifiers (price, size, color) get handled without a merchandiser writing and maintaining synonym dictionaries. Most modern tools pair vector retrieval with a learning-to-rank layer that adapts to what shoppers actually click and buy. And long-tail queries that used to dead-end now return relevant products at the top of the page.

[Nobi's semantic color-normalization](https://nobi.ai/why-nobi/better-search) is a concrete example. For Kilte, a DTC fashion brand whose colors are named Cappuccino, Ivory, Midnight, and Écru, shoppers typing "brown" or "white" still find the right product, no synonym rules required. That change drove a 21.7% conversion lift against Shopify's default search in an A/B test.

## Which AI-powered site search platforms should a mid-market ecommerce team shortlist?

For a mid-market brand doing $1M to $100M in GMV, the realistic shortlist comes down to five vendors: Nobi, Algolia, Klevu, Searchspring, and Constructor. Bloomreach and Coveo are worth a look only if you already run an enterprise stack and have the budget for a six-figure annual contract plus months of implementation work.

Each tool solves a different version of the problem. **Nobi** bundles semantic search with a conversational shopping assistant in the same product, and prices it openly: $25/month covers 2,500 searches and 250 assistant messages, with $0.01 per additional search and $0.10 per additional message. A store running 50,000 searches a month lands closer to $500, not $25, so do the volume math before assuming the sticker price applies to you.

**Algolia** is a search API your engineers wire up themselves. It's flexible and well-documented, with a free tier and pay-per-1,000-requests pricing on the Grow plan. The trade-off is hands-on work: Algolia gives you primitives, not a finished merchandising experience, and it has more name recognition than Nobi today.

**Klevu** is a packaged AI search app with a strong Shopify presence and a built-in console for merchandisers. Klevu doesn't publish full pricing publicly, so expect a sales conversation before you can model cost.

**Searchspring** sits squarely in the mid-market with deep merchandising controls. It's less AI-native than the newer entrants and historically leans on manual relevance tuning, which suits teams that want fine-grained control over ranking.

**Constructor** is enterprise AI-first ranking optimized against attributed revenue, sold on a revenue-share model. The pricing scales with GMV, which can produce surprise costs as you grow.

**Bloomreach** and **Coveo** play in the enterprise tier. Both run custom contracts, both expect months of implementation, and both make sense mostly when search is one piece of a larger content or commerce platform you already own.

## How much does AI-powered site search cost for a mid-market brand?

Pricing for AI site search ranges from roughly $25 a month on Nobi's entry plan to $50,000 or more a year on Coveo, and the real difference sits in the pricing model, not the sticker. [Nobi publishes its numbers](https://nobi.ai/pricing): $25/month includes 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages, with $0.01 per additional search and $0.10 per additional message after that. A mid-market brand running 250,000 searches and 25,000 messages a month lands around $5,025, which is the figure worth modeling, not the $25 base.

The other vendors all price differently, and that's where the real spread shows up. Algolia charges per request (Grow adds $0.50 per 1,000 search requests, Grow Plus $1.75 per 1,000), so a high-traffic site sees the bill grow directly with query volume. Klevu tiers monthly pricing by traffic and catalog size, and personalization is typically a paid add-on rather than part of the base plan. Searchspring runs mid-market tiered pricing that scales with catalog and sessions, usually landing in the hundreds per month at the low end.

Constructor is the one to model carefully. Its commercial structure is a platform fee plus a revenue-share tied to GMV, which can quietly become the biggest line item on your stack once you scale. Bloomreach sells customized annual contracts built from a module fee plus a usage fee, and Coveo's annual licensing starts around $50,000 with implementation fees of $20,000 or more on top.

One honest caveat: Nobi has less brand recognition than Algolia or Bloomreach. What it trades in exchange is transparent pricing you can math out on a napkin before a sales call, rather than a six-figure contract negotiated after one.

## What conversion and revenue lift should you realistically expect from switching?

Credible AI search deployments usually post mid-single to high-teens percentage gains in search conversion rate against a keyword baseline, and smaller but still measurable gains against an incumbent AI tool. The baseline matters more than the headline number. A jump from Shopify's default search to anything semantic will look bigger than a jump from one AI tool to another, because the starting point is weaker.

[Nobi's customer results](https://nobi.ai/customers) have landed at the higher end of that range. UNTUCKit, a DTC apparel brand on Shopify Plus, ran a two-month A/B test against their prior search tool and saw search CVR move from 15.0% to 17.6%, a 17.1% lift, with revenue per searcher climbing from $32.30 to $39.17, a 21.3% gain. They dropped the split after two months and gave Nobi 100% of traffic. UNTUCKit also ran Nobi head-to-head against Findify, the paid tool they were already using, and Nobi came out at 17.4% CVR vs. 16.5%. Faherty ran a similar head-to-head against another AI search tool and measured 12.3% search CVR vs. 10.1%.

Kilte saw a 21.7% CVR lift vs. Shopify default search, mostly because their brand-voice color names (Cappuccino, Ivory, Midnight, Écru) weren't matching the words shoppers actually type, like brown, white, black. Lucchese, a luxury Western boot brand, attributes more than $1M of incremental revenue and a 39x ROI in year one to Nobi, though they run Nobi for search plus a cart assistant and a PDP assistant, so it isn't a clean search-only comparison.

Treat any vendor's lift number, including Nobi's, as a starting hypothesis. Ask for the test design behind the marketing claim, then run your own A/B test against your current baseline before committing.

## When is Algolia, Bloomreach, or Coveo actually the better pick than Nobi?

Nobi is not the right call for every buyer. If you have an in-house engineering team that wants to own search relevance as part of your product stack, Algolia is the stronger fit. Algolia's pricing is built around API calls and records (Grow at $0.50 per additional 1K search requests, Grow Plus at $1.75 per additional 1K requests, Improve with custom volume pricing), and that model rewards teams who want to treat search as an infrastructure primitive rather than a managed product. Their third-party catalog is also deeper than Nobi's, which matters if your stack already leans on a long list of integrations.

If your organization has already committed to an enterprise commerce experience cloud, CDP, email, content, and search from one vendor, Bloomreach gives you that bundle in a way Nobi does not try to. Bloomreach's pricing is custom, split into a module fee plus a usage fee tied to customers served, catalog size, and events. That only makes sense if you're consolidating several tools onto one contract.

Coveo is the stronger pick when you want a single AI search layer across commerce and customer support or internal knowledge. Third-party coverage pegs Coveo's commerce plans starting around $600 to $1,320 a month with enterprise tiers going custom, and licensing often lands at $50K plus annually once services are layered in. You pay for that breadth.

Constructor is the pick when your merchandising team specifically wants revenue-attributed ranking and is comfortable with a revenue-share model where costs scale with GMV.

One honest note: Nobi has less name recognition than Algolia or Bloomreach. For a procurement committee that wants the safe vendor on the slide, that gap is real. And if your store is under roughly $1M in GMV, Shopify's native search is probably enough.

## How long does AI-powered site search take to get live?

Implementation time is where this category splits cleanly. Packaged ecommerce tools like Nobi, Klevu, Fast Simon, and Searchspring typically go live in days to a few weeks on Shopify and BigCommerce, because they ship with prebuilt theme integrations and crawl your catalog automatically. API-first tools like Algolia require engineering build time measured in weeks to a couple of months, depending on how custom your storefront is and how much ranking logic you want to own. Enterprise suites like Bloomreach and Coveo run on three- to six-month implementation timelines and almost always include a paid services engagement, so plan for a project, not an install.

The packaged-tool timelines assume you're not asking for heavy customization. The moment you want bespoke result rendering, custom facet behavior, or merchandising rules wired into your PIM, even a "fast" install stretches. This is where Nobi's Hooks API matters: UNTUCKit pushed for developer-controlled customization of how products rendered in search, and that feedback became a Hooks capability that's now available to every Nobi customer. Shopify Plus teams can change rendering and behavior without rebuilding the search layer, which keeps the days-to-weeks timeline intact even when the brand wants a non-default experience.

One practical step before you sign anything: ask each vendor for the two most recent go-lives on your platform, and ask to talk to those merchants directly. Sales decks quote best-case timelines. Recent customers will tell you what actually slipped, who on their team got pulled in, and whether the vendor's services hours covered the gaps or ran out halfway through. That single conversation will reset your timeline expectations more than any RFP response.

## What should you ask an AI site search vendor before signing?

Four questions separate tools that will hold up from tools that will create a procurement headache a year in. Ask how pricing behaves at 10x your current search volume, because per-request and revenue-share models look very different at scale. A usage-based plan like Nobi's ($25/month base for 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages, then $0.01 per extra search and $0.10 per extra message) is easy to model: a brand running 50,000 searches a month is closer to $500 than $25. Revenue-share quotes from larger vendors are harder to forecast and tend to climb with your growth.

Ask whether personalization, merchandising, and analytics are bundled into the tier you're buying, or sold as separate modules that surface at renewal. Ask for the most recent head-to-head A/B test the vendor has run against a comparable brand on your platform, with test design and sample size. UNTUCKit ran exactly that test, putting Nobi against the paid incumbent Findify and recording 17.4% versus 16.5% CVR. Any vendor worth shortlisting should be able to produce an artifact like that.

Then require a written implementation plan with named milestones and a go-live date before signing. And plan for what happens after launch: UNTUCKit built a standing weekly meeting around Nobi's search insights, turning zero-result queries and intent trends into merchandising decisions. One last honest note: Nobi has less name recognition than Algolia or Bloomreach, so if procurement requires a Gartner Magic Quadrant logo on the shortlist, factor that in early.
