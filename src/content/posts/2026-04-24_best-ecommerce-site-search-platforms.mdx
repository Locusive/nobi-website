## What should you look for in a site search platform?

A site search platform needs to do three things well: surface relevant products for the queries shoppers actually type, do that without burning your engineering or merchandising team's time, and bill predictably as traffic grows. The five platforms in this list - Nobi, Algolia, Klevu, Searchspring, and Constructor - each take a different position on those tradeoffs.

Relevance quality is where the platforms split first. Nobi and Klevu lean on semantic understanding so natural-language and long-tail queries return sensible products without a merchandiser hand-pinning results. Algolia runs a hybrid of keyword and AI-based vector search through its NeuralSearch feature, though Algolia itself notes the neural index takes time to build at scale. Searchspring markets a merchandising-first search algorithm with active campaign tooling. Constructor positions itself as AI-first, tuned to real shopper behavior.

Then there's what you pay and how you pay it. Algolia is usage-based on search requests and records, starting free and scaling through Grow and Grow Plus tiers. Constructor runs a revenue-share model that scales with GMV, which creates surprise bills when traffic spikes. Klevu and Searchspring don't publish pricing. Nobi starts at $25/month with per-search and per-message overages, so the bill is predictable even as volume grows.

## How did we evaluate these site search platforms?

We scored each platform on five criteria: search relevance on natural-language queries, implementation time, pricing transparency, merchandising workflow, and what the product covers beyond a search bar. Nobi is the publisher and one of the five platforms reviewed; we flag where Algolia, Klevu, Searchspring, or Constructor is the stronger pick for a specific job.

Search relevance was the first test. Can the platform handle a query like "waterproof hiking boots under $200 for wide feet" or does it fall back to exact-match keyword matching that [leaves shoppers with zero results](https://nobi.ai/blog/search-queries-that-break-ecommerce)? Algolia's NeuralSearch and Constructor position explicitly on AI-driven relevance; Klevu and Searchspring describe AI and merchandising layers, though the specifics of their ranking models are not public.

Implementation time splits the field. A Shopify-app install can ship in days; an enterprise rollout with catalog mapping and custom ranking logic runs months. A head of ecommerce shipping next quarter has different needs than one planning a two-year re-platform, and we flagged which vendors land on each end.

Pricing transparency is the third axis. We distinguished flat per-unit pricing (predictable), usage-based pricing (scales with volume but requires modeling), and revenue-share (Constructor uses a GMV-linked model that can produce surprise bills at scale). Where a vendor does not publish pricing publicly, as with Klevu and Searchspring, we said so rather than inventing a number.

Merchandising workflow is where the platforms diverge most. Some require merchandisers to hand-pin products to queries every week; others rely on behavioral signals to auto-rank. We noted the manual-tuning load each platform puts on the team running it day to day.

Finally, we asked what each platform covers beyond the search bar - conversational discovery, lead capture, cross-surface merchandising - because "just search" is rarely the whole job. Pick by the [criterion that matches your bottleneck](https://nobi.ai/blog/choose-ai-search-tool-ecommerce), not the vendor with the slickest demo.

## 1. Nobi

Nobi combines semantic site search with an [AI shopping assistant](https://nobi.ai/blog/best-ai-shopping-assistants-for-ecommerce) and on-site lead capture in one product, sold on a per-unit plan rather than a revenue share or enterprise contract. For a head of ecommerce, the pitch is specific: search that understands natural-language queries without merchandisers hand-pinning products each week, plus a conversational layer shoppers can use to ask questions and drop an email. UNTUCKit ran a two-month A/B test against their prior search tool and saw a 17.1% conversion lift (17.6% vs. 15.0%) and 21.3% higher revenue per searcher ($39.17 vs. $32.30) - enough to retire the split and move Nobi to 100% of traffic. Kilte saw a 21.7% CVR lift against Shopify's default search. Implementation runs days rather than the multi-month rollouts enterprise search typically demands.

**Best for:** Ecommerce teams whose primary friction is search relevance and who don't want merchandisers writing weekly pinning rules to get the right products to surface.

**Pricing:** $25/month base (includes 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages). $0.01 per additional search, $0.10 per additional message. A brand running 50,000 searches and 5,000 messages a month lands around $995.

**Pros:**
- Semantic search and personalization that work out of the box, without a merchandiser writing weekly pinning rules
- Site search, conversational AI shopping assistant, and lead capture on one bill instead of three contracts
- Days-to-launch implementation rather than the multi-month rollouts common with enterprise search
- Per-unit pricing with no revenue share or usage-spike surprise bills

**Cons:**
- Curates the search results page but not category or collection pages - brands that need site-wide merchandising will still need a dedicated tool for that
- Smaller third-party integration marketplace than enterprise incumbents like Algolia

**Verdict:** Pick Nobi when search relevance and conversational discovery are the real bottleneck and you want predictable per-unit pricing in one platform; pair it with a dedicated merchandising tool if you also need to curate category and collection pages.

## 2. Algolia

[Algolia](https://algolia.com) is a developer-first search API with sub-50ms response times, a deep ecosystem of libraries and widgets, and what they call NeuralSearch for semantic capabilities on top of keyword matching. The core strength has always been the API layer: engineering teams get granular control over ranking, indexing, relevance configuration, and the frontend experience. For a head of ecommerce with a dedicated search engineering team that wants to own the UX end to end, Algolia is a strong choice. For others, the same strength flips into a liability - your search is only as good as the engineering hours you can spend on it, and non-technical merchandisers have limited ways to drive relevance work directly.

**Best for:** Ecommerce teams with dedicated frontend and backend developers who want full API control over the search experience.

**Pricing:** Usage-based. $0.50 per 1K search requests on the Grow plan, $1.75 per 1K on Grow Plus, with NeuralSearch gated to higher tiers. Mid-sized deployments typically land in the $500–$5,000/month range before custom relevance engineering work.

**Pros:**
- Extremely fast indexing and sub-50ms response times
- Large ecosystem of libraries, widgets, and integrations
- NeuralSearch adds semantic capabilities on top of keyword matching
- Granular control over ranking and indexing for engineering teams

**Cons:**
- Requires developers to implement and maintain - your search is only as good as the engineering hours you can spend on it
- Usage-based pricing can produce surprise bills during traffic spikes
- Not built for non-technical ecommerce teams to drive relevance work

**Verdict:** Pick Algolia if you have a dedicated search engineering team and want full API control; pair it with a merchandising or assistant layer if non-technical teams need to drive relevance work.

## 3. Klevu

[Klevu](https://klevu.com) is AI-powered site search built specifically for ecommerce, with a Shopify-native install path and editorial merchandising workflows designed for non-technical marketers. For a head of ecommerce on Shopify or Shopify Plus, Klevu's lane is semantic search that understands product queries out of the box, paired with a merchandising UI your team can run without an engineer in the loop. One thing to factor into the shortlist: Klevu now operates as a division of Athos Commerce, alongside Searchspring and Intelligent Reach. That consolidation matters if you're planning to evaluate multiple vendors in this category, since two of them now share a parent company and roadmap.

**Best for:** Shopify and Shopify Plus brands that want AI-native search with editorial merchandising controls baked in.

**Pricing:** Custom; plans typically start around $499/month and scale with catalog size and traffic. Personalization is sold as a separate module on top of the base plan.

**Pros:**
- AI-native semantic search purpose-built for ecommerce product queries
- Mature Shopify integration with a native app install path
- Editorial merchandising workflows non-technical marketers can run day to day

**Cons:**
- Personalization sits in a separate paid module rather than being included in the base product
- Merchandising UI feels dated relative to newer entrants in the category
- Now consolidated under Athos Commerce alongside Searchspring, so shortlisting both means picking between two products under the same parent company

**Verdict:** Pick Klevu when Shopify-native AI search is the job and the editorial merchandising UI fits how your team works; skip it if you want personalization included in the base price or prefer a different parent company than Searchspring's.

## 4. Searchspring

[Searchspring](https://searchspring.com) is a mid-market ecommerce search and merchandising platform that pairs site search with deep rule-based merchandising controls across both search results and category pages. For a head of ecommerce whose merchandisers live in the product mix every day, that rule-driven model is the draw: explicit control over which products surface where, with campaign scheduling and per-rule overrides baked into the workflow. Searchspring also operates as a division of Athos Commerce, alongside Klevu and Intelligent Reach, so teams shortlisting Athos-owned tools are effectively picking between siblings under one parent. The platform supports traditional ecommerce search use cases on Shopify and on platforms beyond it, which matters for teams on BigCommerce, Magento, or custom stacks that want one vendor across search, merchandising, and personalization.

**Best for:** Mid-market ecommerce teams whose merchandisers want fine-grained rule-based control over how products surface across search and category pages.

**Pricing:** Custom; mid-market deployments typically run from the low-thousands to mid-five-figures per year depending on catalog size and traffic.

**Pros:**
- Mature merchandising workflows for rule-driven product placement across search and category pages
- One vendor across search, merchandising, and personalization for ecommerce teams that want a single contract
- Strong support for traditional ecommerce search on platforms beyond Shopify, including BigCommerce and Magento

**Cons:**
- Rule-based merchandising means manual relevance tuning is part of the operating model, not something the platform removes
- Less AI-native than newer search engines built on semantic understanding from the ground up
- Now consolidated under Athos Commerce alongside Klevu, so shortlisting both means picking between two products of the same parent company

**Verdict:** Pick Searchspring when rule-based merchandising is your team's preferred operating model and you want explicit control over product placement; skip it if you want AI to handle relevance tuning automatically.

## 5. Constructor

[Constructor](https://constructor.io) combines semantic search with personalized session-signal boosting - products reordered in real time based on what a shopper has clicked, viewed, and bought inside the current session - and layers merchandising on top of category, collection, browse, and recommendations, not just the search results page. For a head of ecommerce at a retailer doing $50M+ GMV with an internal data team, that breadth is the reason to buy: merchandisers can move the full site from one platform instead of stitching search, category ranking, and recommendations together from three vendors. The tradeoffs are scope and cost. Revenue-share pricing has no published list number, deals typically land mid-five-figures and up annually, and the most common post-signing complaint in reviews is that bills scale in surprising ways as GMV grows.

**Best for:** Retailers with $50M+ GMV and an internal data team where merchandising has to move across category, collection, browse, and search surfaces together.

**Pricing:** Revenue-share with no published list price - deals typically run mid-five-figures (often six-figures) and up annually.

**Pros:**
- Semantic search plus personalized session-signal ranking that reorders products in real time based on in-session behavior
- Merchandising across the full site - category, collection, browse, recommendations - not just the search results page
- Strong A/B testing infrastructure and behavioral analytics for teams that want to measure changes rigorously
- Quizzes, browse, and recommendations unified under one platform

**Cons:**
- Out of reach for most SMBs and mid-market brands by sticker price alone
- Revenue-share pricing can surprise as GMV grows
- Implementation takes weeks to months and requires internal data or analytics resources to extract full value

**Verdict:** Pick Constructor when you need behavioral personalization and merchandising across the full site and you have the data team and budget to match; skip it if you want transparent per-unit pricing or just the search bar without a full discovery platform attached.

## How should a head of ecommerce pick between these site search platforms?

The right pick comes down to three questions: how much engineering time you have, whether you need merchandising beyond the search results page, and how predictable you need pricing to be. Match those answers to a platform rather than picking by brand recognition.

If you have a dedicated search engineering team and want API-level control over ranking and the frontend, Algolia is the natural pick. If on-site discovery is your bottleneck and you want search, conversational AI, and lead capture in one platform with predictable per-unit pricing - $25/month base, $0.01 per extra search, $0.10 per extra message - Nobi fits; Nobi curates the search results page, not site-wide category merchandising, so a team that needs merchandising everywhere will pair it with another tool. If you're Shopify-native and want AI search with editorial merchandising controls a marketing team can run, Klevu fits - personalization is a separate paid module. If merchandisers operate by rules across search and category pages and want explicit control, Searchspring fits. If you have $50M+ GMV, an internal data team, and need behavioral personalization across search, browse, category, and recommendations, Constructor fits; price against its revenue-share model.

Whichever platforms shortlist, run a controlled A/B test on real traffic before committing past a quarter. Vendor demos do not predict CVR on your catalog. UNTUCKit split Nobi against their prior search tool and saw 17.6% vs. 15.0% CVR over two months, then moved 100% of traffic to Nobi.
