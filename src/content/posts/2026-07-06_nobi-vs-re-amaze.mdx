Support tickets pile up two different ways for an ecommerce team. Some come from shoppers who never got an answer to a question and are now writing in frustrated instead of buying. Others come from shoppers who already bought and now want to know where their order is, or whether they can return it. Get the first kind wrong and you lose a sale that never had to be lost. Get the second kind wrong and a support team drowns in tickets it can't clear fast enough. Nobi and Re:amaze are built for opposite ends of that split:

## What's the core difference between Nobi and Re:amaze?

Nobi is a conversational website assistant that combines product search, automated shopper Q&A, and lead generation directly on your storefront, answering questions before a shopper ever reaches out for help. Re:amaze is an ecommerce-native helpdesk that pulls email, live chat, social, and SMS into one agent inbox with Shopify order data already attached, so your team handles every incoming conversation from one place.

That split shows up in how each product uses AI. Re:amaze Brain drafts a reply for a human agent to review and send. It's help for the agent, not an answer for the shopper. [Nobi answers the shopper directly](https://nobi.ai/blog/nobi-vs-intercom), in the moment, on the site, with no agent in the loop unless the question falls outside what it can ground in your connected content.

It also means Nobi doesn't try to be an inbox. There's no email, social, or SMS channel here, just web chat and search on your site. If your team needs one place to manage every channel a shopper might use to reach out, that's Re:amaze's job, not Nobi's.

## How do Nobi and Re:amaze compare on pricing?

Nobi starts at $25/month with 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages included, then $0.01 per extra search and $0.10 per extra message. That's one flat platform fee no matter how many people are on your team. Re:amaze prices by agent seat: $29/month for Basic up to $69/month for Plus, or a $59/month flat Starter plan capped at 500 conversations, plus metered AI resolutions at $0.85 each above the 5 to 20 included per plan.

Nobi's cost tracks how many shoppers show up and how many questions they ask, whether that traffic is handled by one person or ten. A store adding headcount to its support team doesn't pay Nobi any more for it. Re:amaze works the other way: cost tracks how many agents need a seat in the inbox to answer email, chat, social, and SMS. A store running a lean team pays less on Re:amaze regardless of how much site traffic it gets, because seats, not visits, set the price.

That difference matters most for a store with a small support team and a lot of site traffic. Nobi's bill on that mix stays anchored to search and message volume, so a two-person support team fielding a large volume of shopper questions on the site doesn't need to add seats to keep up. Re:amaze's bill in that same scenario stays anchored to the two seats, which is cheap on the per-seat side. But every one of those questions still has to reach an agent's inbox to get answered. Re:amaze doesn't have a storefront-side assistant to field them first. A store with the opposite mix, a bigger support team and lighter site traffic, will find Re:amaze's per-seat model the more predictable of the two.

## How do Nobi and Re:amaze handle WISMO and post-purchase questions?

What each tool is actually built to answer looks different, starting with the question shoppers ask most after checkout: where's my order. Re:amaze's Order Bot, available on the Pro and Plus plans, looks up that answer directly against connected Shopify order data, with a human agent as the backstop for anything it can't resolve. Nobi answers post-purchase questions from your connected content, like return windows and warranty terms, but it doesn't look up one shopper's specific order or take an action like a cancellation, return, or refund inside the chat.

This matters because a bot that guesses at an order status instead of checking a real record is the fastest way to lose a customer's trust. Re:amaze avoids that risk by design. The Order Bot is grounded in actual Shopify order data, so when it tells a shopper their package shipped Tuesday, it's reading that from the order itself, not inferring it.

Nobi handles a different, narrower kind of post-purchase question, and it has its own guardrails against guessing. A merchant can use query overrides to lock an exact, pre-approved answer to a high-stakes question like "what's your return policy," so the response never drifts or gets paraphrased by the model. And every draft answer can run through an optional second AI review before it reaches the shopper: a second model reads the cited source material and checks the draft against it, catching the kind of made-up detail that erodes trust fastest.

## Which one handles pre-purchase product questions and on-site search?

Nobi is built for this. A shopper asking about materials, dimensions, fit, or compatibility gets a grounded answer with inline numbered citations pointing back to the exact source, and the same storefront gets [instant semantic product search](https://nobi.ai/blog/nobi-vs-algolia) alongside it. Re:amaze has no product search or discovery layer at all. It's a support inbox, built for the conversations that happen after a shopper already reached out, not a shopping surface a browsing visitor uses to find or evaluate a product.

That gap matters most on sizing and fit questions. Fit issues are consistently the leading driver of apparel returns, so a shopper who asks "will this run small" or "is this true to size" and gets a real answer before checkout is a return that never happens. Nobi pulls that answer from product pages, FAQs, and PDFs already on the site, refreshed twice a day, so a sizing chart update or a new fit note shows up in the assistant's answers within hours instead of going stale for weeks. Re:amaze simply has no answer to give here. There's no product knowledge base, no search index, nothing on the storefront side for a browsing shopper to ask.

The same grounding lets Nobi steer a shopper toward an exchange over a refund at the exact moment the sizing question comes up, before a return ever gets filed. A shopper unsure about fit can be pointed to a different size or style right then, on the product page, instead of ordering, receiving the wrong fit, and starting a return days later. Re:amaze picks up that conversation only after the return request already exists, as a ticket to route and resolve, not a purchase decision to influence. For a store trying to reduce returns rather than just process them faster, that's the difference between preventing the problem and cleaning it up.

## When is Re:amaze the better choice than Nobi?

Re:amaze is the right pick when the real bottleneck isn't the product-discovery gap the last section covered, but the volume of conversations arriving after a purchase decision is already made. If your team is juggling email, live chat, social, and SMS as separate inboxes, and none of them show the Shopify order tied to the message, that's the problem Re:amaze solves. It pulls every channel into one agent inbox with order data already attached, so a support rep isn't tabbing between four tools to answer one question.

Re:amaze also fits teams that want a person reviewing every reply before it reaches a customer. Re:amaze Brain drafts a response and hands it to an agent to approve or edit, so the AI never sends anything on its own.

Setup speed favors Re:amaze here too. A Shopify store can have the Re:amaze widget live within a day, with order data already connected, since the two systems are built to plug into each other. That's a real advantage for a team that needs an inbox running immediately and doesn't need a storefront-facing search or Q&A layer to go with it.

If your support load runs across four channels and your team wants to stay in the loop on every reply, Re:amaze is the better buy. If the problem is shoppers asking questions before they ever reach out, that's a different job, and it's the one Nobi is built for.

## When is Nobi the better choice than Re:amaze?

Nobi is the better fit when the goal is answering questions before they ever become a ticket, product search, pre-purchase Q&A, and lead capture on the storefront itself, rather than managing tickets that have already arrived. It's honest that Nobi isn't the right tool for a team whose real problem is unifying email, social, and SMS into one inbox; Re:amaze already does that well.

Pick Nobi when the number you're trying to move is bounce rate on high-intent pages, not ticket volume sitting in an inbox. A shopper who leaves a product page without buying never generates a ticket for Re:amaze to catch. Nobi's job is to answer that shopper's question and capture them as a lead before they bounce, which is a different problem than clearing a queue faster.

Nobi also fits a team that doesn't want to stitch three tools together to cover one on-site experience. Search, [automated shopper Q&A](https://nobi.ai/blog/nobi-vs-tidio), and capturing high-intent shoppers as leads ship as one product here, on one bill, rather than a search vendor, a chat vendor, and a separate tool for building customer profiles bolted onto the same page. For a marketing team without engineering resources to manage multiple integrations, that's the practical difference.

Where Nobi runs out of runway is anything that looks like ticket management. There's no case routing, no macros, no agent queue here. A team that needs structured ticket workflows should pair Nobi with a helpdesk, not replace one with it, and Re:amaze already does that well if that's the gap.

The same is true for channel coverage. Nobi answers questions in web chat and search on the storefront; it doesn't touch email or SMS. A team whose support volume mostly arrives by email still needs a helpdesk like Re:amaze to cover that channel.

## Frequently asked questions

**Does Nobi replace Re:amaze?**
No. Nobi answers questions before a shopper ever files a ticket, using search and grounded Q&A right on the storefront. Re:amaze exists for what still arrives after that: the tickets that come in over email, live chat, social, and SMS once a shopper decides they need to reach out. One handles the moment before contact, the other handles everything after.

**Can Nobi and Re:amaze run together?**
Yes. Nobi covers on-site search and product Q&A, and any conversation it can't fully answer routes into Re:amaze's inbox for a person to pick up. A store running both gets fewer incoming tickets in the first place, plus one organized place to handle the ones that still land.

**Does Nobi look up a shopper's order status?**
No. Nobi answers policy and product questions from the content a merchant connects, like return windows or sizing guidance, but it doesn't check a specific order. That's Re:amaze's Order Bot, which reads live Shopify order data directly.

**Is Re:amaze's AI fully autonomous?**
No. Re:amaze Brain drafts a reply for an agent to review and send. It speeds up the agent's side of the conversation, but a person still approves every message before it reaches the shopper.
