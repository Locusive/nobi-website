## What is AI deep search, and why is it different from regular site search for B2B distributors?

AI deep search interprets a query beyond literal keyword matches, using vector embeddings, learned synonyms, and query-intent recognition to map what a buyer typed to what they actually meant. For distributors with hundreds of thousands of SKUs, abbreviated part numbers, and catalogs full of technical jargon, that matters because buyers rarely type the canonical product name. They type a fragment of a part number, a competing manufacturer's term, or a raw spec value, and a keyword index that can't see "1/2-13 SS hex bolt" and "half-inch stainless 13 thread hex" as the same SKU [returns zero results](https://nobi.ai/blog/ai-search-tools-zero-results-ecommerce).

Nobi, Algolia, Coveo, and Hawk Search all close that gap; what differs is how much engineering and services budget each one demands. Nobi runs vector search out of the box on a usage-based plan from $25/month base, with no manual rule tuning required from merchandisers. Algolia's NeuralSearch blends keyword and vector retrieval but gates AI features behind higher pricing tiers and expects a developer team to wire it in. Coveo brings ML-tuned relevance with Base plans around $600/month and full deployments commonly clearing $100K/year all-in once licensing, implementation, and services stack up. Hawk Search targets distributors specifically, with customer-specific pricing visibility, faceted navigation, and integrations into Optimizely and Adobe Commerce.

## 2. Algolia

[Algolia](https://algolia.com) is a developer-first search API with sub-50ms response times, a NeuralSearch layer that adds vector matching on top of the keyword index, and granular control over ranking, tokenization, and synonym dictionaries. For a head of digital at a distributor with engineers on staff who want to design their own part-number tokenization and ranking signals, Algolia is built for exactly that. The trade-off is that the search quality you ship is the search quality your team builds: Algolia hands you the primitives, the indexing controls, and a deep API surface, then expects you to wire them into your site and tune them against your catalog. Distributors who want semantic matching working on day one without engineering investment will find the cheapest Algolia tier is still keyword-only.

**Best for:** Distribution teams with dedicated search engineers who want to own part-number tokenization, custom ranking rules, and the frontend search UX end-to-end.

**Pricing:** Usage-based. $0.50 per 1K search requests on Grow, $1.75 per 1K on Grow Plus, with NeuralSearch gated to higher tiers. Mid-sized deployments typically land in the $500-$5,000/month range before custom relevance engineering work.

**Pros:**
- Granular API control over ranking, tokenization, and synonym dictionaries, useful for non-standard part-number formats
- NeuralSearch adds semantic matching on top of the keyword index for query-intent fallback
- Large ecosystem of libraries and widgets to plug into custom B2B sites
- Sub-50ms response times that hold up at distributor catalog sizes

**Cons:**
- Requires developers to implement and maintain; search quality tracks the engineering hours you spend on it
- Usage-based pricing can produce surprise bills during traffic spikes or peak buying seasons
- NeuralSearch sits on higher tiers, so the cheapest Algolia setup is still keyword-only

**Verdict:** Pick Algolia if you have a search engineering team that wants full API control over part-number ranking and is happy owning that surface; skip it if your catalog team needs to drive relevance without filing engineering tickets.

## 3. Coveo

[Coveo](https://coveo.com) runs one ML relevance engine across commerce search, customer support, and internal knowledge - the same model ranks results on the public site, the dealer or rep portal, and the back-office knowledge bases your product team works in. For a head of digital at a distributor whose buyers, account managers, and support reps all hunt through different surfaces but the same catalog, that single-engine posture is the point. Cross-surface personalization carries signals from one context into another, so a rep searching internally benefits from what buyers click on the public site. The trade-off is the deployment shape: Coveo expects an integration team, a services budget, and a longer rollout than a Shopify-native distributor would tolerate for a standalone search project.

**Best for:** Distributors unifying AI search across the public site, the dealer or rep support portal, and internal product knowledge bases on a single relevance engine.

**Pricing:** Enterprise. Third-party references put Base plans around $600/month, but real all-in deployments commonly clear $100K/year once annual licensing (about $50K+), implementation (about $20K+), and professional services ($200-$300/hour) stack up.

**Pros:**
- One ML relevance engine spans commerce, support, and workplace search, so ranking signals travel across surfaces
- Cross-surface personalization carries buyer, dealer, and rep behavior into a single profile
- Mature analytics and tuning tooling sized for enterprise distribution catalogs

**Cons:**
- Long sales cycle and services-heavy implementation
- Overkill for a standalone site search problem
- Not built for Shopify-native or quick-rollout workflows

**Verdict:** Pick Coveo when you are unifying search across the public site, support, and internal surfaces at distribution scale; skip it when the job is standalone B2B site search and the implementation tail isn't worth the breadth.

Account-restricted catalogs, contract pricing visible inside search results, parametric filtering across deeply nested categories, and SKU-level access control all ship as native capabilities, not as custom engineering work layered on top of a generic search index. For a head of digital at a distributor whose buying flow depends on showing different accounts different SKUs at different prices, that's the right shape of tool. Hawk Search integrates natively with Optimizely B2B, Adobe Commerce, and ROC Commerce, which fits the stack most large industrial distributors are already running. The trade-off is deployment posture: this is a services-led rollout with a longer implementation tail, not a self-serve admin you stand up in a week.

**Best for:** Distributors whose buying flow depends on account-restricted catalogs, contract-specific pricing visible in search, and parametric filtering across hundreds of thousands of SKUs.

**Pricing:** Enterprise contracts quoted by catalog size, traffic, and account-pricing complexity; implementation services are typically part of the engagement. Public starting tier is not published.

**Pros:**

- Built for very large, deeply nested SKU hierarchies typical of industrial distributors
- Strong merchandising tools for catalog managers handling thousands of categories
- Native integrations with Optimizely B2B, Adobe Commerce, and ROC Commerce

**Cons:**
- Significant implementation overhead - services-led rollout rather than self-serve
- Less Shopify-native; pairs more naturally with custom or BigCommerce B2B stacks

**Verdict:** Pick Hawk Search when account-restricted catalogs and contract-pricing-aware search are core to your buying flow and the implementation cost is justified; skip it if you need a quick rollout or a self-serve admin experience for a smaller team.

## Frequently asked questions

**Does AI deep search replace your PIM?** No. None of these tools own your product data; they index against the catalog feed your PIM already publishes. Nobi, Algolia, Coveo, and Hawk Search all sit downstream of the PIM, reading the attributes, descriptions, and spec values your team maintains there. If your PIM is messy, search quality will track the mess - the tool surfaces what's in the feed.

**Can these tools handle account-specific catalogs?** Hawk Search supports account-restricted catalogs and contract pricing in results as native capabilities. Algolia and Nobi can model account-specific visibility through filter logic tied to the logged-in buyer, which works but is configuration the team has to build and maintain. Coveo handles it through its commerce module configuration. If account-restricted catalogs are core to the buying flow rather than an edge case, Hawk Search is the path of least resistance.

**How long does implementation take?** Nobi typically ships in days - a quick site-side install plus a catalog feed connection. Algolia runs from weeks to months depending on how much custom ranking work the engineering team takes on. Coveo and Hawk Search are services-led, multi-month engagements.

**Is semantic search worth it for a smaller catalog?** It depends on catalog complexity, not raw SKU count. If your catalog is full of part-number variants, manufacturer synonyms, and dense spec language, semantic matching pays off well below 10,000 SKUs. A flat catalog with clean naming gets less mileage from it.
