## What are Algolia's free tier limits, and what should you do when you hit them?

You signed up for Algolia's free tier - or you're about to - and you want to know if it'll last. Probably not as long as you think. The Build plan gives you 10,000 search requests per month and a million indexed records at no cost, which sounds generous until you see how a predictive search widget counts requests. Every keystroke fires a separate API call. A visitor typing five characters sends four or five requests before a single result appears. A store with 2,000 active monthly users can exhaust that cap in two or three weeks of normal usage. When you do hit it, there's no warning - the bill appears, or results start throttling, or both. This article covers what you actually get on the free tier, what it costs when you outgrow it, and which alternatives are worth comparing.

## What does Algolia's free tier actually include?

Algolia's free plan, called the Build tier, includes 10,000 search requests per month and 1 million indexed records at no cost. Full API access and the InstantSearch widget library are both included on Build, so a developer can put together a working search experience without spending anything up front.

What Build does not include is NeuralSearch - Algolia's semantic, meaning-aware matching layer. That feature is restricted to the top-tier Elevate plan and is unavailable on Build, Grow, Grow Plus, and Premium. Every tier below Elevate runs keyword-first matching, which means results are based on exact or near-exact word matches rather than what a user actually means.

Being on the free tier does not change how much configuration work is involved. Rules, synonyms, and ranking logic all still need to be set up by a developer - that work stays with your engineering team regardless of plan.

One more thing worth knowing before you start: there is no warning gate on the Build plan's limits. When usage goes over 10,000 requests or 1 million records, overage billing kicks in automatically.

## When do small stores typically hit Algolia's 10K search limit?

That automatic overage billing arrives faster than most founders expect - and the reason is how predictive search actually counts requests.

A predictive search widget fires one API call per keystroke, not one per search session. A visitor typing a five-character query sends four or five requests before they see any results. That's four or five against your monthly cap for a single search attempt, not one. If they refine their query or search again in the same visit, the count keeps climbing from there.

Do the math with realistic traffic. A site with 2,000 to 3,000 active monthly users using a predictive widget can exhaust 10,000 requests within the first few weeks of the month. Visitors who type and backspace before settling on a phrase, or who run two or three searches per session, push the count higher still. A site hitting the cap in week three isn't a sign of exceptional traffic - it's what normal search usage looks like once you account for how keystrokes are counted.

The 1 million record limit is a different story. It's generous. A small catalog of a few thousand SKUs won't come close to it. Search volume is what triggers the ceiling on Build, not catalog size. That distinction matters when you're estimating how long the free tier will last.

The harder problem is the lack of any configurable alert. Algolia doesn't send a warning when you approach the limit. You find out two ways: the bill arrives with overage charges, or Algolia begins throttling responses and users see slow or missing results. Either way, the limit is already behind you when you learn about it.

## How much does Algolia cost once you're past the free tier?

The bill that arrives - the one you don't see coming - runs on Grow or Grow Plus rates. Both plans charge per search request and per indexed record above the plan baseline. The $2,000+/month figure that circulates online applies at millions of searches per month or on enterprise Elevate contracts, not at typical mid-market volumes. A company running hundreds of thousands of searches a month on Grow's pay-as-you-go pricing will usually stay well under $500/month.

The overage rate depends on which paid plan you're on. Grow Plus costs more per additional search request than standard Grow. That higher rate comes with AI features not available on the standard Grow plan, along with expanded analytics retention.

What spending more on either plan does NOT get you is NeuralSearch. Algolia's semantic matching layer is exclusive to the Elevate (enterprise) plan. There is no per-query add-on or usage-based path to it at any Grow spend level. If semantic search is a hard requirement, that means an enterprise contract - not a self-serve upgrade.

The last cost worth accounting for doesn't appear on an invoice: developer time. Synonym lists and ranking rules need to be written and maintained at every paid tier. Grow caps rules at 10 per index; Grow Plus raises that limit to 10,000. Regardless of the cap, someone has to write and update them. As your catalog grows or your search patterns shift, that list grows with it. A team without a dedicated search engineer will feel this cost most.

## What does Algolia's free tier, and every tier below Elevate, leave out?

Spending more on Grow or Grow Plus doesn't close the capability gap - it narrows the symptom at most. Below Elevate, Algolia's matching is keyword-first: a visitor who types "something lightweight for a summer wedding" gets results that match those exact words, not the intent behind them.

NeuralSearch - Algolia's semantic matching layer - is exclusive to the Elevate enterprise tier. There's no add-on or spend threshold that unlocks it on any self-serve plan. A user typing a descriptive or long-tail query that doesn't match catalog titles word-for-word simply falls through. They get irrelevant results, or nothing.

On Build and standard Grow, the only vocabulary workaround is a manually maintained synonym list. Grow Plus adds AI-assisted synonym generation, which reduces the manual load but doesn't change the underlying architecture. Results are still keyword-driven. Every synonym you add papers over one specific phrasing gap; the next variation opens another.

The second gap runs deeper and holds at every tier: there's no native conversational Q&A anywhere in Algolia's lineup. A customer who asks "is this fabric machine washable?" gets a search results page, not a direct answer. That's true on Build, Grow, Grow Plus, and Improve alike. Algolia can surface products based on keywords in the question, but it can't read the question and answer it. The visitor either rewrites their question as a keyword or leaves. Closing the true Q&A gap requires either a custom build on top of the API or a separate tool entirely.

Both are capability gaps. Spending more on self-serve plans doesn't close either one. They are structural limits of keyword-first matching, and the only path through them on Algolia is an enterprise contract.
