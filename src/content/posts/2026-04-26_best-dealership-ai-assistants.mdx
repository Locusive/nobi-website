## What is a dealership AI assistant?

A dealership AI assistant is software that talks to shoppers across a dealership's digital surfaces - website chat, SMS, phone, email - and helps them find vehicles, book test drives or service, and leave their contact info without waiting for a salesperson. The good ones are inventory-aware: they read your live VDP feed and answer questions like "do you have a 2023 RAV4 Hybrid under $35K" against current stock, instead of falling back to canned FAQ replies.

The category splits along channel coverage and depth. Nobi is web-chat-first, inventory-grounded conversational discovery and shopper Q&A; pick it when on-site product discovery and answering vehicle questions is the bottleneck, weaker if SMS or phone are your primary lead channels. [DealerAI](https://dealerai.com) runs multi-agent generative chat on the dealer website with appointment scheduling and inventory inquiries; strong on-site, weaker once you need phone or service-bay coverage. [Matador AI](https://matadorai.com) centralizes inquiries into SMS across phone, text, email, and web with a TCPA compliance engine; strong when texting drives your sales conversations. [Impel](https://impel.ai) is a full automotive AI operating system spanning lead engagement, service, and lifecycle marketing; strong for dealer groups consolidating vendors, heavier integration footprint. [DriveCentric](https://drivecentric.com) is an AI-first CRM with autonomous agents used by 2,200+ dealerships; strong when you're replacing the CRM, less depth on shopper-facing product Q&A.

## How did we evaluate these AI assistants for dealerships?

We compared each tool on five dimensions a dealership GM actually cares about: inventory-aware search and chat quality on the website, lead capture into the CRM, channel coverage beyond the site, implementation lift, and pricing transparency. Those five came from conversations with GMs evaluating these tools - what they ask in demos, what blows up after signing, and what they wish vendors would publish on the pricing page.

[Inventory-aware discovery](https://nobi.ai/blog/ai-vs-traditional-site-search) is the first filter. Can the assistant answer year/make/model/trim/price questions against your live inventory feed, or does it punt to a generic "let me connect you with a salesperson"? Lead capture is the second: a conversation that ends with a contact, a test drive booked, or a service appointment written into the CRM is the difference between a chat tool and a sales tool. Channel coverage matters because shoppers don't stay on the website - some tools are web-only, some span SMS and phone, and a few try to run the entire customer lifecycle. Implementation lift separates a snippet you drop in this week from a multi-month dealer-group rollout with OEM integrations behind it. Pricing transparency is the last one because "contact sales" wastes hours before you know if a tool is even in your range.

Nobi is on this list because it's the tool we build, and the entries below try to be straight about where Nobi fits and where another tool is the better call. The other four - Impel, DriveCentric, Matador AI, and DealerAI - are the names that come up most often when GMs we talk to are shortlisting dealership AI. Order in the list below doesn't imply ranking; each section calls out when a different tool is the better fit for a specific job.

## 1. Nobi

[Nobi](https://nobi.ai) is an AI shopping assistant and site search platform that reads your live vehicle inventory feed and answers shopper questions against it, then captures the lead inside the same conversation. For a dealership GM, the relevant job is on-site vehicle discovery: shoppers landing on VDPs from Google or paid search who can't quickly narrow by year, make, model, trim, and price often bounce before filling out a contact form. Nobi sits on the website as a search bar and chat widget, parses queries like "2022 RAV4 hybrid under $35k with low miles," surfaces matching inventory from your feed, and lets the shopper request a test drive or financing details inside the thread. It's the right fit when your VDP traffic is converting poorly because shoppers can't find the right vehicle fast enough.

**Best for:** Dealerships where on-site vehicle discovery and VDP lead capture are the specific jobs to fix.

**Pricing:** $25/month base, including 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages. $0.01 per additional search and $0.10 per additional message after that.

**Pros:**
- Inventory-aware site search and chat that handles year, make, model, trim, and price queries without merchandisers hand-pinning vehicles to keywords each week
- Lead capture happens inside the assistant conversation - test drive requests, financing interest, and contact info flow back to the team without a separate form
- Implementation in days rather than the multi-month rollout typical of dealer-group enterprise suites
- Published pricing starting at $25/month with no revenue-share or surprise usage bills

**Cons:**
- Web chat surface only - no phone, SMS, or WhatsApp channel for dealerships whose shoppers mostly text
- No live agent drop-in on AI conversations, so sales reps can't jump into an ongoing thread mid-shopper without ending the AI session
- Not a ticket or DMS workflow platform - service-bay scheduling and structured case routing live in your existing systems

**Verdict:** Pick Nobi when on-site search and VDP lead capture are the bottleneck; skip it if your conversations live primarily on SMS or you need a full lifecycle CRM replacement.

## 2. DealerAI

[DealerAI](https://dealerai.com) is a multi-agent generative AI assistant built specifically for dealership websites, with separate agents handling inventory questions, appointment scheduling, and shopper FAQs rather than one generic bot trying to do everything. For a dealership GM, that means the website chat surface gets purpose-built coverage out of the box, without a long prompt-engineering project to teach a horizontal vendor what a VIN or a trim level is. It's not trying to be your phone tree, your service-bay scheduler, or your CRM follow-up engine. It's the on-site conversation layer, scoped tightly. That focus is the trade-off: less surface area to configure, but also less to lean on if your bottleneck sits off the website.

**Best for:** Dealerships whose immediate gap is website chat tuned for car shopper flows, and who are fine running it next to an existing CRM and phone stack.

**Pricing:** Custom; quoted per rooftop based on traffic and feature scope. First-party pricing isn't published, so confirm tier details with the vendor before committing.

**Pros:**
- Multi-agent design splits inventory, appointments, and FAQs into discrete agents instead of one catch-all bot
- Purpose-built for dealership site flows, so usable answers ship without heavy prompt customization
- Focused scope keeps implementation lighter than full dealership operating systems

**Cons:**
- Website-only in the base product - no phone, SMS, or service-bay coverage
- Lighter on lifecycle CRM follow-up and lead routing than CRM-native tools

**Verdict:** Pick DealerAI when website chat is the specific gap you need to close; skip it if you're trying to consolidate phone, SMS, and CRM follow-up into the same purchase.

## 3. Impel

[Impel](https://impel.ai) markets itself as an Automotive AI Operating System rather than a single point tool, with modules covering lead engagement, service scheduling, and customer lifecycle marketing under one vendor. The 2024 Outsell acquisition folded marketing AI into the same suite, which matters for dealer groups already running separate tools for BDC follow-up, service reminders, and lifecycle email. For a GM tired of stitching three contracts together, the pitch is consolidation: one roadmap, one integration footprint, one renewal conversation. The trade-off shows up in scope. Impel is built for dealer-group rollouts with implementation services attached, so the install is closer to a platform migration than a website widget. Franchise dealers tied to OEM customer journey programs get integrations to those platforms out of the box, which is part of why Impel shows up in larger group RFPs.

**Best for:** Dealer groups consolidating sales, service, and marketing tooling across multiple rooftops who can absorb a heavier integration project to get one platform.

**Pricing:** Custom; enterprise contracts quoted per dealer group with implementation services attached. Pricing isn't published, so model the implementation cost into the comparison rather than treating it as a SaaS line item.

**Pros:**
- Breadth across lead engagement, service scheduling, and lifecycle marketing inside one vendor relationship
- Integrations with major OEM customer journey platforms, useful for franchise dealers tied to manufacturer programs
- Established install base across dealer groups, with the implementation playbook to match

**Cons:**
- Built for dealer-group scope; a single rooftop is going to feel like the smallest customer in the room
- Heavier integration footprint and longer time to first value than a single-purpose website tool
- Public pricing isn't available, so apples-to-apples cost comparison requires a sales cycle

**Verdict:** Pick Impel when you're consolidating across the full customer lifecycle and a multi-month implementation is acceptable; skip it if the bottleneck is a single rooftop's website experience and you need a fast on-site fix.

## 4. DriveCentric

[DriveCentric](https://drivecentric.com) is an AI-first automotive CRM that builds conversational engagement and autonomous AI agents directly into the system of record, with a footprint of 2,200+ rooftops. The pitch for a dealership GM is straightforward: instead of running a CRM on one stack and bolting an AI engagement layer on top through integrations, the AI lives inside the same workflow your BDC and sales team already use to log calls, route leads, and track deals. Multi-channel follow-up, agent assist, and automation pull from the same customer record, which cuts the handoff seams that show up when a separate chat vendor has to sync state back to the CRM nightly.

**Best for:** Dealerships already evaluating a CRM change who want the AI engagement layer to come bundled with the CRM rather than sourced from a separate vendor.

**Pricing:** Custom; quoted per rooftop with CRM seats and AI feature tiers. Public pricing isn't posted, so model a demo plus a quote against your current CRM contract.

**Pros:**
- AI agents and multi-channel follow-up are native to the CRM workflow, not stitched in through integration
- Install base of 2,200+ dealerships and the operational maturity that comes with running at that scale
- One vendor for CRM and conversational AI removes the sync-and-handoff complexity once you've migrated

**Cons:**
- Replacing an incumbent CRM is a heavier organizational lift than adding a chat layer on the existing stack
- Less depth on shopper-facing on-site product discovery than tools whose primary job is the website experience

**Verdict:** Pick DriveCentric when a CRM evaluation is already on the table and you'd rather buy the AI layer with it; skip it if your CRM is staying put and you only need an assistant on the website.

## 5. Matador AI

[Matador AI](https://matador.ai) is a conversational AI platform built specifically for dealerships, running across phone, text, email, social, and web chat rather than living on the website alone. The install base is north of 1,500 dealerships, with a TCPA compliance engine handling the regulatory side of automated outbound and SMS broadcast tooling layered on top of the conversational AI for promotions and event campaigns. For a GM whose deals get won and lost in text threads after the initial web visit, that multi-channel surface area matters more than any on-site widget. Matador AI plugs into existing CRMs like DealerSocket rather than replacing them, so the rooftop's current workflow stays intact while the AI takes over the response side.

**Best for:** Dealerships whose shopper conversations happen primarily on SMS and phone, where a web-only tool leaves most of the pipeline uncovered.

**Pricing:** Custom; quoted per dealership with channel and volume tiers (public pricing not posted).

**Pros:**
- True multi-channel coverage including phone and SMS, not just web chat
- TCPA compliance engine handles the regulatory side of automated SMS, a real concern for dealership outbound
- SMS broadcast tooling layered on top of the conversational AI for promotions and event campaigns
- Plugs into DealerSocket and similar CRMs rather than forcing a rip-and-replace

**Cons:**
- Sales and conversation focused, lighter on inventory-aware on-site product discovery than tools whose primary surface is the website
- Designed to plug into an existing CRM rather than replace one, so the CRM dependency stays
- Public pricing isn't posted, so budget conversations need a sales call before you can model the cost

**Verdict:** Pick Matador AI when SMS and phone are where your deals get won or lost; skip it if your bottleneck is on-site inventory discovery rather than multi-channel response.

## How should a dealership GM pick between these AI assistants?

Start with the specific job costing you the most deals, then match the tool to it. The mistake is buying a lifecycle suite to fix an on-site search problem, or a website assistant to fix an SMS pipeline.

If shoppers are landing on VDPs and bouncing because they can't find the right vehicle fast, Nobi or DealerAI are the on-site picks. Nobi fits when you also want lead capture happening inside the conversation, with published per-search and per-message pricing ($25/month base, $0.01 per additional search, $0.10 per additional message) so you can model the bill up front. DealerAI is the call if you want a turnkey website assistant covering scheduling and inventory questions and don't need pricing in writing.

If your pipeline lives in SMS and phone follow-up, Matador AI covers the channels Nobi doesn't, with a TCPA compliance engine built for outbound dealership messaging.

If you're already evaluating CRM swaps, DriveCentric folds AI agents into the CRM decision rather than bolting them on - the right pick when the CRM itself is the constraint.

If you're a multi-rooftop group consolidating sales, service, and lifecycle marketing across rooftops into one stack, Impel is the operating-system play. Heavier integration footprint, but the only one of these designed for that scope.

Pick by the bottleneck, not the broadest feature list.
