## What is AI deep search for a marketplace?

AI deep search ranks across listings, categories, and attributes in a single pass, using machine learning instead of hand-tuned keyword rules. On a marketplace with millions of listings, a query like [wireless noise cancelling headphones under $200](https://nobi.ai/blog/handling-long-tail-queries) has to filter on attributes (wireless, noise cancelling, price), surface listings shoppers actually click and buy, and adapt to what each visitor has browsed before - all without a merchandiser pinning products by hand.

The four tools in this comparison approach that problem from different angles. Nobi is a packaged AI catalog search and shopping assistant with a usage-based price, built for single-brand ecommerce catalogs. [Algolia](https://www.algolia.com) is API-first, with NeuralSearch layering vector search onto its keyword index for developer teams. [Coveo](https://www.coveo.com) is enterprise-tier, ML-driven, and priced accordingly, with explicit marketplace personalization in its commerce product. [Hawk Search](https://www.hawksearch.com) leans on faceted navigation and merchandiser controls for B2B and visually dynamic catalogs.

## 2. Algolia

[Algolia](https://algolia.com) is a developer-first search API built around sub-50ms response times, a deep library of frontend widgets, and a semantic layer called NeuralSearch on higher tiers. The core value is the API itself: engineering teams get granular control over indexing, ranking, and the search UI, and the platform scales cleanly into multi-million-listing catalogs. For a head of ecommerce running a marketplace with a dedicated search engineering team, that level of control is the point - you can tune relevance per query class, ship custom ranking signals from buyer behavior, and own the frontend end-to-end. The trade-off is that Algolia ships a powerful toolkit, not a packaged ranker; how good search feels depends on how many engineering hours you put into tuning it.

**Best for:** Marketplaces with a dedicated search engineering team that wants to own ranking logic, indexing strategy, and the search UI end-to-end.

**Pricing:** Free plan covers 10K search requests and 100K records per month. Grow is $0.50 per 1,000 search requests above 10K plus $0.40 per 1,000 records above 100K; Grow Plus jumps to $1.75 per 1,000 search requests on the same record overage. Elevate is volume-discounted enterprise pricing. NeuralSearch is gated to higher tiers. Mid-sized deployments typically land in the $500-$5,000/month range before custom relevance engineering.

**Pros:**
- Sub-50ms response times even on multi-million-listing catalogs
- Large ecosystem of libraries, widgets, and integrations for any storefront stack
- NeuralSearch adds vector-based semantic matching on top of keyword search at higher tiers
- Granular API control over ranking, indexing, and personalization

**Cons:**
- API-first operating model - relevance is tuned through engineering code rather than a merchandiser UI
- Usage-based pricing can produce surprise bills when query volume spikes
- NeuralSearch index build time scales with catalog size, so very large catalogs see longer initial setup

**Verdict:** Pick Algolia if you have a dedicated search engineering team and want full API control over ranking and the frontend.

## 3. Coveo

[Coveo](https://coveo.com) brings ML-driven relevance across commerce, customer support, and internal workplace search, with one model spanning every surface. Its commerce product explicitly tunes personalization for marketplaces, including buyer-aware product displays. For a head of ecommerce running a marketplace that also operates a seller support portal and an internal knowledge base, that unification is the point: the same buyer signals that rank listings also feed support and internal search, so personalization compounds across surfaces instead of living in three disconnected indexes. The trade-off is scope and scale - Coveo is built for enterprise programs that genuinely need cross-surface coverage, with a sales cycle and services-heavy implementation measured in months. If marketplace search is the only surface you're ranking, the platform is more than the job requires.

**Best for:** Enterprise marketplaces unifying buyer search, seller and buyer support, and internal knowledge under one AI relevance model.

**Pricing:** Base at $600/month and Pro at $1,320/month per third-party references, with enterprise all-in deployments commonly landing at $100K+ per year once licensing (~$50K+), implementation (~$20K+), and professional services ($200-$300/hour) are added.

**Pros:**
- ML relevance with explicit marketplace personalization in the commerce product
- Cross-surface coverage - the same model spans search, support, and workplace knowledge
- Mature analytics and reporting tooling built for enterprise operations

**Cons:**
- Long sales cycle and services-heavy implementation measured in months, not days
- Enterprise scope and price - oversized if marketplace search is the only surface you need to rank
- Built for enterprise programs with internal services teams, not lighter technical orgs

**Verdict:** Pick Coveo when unifying search across multiple surfaces at enterprise scale is the actual job.

## 4. Hawk Search

[Hawk Search](https://hawksearch.com) is enterprise site search built for B2B and complex mid-market catalogs, with deep faceting and merchandising tooling tuned for catalogs that carry hundreds of attributes per product. For a head of ecommerce running a B2B-shaped marketplace where buyers filter on technical specs, order in bulk, and expect account-specific pricing to flow through search results, Hawk's faceted navigation is purpose-built for that catalog shape. It also handles unified product-and-content search, customer-specific pricing and availability, and visually descriptive query understanding for catalogs where image-driven discovery matters. The trade-off is scope: this is an enterprise integration with services-heavy implementation.

**Best for:** B2B marketplaces and attribute-heavy catalogs where buyers filter on hundreds of specs per listing and need account-specific pricing flowing through search.

**Pricing:** Core starts at $500/month and Premium at $850/month per Hawk Search's published tiers; Enterprise tier is sized per implementation based on catalog size, traffic, and integration scope.

**Pros:**
- Deep faceting and attribute-driven filtering built for catalogs with hundreds of specs per listing
- Merchandiser tools designed for B2B buying patterns, including account-specific pricing and entitlements at the search layer
- Established footprint in B2B and complex mid-market verticals where catalog depth matters

**Cons:**
- Services-heavy enterprise implementation, not a self-serve install
- Built for enterprise B2B and visually dynamic catalogs - lighter D2C marketplaces will feel rollout friction

**Verdict:** Pick Hawk Search when your marketplace is B2B-shaped or attribute-heavy and faceted navigation is the bottleneck.

## Frequently asked questions

**How is marketplace search different from standard ecommerce search?** A single-brand storefront ranks one catalog with one merchandising voice. A marketplace ranks across many sellers and listings, has to handle attribute filters that change as inventory turns over daily, and often layers in account-specific availability. [Manual keyword pinning](https://nobi.ai/blog/ai-vs-traditional-site-search) collapses at that scale.

**How long does AI deep search take to implement?** It depends on the tool. Nobi and Hawk Search publish concrete starter pricing - Nobi ships in days with a quick site-side install; Hawk's Core and Premium tiers ship in days while its Enterprise tier is a longer integration. Algolia and Coveo run longer at the high end - Algolia because relevance quality scales with engineering hours on the API, Coveo because enterprise rollouts are services-heavy and measured in months.

**Do these tools handle multi-seller ranking out of the box?** Coveo and Hawk Search are built for enterprise marketplace catalogs and tune ranking and merchandising for multi-vendor environments. Nobi personalizes ranking based on shopper click and purchase behavior on your catalog; explicit multi-seller ranking diversity (so smaller sellers don't get buried) is not a built-in feature, so marketplace teams whose primary bottleneck is seller-balancing should evaluate Coveo or Hawk specifically. Algolia hands engineering teams the API to build multi-seller ranking themselves.

**What does pricing look like as query volume scales?** Nobi is $25/month base with 2,500 searches and 250 messages included, then $0.01 per additional search and $0.10 per additional message - flat per-event math. A brand running 50,000 searches and 5,000 messages a month lands around $995. Algolia, Coveo, and Hawk all scale with tier jumps or enterprise contracts.

**Can a non-technical merchandising team run AI deep search without engineering support?** With Nobi, yes - that's the design point. With Algolia, no; the API expects engineering ownership. Coveo and Hawk sit in the middle, with merchandiser tooling but services-heavy setup.
