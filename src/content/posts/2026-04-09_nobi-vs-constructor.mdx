# Nobi vs Constructor: Which Site Search Platform Is Right for Your Ecommerce Store?

## What Nobi and Constructor Actually Do

Constructor is built for large retailers, and it shows. The platform covers search, browse, recommendations, and quizzes, but it assumes you have the internal infrastructure to match: dedicated merchandising staff, engineers who can configure relevance rules, and a team to maintain it all over time.

Nobi is built for mid-market ecommerce brands that don't have that kind of headcount. It combines site search, personalization, a conversational shopping assistant, and lead capture in one product. Out of the box, it works without manual rule-tuning.

Both platforms index your product catalog and surface results based on shopper behavior. That's where the similarity ends. Constructor's depth is a strength if you have the resources to use it. If you don't, that depth becomes overhead.

The practical difference: Constructor is a platform you build on. Nobi is a product you turn on. A brand like UNTUCKit, which runs a focused catalog and needs fast results without a dedicated merchandising team, is closer to Nobi's target customer. A retailer with millions of SKUs, regional buying teams, and an in-house engineering function is closer to Constructor's.

Choosing between them starts with an honest look at your team size and how much configuration work you're willing to own.

## How the Search Engines Compare: Behavioral AI vs Manual Tuning

Both platforms use machine learning, but they apply it differently, and that gap shows up in results.

Constructor ranks search results using behavioral signals, but it still depends on merchandising rules, synonym dictionaries, and manual configuration to achieve good relevance. Someone, usually a developer or merchandiser, needs to build and maintain those rules over time. That setup work is front-loaded, and keeping rules current as your catalog changes is an ongoing task.

Nobi takes a different approach. Its search engine watches what shoppers click, add to cart, and buy, then adjusts result rankings continuously based on that behavior. No rule maintenance required. The engine learns from your actual traffic and recalibrates on its own.

The practical difference shows up in conversion data. Faherty switched to Nobi from a prior AI search platform and saw their search conversion rate climb from 10.1% to 12.3%, a 21.8% relative improvement. That kind of lift after moving from one AI tool to another suggests behavioral learning at the individual-session level produces meaningfully better relevance than static or rule-governed approaches.

Kilte's results make a similar point from a different starting point. After replacing Shopify's default search with Nobi, they improved conversion by 21.7%. That's the gap between rule-based search and a system that adapts to real shopping behavior.

Nobi also includes a **conversational shopping assistant** that lets shoppers describe what they want in plain language. A shopper can type "blue dress under $100" and get filtered, ranked results, not a blank box or a wall of unrelated products. Constructor does not offer a native conversational interface. If your customers tend to search the way they talk, that's a meaningful functional difference, not just a nice-to-have.

For teams that want search to improve without ongoing manual tuning, Nobi's behavioral learning model removes a layer of operational work that Constructor's approach still requires.

## Pricing: Flat Subscription vs Revenue-Share

Pricing models matter as much as features, especially when your search platform starts driving real revenue. Constructor uses a revenue-share model: you pay a percentage of the revenue attributed to search and discovery results. That sounds aligned at first, but the math shifts as your store grows. A platform that drives more sales also generates a larger invoice, which puts a ceiling on how much ROI you can actually keep.

Nobi charges a flat monthly subscription. Your costs stay fixed regardless of how much revenue the product helps generate. That predictability matters for budgeting, and it means the upside belongs entirely to you.

The Lucchese case makes this concrete. After implementing Nobi, Lucchese reported over $1M in incremental revenue and a 39x ROI. Under a revenue-share arrangement, a meaningful slice of that million would flow back to the vendor. Under Nobi's flat pricing, the brand keeps all of it.

Constructor's pricing is not public. Getting a number typically involves a sales process, custom contracts, and minimum commitments that can be difficult for mid-market brands to negotiate. That structure reflects an enterprise sales motion, not a self-serve one. For a brand doing $10M to $50M in annual GMV, the barrier to even evaluate Constructor properly can be significant.

For stores in that range, the difference between a fixed monthly fee and a percentage of attributed revenue compounds quickly over a two- or three-year contract. When search starts performing, you want that compound effect working for your margins, not your vendor's.

## Implementation Time and Internal Resources Required

Getting a search platform live is not just a launch task. It affects how quickly you can start improving results, collecting behavioral data, and justifying the spend.

Constructor implementations typically take several weeks to a few months. Integrating the API, configuring relevance settings, and building out merchandising rules all require developer time before the platform is ready for production traffic. That is not a criticism. Constructor is built for large retailers with dedicated technical teams, and the depth of configuration it offers reflects that. If you have the engineering capacity, that investment pays off.

For smaller teams, the calculus is different. One or two developers managing an implementation alongside other priorities means that setup time is real overhead, and so is ongoing maintenance.

Nobi connects to Shopify, BigCommerce, and other major platforms through native integrations. After connecting, catalog sync, behavioral tracking, and default ranking configuration run automatically. Most stores are live within days. That faster start means you begin collecting behavioral data sooner, which directly feeds the ranking model. Better data earlier means better search results earlier, which matters a lot when you are mid-trial and trying to build an internal case.

For ongoing management, Nobi gives merchandising teams direct control over boost rules, pinning, and custom collections without needing to file a ticket. The Custom Actions API is there when you need store-specific logic, but day-to-day adjustments do not require developer involvement.

The trade-off is straightforward: Constructor offers more configurability in exchange for more setup work. Nobi trades some of that depth for speed and operational simplicity.

## Personalization and the Conversational Shopping Assistant

Personalization only matters if it actually changes what shoppers buy. Nobi personalizes search results at the individual visitor level, using session behavior and historical signals to surface the products each shopper is most likely to purchase. No manual segmentation, no rule writing. The engine adjusts in real time as a visitor moves through the site.

UNTUCKit saw this play out directly. After deploying Nobi's personalized search, their search conversion rate moved from 15.0% to 17.4%, a 16% relative improvement. The driver was Nobi's ability to surface size- and style-relevant results per visitor rather than returning the same ranked list for everyone who searched the same term.

Beyond search results, Nobi includes a **conversational shopping assistant** that lets shoppers describe what they want in plain language. A visitor can type "lightweight jacket for layering under 200 dollars" and get filtered, ranked results rather than a wall of loosely matched products. For stores with large catalogs, this matters. Standard keyword search on a 5,000-SKU catalog often returns so many results that shoppers give up. The conversational layer acts as a filtering mechanism that keeps the experience focused.

Nobi also captures leads within the assistant flow. When a shopper shows high purchase intent but doesn't convert during that session, the assistant can prompt for an email. That contact then goes back to the brand for follow-up. Constructor has no equivalent built-in feature.

Constructor does offer personalization across its recommendations and browse modules. The gap is in setup. Configuring those features typically requires working with their implementation team and often involves custom development before they perform well. That is not a knock on the quality of the output, but it is time and cost that smaller teams may not have available.

Nobi's personalization works without that ramp-up period. Out of the box, it applies to search, recommendations, and the assistant in the same deployment.

## Where Constructor May Be the Better Fit

Constructor is built for scale. If your brand does **$100M or more in GMV**, has a dedicated merchandising team, and runs a catalog complex enough to require fine-grained control over relevance and browse, Constructor is worth serious consideration.

It also handles multi-locale deployments and advanced A/B testing infrastructure directly within the search layer. For enterprise teams that need to run controlled experiments on ranking logic across different market segments, or build highly customized browse experiences by region, Constructor has the depth to support that work.

The tradeoff is investment. That depth requires ongoing configuration from people who know the platform. It rewards teams with the engineering and merchandising bandwidth to tune it continuously. If you have that capacity, you'll use it well.

On the other end of the spectrum, **Nobi is not the right fit for very small stores**, generally those under $1M in GMV. At that revenue level, the ROI on any dedicated search platform is limited, regardless of which one you pick.

The clearest deciding factor is your team's capacity. Constructor gives more fine-grained control to teams willing to maintain it. Nobi is built to perform well without constant attention from a dedicated team.
