## What does 'support deflection' actually mean in ecommerce live chat?

Support deflection means resolving a shopper's question before it becomes an agent ticket, usually through self-service content, an AI chat agent, or a smart order lookup. The metric that matters is resolved deflection, not containment. A closed chat with an unanswered shopper usually becomes an angry email an hour later, so track re-contact rate within 48 hours alongside session-level outcomes.

Split your deflection strategy into two lanes. Pre-purchase questions (fit, sizing, availability, product comparisons) need catalog-aware tooling that can read your product data. Post-purchase questions (WISMO, returns, refunds) need order-system lookups and policy logic. The tools that handle one well rarely handle the other well out of the box.

WISMO and returns alone account for 40-60% of ecommerce ticket volume at most brands, and they're the easiest wins because the answer lives in structured data your shop already owns.

Measure CSAT on deflected interactions and compare it to agent CSAT. If the AI is scoring meaningfully lower, it's handling conversations it should be routing to a human.

## Which ticket types should you try to deflect first, and which should always reach a human?

The fastest wins come from tickets with a single factual answer: order status, return policy, shipping windows, sizing, and product availability. Deflect those first with AI or self-service. Anything involving a refund exception, a damaged item, a complaint, or a VIP account should skip the bot and route to a person immediately. The risk of a wrong AI answer on those tickets is larger than the labor you'd save.

Start with the questions where the answer is the same every time, regardless of who's asking. Order status pulls from your OMS. Return windows and shipping cutoffs are written policies. Sizing charts and stock counts live in your product catalog. An AI that can read those sources will answer correctly nearly every time, and shoppers get a reply in seconds instead of waiting hours for an agent. Basic product comparisons (is this shirt heavier than that one, does this kettle fit my stove) also belong here, since the data is already in your PDPs.

Hold the line on anything with real downside risk. Refund disputes, damaged or missing shipments, allergy or safety questions, chargeback threats, and known VIPs should go straight to a human. A bot that misreads a peanut allergy question or denies a legitimate refund creates a problem that costs far more than one rep's time.

A middle tier exists and deserves testing before you flip it on: discount code troubleshooting, subscription pauses and swaps, and address edits after a label prints. Pilot these with a small percentage of traffic, watch the resolution rate and the reopen rate for two weeks, and only then expand.

The rule that keeps you out of trouble: if getting the answer wrong would cost more than handling the ticket manually, a human handles it. Everything else is a candidate for deflection, and that's where tools like Nobi's support deflection layer earn their keep.

## What does a live chat stack that actually deflects tickets look like?

A chat stack that actually deflects tickets has three layers working together: a self-service layer that catches shoppers before they ever open chat, an AI layer grounded in your real product, policy, and order data that answers factual questions, and a clean handoff to a human inside your existing helpdesk when the AI isn't confident. The layers matter more than the vendor. Any stack missing one of them will underperform, no matter how much you spend on the other two.

**Layer 1: self-serve.** Before chat, shoppers should be able to find answers on their own. That means an order-status lookup linked from the header and order confirmation email, a shipping and returns page that a first-time visitor can find in one click, and size guides embedded on PDPs rather than buried in a help center. The cheapest deflected ticket is the one that never opens the chat widget.

**Layer 2: AI chat.** When a shopper does open chat, the AI needs real context. That means retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) [grounded in your help center articles](https://nobi.ai/knowledge-base), your product catalog, and your order data, so it can answer "where is my order" and "does this run small" with specifics instead of platitudes. Configure it to say "let me get a human" when confidence is low. An AI that guesses creates worse tickets than no AI at all.

**Layer 3: human handoff.** When the AI escalates, the full transcript and any context it pulled (order number, product viewed, return eligibility) should land in the agent's existing helpdesk. If the shopper has to repeat their question, you just spent money to create a worse experience.

Instrument each layer separately: self-serve page views, AI conversations, AI resolution rate, handoff rate, and agent-resolved tickets. Most teams over-invest in layer 2 and under-invest in layer 1, then wonder why deflection plateaus.

Nobi sits in layer 2, grounded in your catalog and site content, and hands off cleanly when it isn't confident.

## How do you measure whether live chat is actually deflecting tickets?

Measure four numbers and watch them month over month: contact rate, AI resolution rate, the CSAT gap between AI and agents, and cost per resolved contact. Containment rate on its own is a vanity metric. A bot that closes the window without answering will post a beautiful containment number and a miserable re-contact rate 48 hours later.

Contact rate is chats plus tickets per 100 sessions. It's the top-line number, and if deflection is actually working, it should fall as traffic grows. If chats drop but email volume spikes the same month, that's not deflection, that's displacement: shoppers routed around the bot and found another way to reach you. Always look at total contact rate across channels, not chat in isolation.

AI resolution rate is the honest version of containment. Count a chat as resolved only if the AI closed it without handing off to an agent AND the same shopper didn't come back within 48 hours on any channel. That second half is the part most teams skip, and it's the one that catches bots that are bluffing.

Track AI CSAT alongside agent CSAT. A 5-to-10 point gap is normal. If the gap is wider than 10 points, raise the handoff threshold: your bot is answering questions it shouldn't be trying to answer. Sampling a few dozen low-CSAT AI chats each week tells you exactly which intents to route straight to a human.

Cost per resolved contact is the number finance cares about. Divide total support cost, including the chat tool, per-message or per-resolution fees, and agent time on escalations, by the count of resolved contacts. For Nobi, the math is straightforward: $25/month base covers 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages, and overages run $0.01 per search and $0.10 per message, so a mid-market brand running 50,000 searches and 5,000 AI chats knows its ceiling before the invoice arrives.

## What's a realistic 30-60-90 day rollout for a deflection-focused live chat program?

In the first 30 days, instrument your current ticket mix and ship the self-service layer: a real order status page, clear return and shipping policy pages, size guides where relevant. That's the cheapest deflection you'll ever get, and it makes every downstream AI decision easier. Tag the last 30 days of tickets, identify your top five drivers, and pick the chat tool based on which of those five it actually handles well, not which has the flashiest demo.

Days 30-60: launch AI chat on your three highest-volume deflectable topics only. Everything else routes to a human. Set a conservative handoff threshold (when confidence is low, hand off). Measure re-contact rate weekly, because a "resolved" conversation that becomes an email two hours later is not resolved. This is also when Nobi earns its keep for product and sizing questions, where [usage-based pricing](https://nobi.ai/pricing) ($25/month base, $0.10 per additional conversational message) means you pay per answer rather than per seat.

Days 60-90: widen coverage based on measured resolution rate and CSAT. Kill any automation where CSAT is materially below your agent baseline. Write down your escalation criteria so the team applies them consistently.

Two traps to avoid: turning AI on for every topic day one (the CSAT hit is hard to walk back with shoppers), and treating the install as one-and-done. Catalogs and policies drift. Schedule a monthly review.
