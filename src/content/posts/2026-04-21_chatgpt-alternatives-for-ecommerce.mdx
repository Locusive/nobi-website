## Why are ecommerce teams looking beyond ChatGPT for shopping assistance?

ChatGPT is a general-purpose model with no native awareness of your catalog, inventory, pricing, or shoppers. Teams using it for shopping help are either pasting product data into prompts by hand or wiring up the API themselves, and the results show it: answers that invent SKUs, miss stock-outs, and leave no revenue trail behind the conversation.

The grounding problem is the first wall. ChatGPT answers questions well, but it does not know which of your products are in stock today, which are on sale this week, or which are your best sellers this quarter. Without a live feed of catalog data, the model fills gaps by guessing, and a confident wrong spec on a $400 jacket is worse than no answer at all.

The storefront problem is the second. ChatGPT has no widget on your PDP, no cart handoff, no merchandising controls. Teams have to build the surface, the catalog sync, and the analytics themselves before a shopper ever sees a reply.

Then there is attribution. Without a tracking layer, you cannot tell which conversations drove revenue, which moved CVR, or which ones quietly sent buyers to a competitor.

## What does a purpose-built ecommerce shopping assistant do that ChatGPT doesn't?

A purpose-built assistant connects to your live product catalog, sits on your storefront, and ties every conversation back to revenue. That means grounded answers about real SKUs at today's price and today's stock level, cart and PDP help that knows what the shopper is looking at, and CVR and AOV numbers you can actually defend in a QBR. ChatGPT can roleplay any of this in a prompt window. It doesn't ship it as a product on your site.

Four things separate the two.

Live catalog grounding. A real shopping assistant pulls from your product feed so answers reflect current inventory, pricing, and variant availability. When a shopper asks "do you have this jacket in a medium under $200," the response is based on what's in stock right now, not on whatever the model saw during training.

Storefront surfaces, not just a chat bubble. The assistant lives in the search bar, on the PDP, and in the cart. At Faherty, the Shop with AI experience is converting at 6.3% with a $315 AOV, numbers that only exist because the assistant is embedded in the buying flow, not parked on a separate page. Lucchese's cart and PDP assistants drove meaningful incremental checkouts on top of what search was already contributing.

Session-level attribution. Conversations roll up to CVR, AOV, and revenue per visitor, per session. You can answer "did the assistant make us money this month" with a number, not a hunch.

Merchandising controls your team will actually open. Merchandisers boost, pin, bury, and curate through a UI, not by rewriting system prompts every time a collection drops.

Nobi bundles site search, conversational assistance, and [lead capture](https://nobi.ai/lead-capture) into one platform, so you're not stitching three tools together and reconciling three sets of analytics to see what's working.

## When is ChatGPT or a general-purpose AI still the right call?

ChatGPT is the right tool for internal workflows. It's the wrong tool for a storefront-facing assistant that needs to know which size is in stock. If your use case falls into the first bucket and you don't need revenue attribution or catalog grounding, staying on ChatGPT is the pragmatic call, and anything else is over-engineering.

A few concrete scenarios where general-purpose AI still wins:

**Internal team research and content drafting.** Writing product descriptions, brainstorming campaign angles, summarizing a 40-page trend report, drafting responses to a wholesale inquiry. ChatGPT's language quality is hard to beat, and your merchandisers and copywriters don't need it wired to your catalog to get value.

**One-off buyer question triage over email or Slack.** If your CX lead is fielding twenty questions a week and wants to draft replies faster, a ChatGPT tab open next to their inbox is fine. You don't need a storefront deployment, a widget, or an integration. A paste-and-edit loop works.

**Exploratory prototyping before you commit to a product strategy.** Spinning up a quick conversational demo to show your CEO what "AI shopping" could feel like on your site. ChatGPT or a GPT with your PDF catalog uploaded gets you 80% of the way to a conversation you can pitch with, and you can kill it cheaply if the team decides to go a different direction.

**Anything without SLA or catalog-accuracy requirements.** If a wrong answer has no revenue consequence, general-purpose AI is fine. The moment a hallucinated "yes we have that in stock" costs you a refund and a chargeback, the calculus flips.

The tell is simple. Is the output going to a shopper on your site, or to a teammate who will read it and decide what to do? If it's the latter, ChatGPT is still a reasonable home base.

## When should you pick Gorgias or Octane AI over Nobi?

Nobi isn't universally the right pick. If your bottleneck is support ticket volume rather than storefront discovery, Gorgias is built for that job and has a mature agent-assist layer Nobi doesn't try to replicate. If you're a beauty or CPG brand whose customers convert through a product-finder quiz, Octane AI's quiz builder is a more natural fit than a conversational shopping assistant.

Pick Gorgias when the problem you're solving is inbox volume. Gorgias is a help desk first and a shopping assistant second: ticketing, macros, and AI resolution live in the same tool, priced around monthly ticket volume. If your team is drowning in "where's my order" emails and Shopify chat, that's what Gorgias is designed to absorb. Nobi is designed to convert a browsing shopper into a buyer, which is a different job.

Pick Octane AI if quiz-led merchandising is central to how your category sells. Skincare, supplements, fragrance, and haircare brands often route customers through a product-finder quiz before anything else, and Octane AI's quiz builder is purpose-built for that flow on Shopify. A conversational assistant can ask similar questions, but a branded, visual quiz is a different shopper experience.

Two more honest caveats. Nobi isn't the right fit for very small stores, roughly under $1M GMV; the $25/month base includes 2,500 searches and 250 conversational messages, and stores with thin traffic won't get enough signal for the ranking model to improve on a default catalog sort. And Nobi has less name recognition than enterprise incumbents. If procurement requires a Gartner Magic Quadrant vendor, that's a real constraint, not one we can argue around.
